01

From Discrete to Continuous

VE and VP as limits of a stochastic differential equation

VE as an SDE

Take the VE update $X_{t} = X_{t-\Delta t} + \sqrt{\beta}\,Z_t$ with $Z_t \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\Delta t)$ and let $\Delta t \to 0$:

$$dX_t = \sqrt{\beta}\,dZ_t.$$

Here $dZ_t$ is an increment of standard Brownian motion: $Z_t - Z_{t-\Delta t} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \Delta t)$. In integrated form, $X_t = X_0 + \sqrt{\beta}\,Z_t$, matching the VE single-step corruption we derived earlier.

VP as an SDE

The VP recursion is $X_t = \sqrt{1-\beta_n}\,X_{t-\Delta t} + \sqrt{\beta_n}\,Z_t$. Setting $\beta_n = \beta_t\,dt$ and Taylor-expanding $\sqrt{1-\beta_t\,dt} \approx 1 - \tfrac{\beta_t\,dt}{2}$:

$$dX_t = -\frac{\beta_t}{2}\,X_t\,dt + \sqrt{\beta_t}\,dZ_t.$$

This is a linear Ornstein–Uhlenbeck SDE: a deterministic pull toward zero plus white noise. It is the continuous-time stand-in for DDPM.

General forward SDE

Both VE and VP fit the template

$$dX_t = f(X_t, t)\,dt + g(t)\,dZ_t,$$

with drift $f$ and diffusion coefficient $g$. The explicit Euler–Maruyama discretization is

$$X_{k+1} = X_k + \Delta t\,f(X_k, k\Delta t) + g(k\Delta t)\sqrt{\Delta t}\,Z_k.$$

VE

$f = 0$, $g(t) = \sqrt{\beta}$.

VP

$f(X_t, t) = -\tfrac{\beta_t}{2}X_t$, $g(t) = \sqrt{\beta_t}$.

The solution is a distribution

Unlike an ODE, an SDE does not trace out a single path. It defines a stochastic process $\{X_t\}_{t \in [0,T]}$ via the Itô stochastic integral:

$$X_t = X_0 + \int_0^t f(X_s, s)\,ds + \int_0^t g(s)\,dW_s,\qquad \int_0^t g(s)\,dW_s = \lim_{\varepsilon\to 0}\sum_{k=0}^{\lfloor t/\varepsilon\rfloor} g(\varepsilon k)\sqrt{\varepsilon}\,Z_k.$$

For diffusion models we do not need the full joint law of $\{X_t\}$. We only need the marginal densities $\{p_t\}_{t\in[0,T]}$ with $X_t \sim p_t$, and their evolution from $p_0$ to $p_T$.

02

Fokker–Planck Equation

The PDE that governs $p_t$

Fokker–Planck, 1D

Theorem (Fokker–Planck)

For the SDE $dX_t = f(X_t, t)\,dt + g(t)\,dZ_t$, the marginal density $p_t(x)$ satisfies

$$\partial_t\,p_t(x) = -\partial_x\bigl(f(x,t)\,p_t(x)\bigr) + \frac{g^2(t)}{2}\,\partial_x^2\,p_t(x).$$

This is a deterministic PDE for the probability density, no longer involving Brownian motion explicitly. We will derive it by testing against smooth functions and integrating by parts.

Derivation: evolve expectations first

Let $\phi$ be smooth with compact support. Then

$$\partial_t\,\mathbb{E}_{X_t \sim p_t}[\phi(X_t)] \approx \frac{1}{\varepsilon}\Bigl(\mathbb{E}_{X\sim p_{t+\varepsilon}}[\phi(X)] - \mathbb{E}_{X\sim p_t}[\phi(X)]\Bigr).$$

Over a small $\varepsilon$, we have $X_{t+\varepsilon} \approx X + \varepsilon f + \sqrt{\varepsilon}\,g\,Z$ with $Z\sim\mathcal{N}(0,1)$. Taylor-expand $\phi$ to second order:

$$\phi(X + \varepsilon f + \sqrt{\varepsilon}\,g\,Z) = \phi(X) + \phi'(X)\,(\varepsilon f + \sqrt{\varepsilon}\,g\,Z) + \tfrac{1}{2}\phi''(X)\,(\sqrt{\varepsilon}\,g\,Z)^2 + O(\varepsilon^{3/2}).$$

Derivation: collect surviving terms

Expectation kills the $\sqrt{\varepsilon}\,\phi'(X) g Z$ term (mean zero) and replaces $Z^2$ by $1$:

$$\partial_t\,\mathbb{E}[\phi(X_t)] = \mathbb{E}_{X \sim p_t}\!\left[\phi'(X)\,f(X, t) + \tfrac{1}{2}\phi''(X)\,g^2(t)\right].$$

Rewrite as integrals and integrate by parts, using $p_t(\pm \infty) = 0$ and $\nabla p_t(\pm\infty) = 0$:

$$\int \phi(x)\,\partial_t p_t(x)\,dx = \int \phi(x)\bigl[-\partial_x(f\,p_t) + \tfrac{g^2}{2}\,\partial_x^2 p_t\bigr]\,dx.$$

Since this holds for all test functions $\phi$, the integrands match pointwise. That is Fokker–Planck.

03

Anderson's Reverse SDE

A backward SDE with the same marginals as the forward one

Anderson's theorem

Theorem (Anderson, 1982)

For the forward SDE $dX_t = f(X_t, t)\,dt + g(t)\,dZ_t$ with $X_0 \sim p_0$, the reverse-time SDE

$$d\bar{X}_t = \bigl(f(\bar{X}_t, t) - g^2(t)\,\partial_x \log p_t(\bar{X}_t)\bigr)\,dt + g(t)\,d\bar{Z}_t,\quad \bar{X}_T \sim p_T,$$

has the same marginals as the forward process: $\bar{X}_t \stackrel{(d)}{=} X_t$ for every $t \in [0,T]$. Here $d\bar{Z}_t$ is reverse-time Brownian motion.

Equivalently, running $Y_t = \bar{X}_{T-t}$ forward in time from $Y_0 \sim p_T$ with drift $-(f - g^2\,\partial_x \log p_{T-t})$ and diffusion $g(T-t)$ produces the same marginals, reversed.

Proof setup

Let $\{q_t\}$ denote the marginals of $Y_t$. By Fokker–Planck applied to $Y_t$:

$$\partial_t q_t(y) = \partial_y\!\Bigl[\bigl(f(y, T-t) - g^2(T-t)\,\partial_y \log p_{T-t}(y)\bigr)\,q_t(y)\Bigr] + \frac{g^2(T-t)}{2}\,\partial_y^2 q_t(y).$$

Let $\bar{p}_t$ be the marginals of $\bar{X}_t$. Because $\bar{p}_{T-t} = q_t$, a change of variable $t \mapsto T-t$ gives the PDE for $\bar{p}_t$ in its own time:

$$\partial_t \bar{p}_t(x) = -\partial_x\!\Bigl[\bigl(f(x, t) - g^2(t)\,\partial_x \log p_t(x)\bigr)\,\bar{p}_t(x)\Bigr] - \frac{g^2(t)}{2}\,\partial_x^2 \bar{p}_t(x).$$

Key step: $p_t$ solves the reverse PDE

Plug $\bar{p}_t = p_t$ into the reverse PDE and expand the score-shift term:

$$\begin{aligned} &-\partial_x\!\Bigl[\bigl(f - g^2\,\partial_x \log p_t\bigr)\,p_t\Bigr] - \tfrac{g^2}{2}\,\partial_x^2 p_t \\ &\qquad= -\partial_x(f\,p_t) + g^2\,\partial_x\bigl((\partial_x \log p_t)\,p_t\bigr) - \tfrac{g^2}{2}\,\partial_x^2 p_t \\ &\qquad= -\partial_x(f\,p_t) + g^2\,\partial_x(\partial_x p_t) - \tfrac{g^2}{2}\,\partial_x^2 p_t \\ &\qquad= -\partial_x(f\,p_t) + \tfrac{g^2}{2}\,\partial_x^2 p_t \;=\; \partial_t p_t. \end{aligned}$$

The middle equality uses $(\partial_x \log p_t)\,p_t = \partial_x p_t$. The last line is the forward Fokker–Planck. So $p_t$ indeed satisfies the reverse PDE, which implies $\bar{p}_t = p_t$ by uniqueness.

Why this matters for generation

Anderson's theorem is the continuous-time analog of the Bayes-based reverse step we derived in Lecture 1: to reverse diffusion we need the score function $\partial_x \log p_t$.

Generation recipe. Sample $\bar{X}_T \sim p_T$ (a known Gaussian for VP), then run the reverse SDE with drift $f - g^2 s_\theta$ and diffusion $g$. Discretize however you like.

The discrete DDPM sampling algorithm from Lecture 2 is exactly the Euler–Maruyama discretization of this reverse SDE at $\Delta t = $ one diffusion step.

04

Score Matching

Learning the score without knowing the marginals

The natural loss is intractable

We would like to train $s_\theta$ against the true score at every time $t$:

$$\mathcal{L}(\theta) = \int_0^T \lambda(t)\,\mathbb{E}_{X_t}\!\left[\bigl\|s_\theta(X_t, t) - \partial_x \log p_t(X_t)\bigr\|^2\right]dt.$$

We have samples from $p_t$ (run the forward process), but not the function $p_t$ itself, let alone its gradient. That makes the natural target untrainable.

The way out: rewrite the gradient of the marginal as an expectation over conditionals we do know.

Score matching theorem

Theorem (Vincent, 2011)

Up to a $\theta$-independent constant,

$$\mathcal{L}(\theta) = \int_0^T \lambda(t)\,\mathbb{E}_{X_0}\!\left[\mathbb{E}_{X_t\,|\,X_0}\!\left[\bigl\|s_\theta(X_t, t) - \partial_x \log p_{t\mid 0}(X_t\,|\,X_0)\bigr\|^2\right]\right] dt \;+\; C.$$

The conditional density $p_{t\mid 0}$ is known in closed form because it is just the noise-corruption kernel. For VP diffusion it is Gaussian, so its score is a trivial expression in $X_0$ and $X_t$.

Proof: marginal score = conditional expectation of conditional score

$$\begin{aligned} \partial_{X_t}\log p_t(X_t) &= \frac{\partial_{X_t}\,p_t(X_t)}{p_t(X_t)} \\ &= \frac{1}{p_t(X_t)}\,\partial_{X_t}\!\int p_{t\mid 0}(X_t\,|\,X_0)\,p_0(X_0)\,dX_0 \\ &= \int \partial_{X_t}\!\log p_{t\mid 0}(X_t\,|\,X_0)\cdot\frac{p_0(X_0)\,p_{t\mid 0}(X_t\,|\,X_0)}{p_t(X_t)}\,dX_0 \\ &= \int \partial_{X_t}\!\log p_{t\mid 0}(X_t\,|\,X_0)\,p_{0\mid t}(X_0\,|\,X_t)\,dX_0 \\ &= \mathbb{E}_{X_0\,|\,X_t}\!\bigl[\partial_{X_t}\!\log p_{t\mid 0}(X_t\,|\,X_0)\bigr]. \end{aligned}$$

Line 3 uses $\partial_{X_t} p_{t\mid 0} = p_{t\mid 0}\,\partial_{X_t}\log p_{t\mid 0}$ (a log-derivative trick). Line 4 is Bayes.

Proof: plug into the loss

Expand the squared norm and focus on the cross term:

$$\begin{aligned} &\mathbb{E}_{X_t}\!\left[\bigl\|s_\theta(X_t, t) - \partial_{X_t}\log p_t(X_t)\bigr\|^2\right] \\ &\quad = \mathbb{E}_{X_t}\!\left[\|s_\theta\|^2 - 2\,\langle s_\theta,\,\partial_{X_t}\log p_t\rangle\right] + C \\ &\quad = \mathbb{E}_{X_t}\!\left[\|s_\theta\|^2 - 2\,\langle s_\theta,\;\mathbb{E}_{X_0\,|\,X_t}[\partial_{X_t}\log p_{t\mid 0}]\rangle\right] + C \\ &\quad = \mathbb{E}_{X_0}\!\mathbb{E}_{X_t\,|\,X_0}\!\left[\|s_\theta - \partial_{X_t}\log p_{t\mid 0}(X_t\,|\,X_0)\|^2\right] + C'. \end{aligned}$$

Absorbing the $\|\partial_{X_t}\log p_t\|^2$ term into the constant completes the proof.

$\epsilon$-reparameterization

For reasonable $f, g$, the noise kernel is Gaussian:

$$X_t \stackrel{(d)}{=} \gamma_t\,X_0 - \sigma_t\,\epsilon,\quad \epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I),$$

so the conditional score is a closed-form expression in $\epsilon$:

$$\nabla\log p_{t\mid 0}(X_t\,|\,X_0) = \frac{\gamma_t X_0 - X_t}{\sigma_t^2} \stackrel{(d)}{=} \frac{\epsilon}{\sigma_t}.$$

Reparameterize the network as $s_\theta(X_t, t) = \epsilon_\theta(X_t, t)\,/\,\sigma_t$. The loss becomes noise prediction:

$$\tilde{\mathcal{L}}(\theta) = T\,\mathbb{E}_{X_0,\,\epsilon,\,t}\!\left[\frac{\lambda(t)}{\sigma_t^2}\,\bigl\|\epsilon_\theta(\gamma_t X_0 - \sigma_t\epsilon, t) - \epsilon\bigr\|^2\right].$$

Example: Ornstein–Uhlenbeck

For $dX_t = -\beta\,X_t\,dt + \sigma\,dW_t$, the conditional density is

$$p_{t\mid 0}(X_t\,|\,X_0) = \mathcal{N}\!\left(e^{-\beta t}\,X_0,\;\frac{\sigma^2}{2\beta}(1 - e^{-2\beta t})\right),$$

so $\gamma_t = e^{-\beta t}$ and $\sigma_t^2 = \tfrac{\sigma^2}{2\beta}(1-e^{-2\beta t})$. The score kernel has a closed form; score matching reduces to regression on a Gaussian mixture parameterized by $X_0$.

In practice, $\epsilon_\theta$ is trained instead of $s_\theta$ because it has a stable scale across time. The two are equivalent up to the factor $\sigma_t$.

A practical floor on $t$

At $t = 0$ the conditional has $\sigma_t = 0$, so the weight $\lambda(t)/\sigma_t^2$ blows up and the loss is ill-defined. A standard fix is to train from a strictly positive floor $t_0 > 0$:

$$\tilde{\mathcal{L}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{X_0,\,\epsilon,\,t \sim \text{Unif}(t_0, T)}\!\left[\frac{\lambda(t)}{\sigma_t^2}\,\|\epsilon_\theta(\gamma_t X_0 - \sigma_t\epsilon, t) - \epsilon\|^2\right].$$

The excluded tiny interval $[0, t_0)$ corresponds to nearly-clean data; empirically, training there is unstable and not particularly useful.

Multi-dimensional extension

Every result in this lecture generalizes to $\mathbf{X} \in \mathbb{R}^d$ with $f: \mathbb{R}^d \times [0,T] \to \mathbb{R}^d$ and $g(t) \in \mathbb{R}^{d\times d}$ (often $g(t) = \rho(t)\,I$):

  • Fokker–Planck becomes $\partial_t p_t = -\nabla_{\mathbf{x}}\cdot (f p_t) + \tfrac{1}{2}\,\mathrm{Tr}\!\bigl(g^\top H_{p_t} g\bigr)$, with Hessian $H_{p_t}$. For isotropic $g$ this simplifies to the Laplacian $\tfrac{\rho^2}{2}\Delta p_t$.
  • Anderson's reverse SDE uses the gradient score $\nabla_{\mathbf{x}}\log p_t$ in place of $\partial_x \log p_t$.
  • Score matching theorem holds verbatim with $\nabla_{\mathbf{X}_t}\log p_{t\mid 0}$.

DDPM and score matching are the same loss

With the DDPM reparameterization

$$\bar{\alpha}_n = \prod_{i=1}^n (1-\beta_i),\quad X^{(n)}\stackrel{(d)}{=}\sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_n}\,X^{(0)} - \sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_n}\,\epsilon_n,\quad \epsilon_\theta = \sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_n}\,s_\theta,\quad \tilde{\lambda}_n = \frac{\lambda_n\beta_n^2}{1-\bar{\alpha}_n},$$

the DDPM variational loss

$$\mathcal{L}(\theta) = \sum_{n=1}^{N}\tilde{\lambda}_n\,\mathbb{E}\!\left[\bigl\|\epsilon - \epsilon_\theta\bigl(\sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_n}X^{(0)} - \sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_n}\epsilon,\; n\bigr)\bigr\|^2\right]$$

is the discretization of the score-matching objective.

Takeaway: DDPM is discrete-time score matching on the VP SDE, trained via $\epsilon$-prediction.

Lecture recap

  • VE and VP are limits of a general SDE $dX_t = f\,dt + g\,dZ_t$.
  • Marginal densities $p_t$ evolve by the Fokker–Planck PDE, derived from test-function expectations and integration by parts.
  • Anderson's theorem gives a reverse-time SDE with matching marginals. Its drift depends on the score $\nabla \log p_t$.
  • The intractable loss $\|s_\theta - \nabla \log p_t\|^2$ equals the tractable score-matching loss using the known kernel $p_{t\mid 0}$, up to a constant.
  • In the $\epsilon$-parameterization, this is just noise regression. DDPM is a special case.
