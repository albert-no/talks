01

The Variance Preserving Process

Scaled noise so variance does not explode

Why variance preserving?

The VE process $X^{(n)} = X^{(n-1)} + Z^{(n)}$ has variance $\sigma_0^2 + n\sigma^2 \to \infty$. The terminal distribution depends on the data variance.

We prefer a process whose terminal distribution is fixed, ideally $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$, independent of the data. Then we can sample the starting point for reverse generation trivially.

Idea: shrink $X^{(n-1)}$ before adding noise, so the contribution of $X^{(0)}$ decays while the noise accumulates to unit variance.

VP forward process

Fix a schedule $\{\beta_n\}_{n=1}^N$ with $0 < \beta_n < 1$. The variance preserving process is

$$X^{(n)} = \sqrt{1-\beta_n}\,X^{(n-1)} + \sqrt{\beta_n}\,Z^{(n)},\quad Z^{(n)} \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)\ \text{i.i.d.}$$

Equivalently,

$$X^{(n)}\,|\,X^{(n-1)} \;\sim\; \mathcal{N}\!\left(\sqrt{1-\beta_n}\,X^{(n-1)},\; \beta_n\right).$$

If $\mathrm{Var}(X^{(n-1)}) = 1$, then $\mathrm{Var}(X^{(n)}) = (1-\beta_n)\cdot 1 + \beta_n = 1$. Unit variance is preserved, hence the name.

$n$ steps in a single shot

Define $\bar{\alpha}_n = \prod_{i=1}^n (1-\beta_i)$. Unrolling the recursion,

$$X^{(n)} \;\stackrel{(d)}{=}\; \sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_n}\,X^{(0)} + \sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_n}\,Z,\quad Z \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1).$$

Two consequences:

  • Training at any noise level is a single Gaussian draw.
  • As $\bar{\alpha}_n \to 0$, the signal fades and the noise approaches $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$.

Convergence to $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$: MGF proof

Using independence of $X^{(n-1)}$ and $Z^{(n)}$,

$$\begin{aligned} M_{X^{(n)}}(t) &= \mathbb{E}\bigl[e^{t\sqrt{1-\beta_n}X^{(n-1)}}\bigr]\,\mathbb{E}\bigl[e^{t\sqrt{\beta_n}Z^{(n)}}\bigr] \\ &= \mathbb{E}\bigl[e^{t\sqrt{1-\beta_n}X^{(n-1)}}\bigr]\,e^{\beta_n t^2/2} \\ &= \cdots \\ &= \mathbb{E}\bigl[e^{t\sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_n}X^{(0)}}\bigr]\,e^{(1-\bar{\alpha}_n)\,t^2/2}. \end{aligned}$$

If $\bar{\alpha}_n \to 0$, the first factor tends to $\mathbb{E}[1] = 1$ and the second to $e^{t^2/2}$. That is the MGF of $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$.

Conclusion: for any schedule with $\bar{\alpha}_n \to 0$, the terminal $X^{(N)}$ converges in distribution to standard Gaussian, independent of the data law.

02

DDPM Reverse Model

Gaussian approximation via Tweedie

Reverse conditional, VP version

Apply the same Bayes-plus-Tweedie argument from Lecture 1. For small $\beta_n$ the reverse conditional is again approximately Gaussian:

$$X^{(n-1)}\,|\,X^{(n)} \;\approx\; \mathcal{N}\bigl(\mu(X^{(n)}, n),\; \beta_n\bigr),$$

where

$$\mu(X^{(n)}, n) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1-\beta_n}}\left(X^{(n)} + \beta_n\,\frac{\partial}{\partial x}\log P_{X^{(n)}}(X^{(n)})\right).$$

This is the DDPM template from Ho, Jain & Abbeel (2020). The mean depends on the score function, exactly as in VE. The novelty is the scalar factors induced by the VP drift.

What the network learns

Replace the unknown score by a neural approximation $s_\theta(X^{(n)}, n)$:

$$\mu_\theta(X^{(n)}, n) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1-\beta_n}}\left(X^{(n)} + \beta_n\,s_\theta(X^{(n)}, n)\right).$$

Given $\mu_\theta$, sampling uses

$$p_\theta(X^{(n-1)}\,|\,X^{(n)}) = \mathcal{N}\bigl(\mu_\theta(X^{(n)}, n),\; \beta_n\bigr).$$

All that is left is picking a loss for $s_\theta$. We derive it from maximum likelihood.

03

Variational Lower Bound

Jensen, Markov identity, three-term decomposition

Notation

Write $X^{(i:j)} = (X^{(i)}, \ldots, X^{(j)})$. Let $q$ denote the forward distribution and $p_\theta$ the learned reverse distribution:

$$\begin{aligned} q\bigl(X^{(1:N)}\,|\,X^{(0)}\bigr) &= \prod_{n=1}^{N} q\bigl(X^{(n)}\,|\,X^{(n-1)}\bigr), \\ p_\theta\bigl(X^{(0:N)}\bigr) &= p\bigl(X^{(N)}\bigr)\prod_{n=1}^{N} p_\theta\bigl(X^{(n-1)}\,|\,X^{(n)}\bigr),\quad p(X^{(N)}) = \mathcal{N}(0,1). \end{aligned}$$

The marginal $p_\theta(X^{(0)}) = \int p_\theta(X^{(0:N)})\,dX^{(1:N)}$ is intractable, so we bound it.

From likelihood to the VLB

Insert $q(X^{(1:N)}\,|\,X^{(0)})$ and apply Jensen:

$$\begin{aligned} -\log p_\theta\bigl(X^{(0)}\bigr) &= -\log \int \frac{p_\theta(X^{(0:N)})}{q(X^{(1:N)}\,|\,X^{(0)})}\,q(X^{(1:N)}\,|\,X^{(0)})\,dX^{(1:N)} \\ &= -\log \mathbb{E}_q\!\left[\frac{p_\theta(X^{(0:N)})}{q(X^{(1:N)}\,|\,X^{(0)})}\;\middle|\;X^{(0)}\right] \\ &\leq \mathbb{E}_q\!\left[-\log \frac{p_\theta(X^{(0:N)})}{q(X^{(1:N)}\,|\,X^{(0)})}\;\middle|\;X^{(0)}\right]. \end{aligned}$$

The right-hand side is the variational upper bound on the negative log-likelihood we will minimize.

A Markov rewrite

Expand the ratio into products of conditionals:

$$\mathbb{E}_q\!\left[-\log p(X^{(N)}) - \sum_{n=1}^{N}\log\frac{p_\theta(X^{(n-1)}\,|\,X^{(n)})}{q(X^{(n)}\,|\,X^{(n-1)})}\right].$$

Using the Markov identity, for $n \geq 2$,

$$q\bigl(X^{(n)}\,|\,X^{(n-1)}\bigr)\,q\bigl(X^{(n-1)}\,|\,X^{(0)}\bigr) = q\bigl(X^{(n-1)}\,|\,X^{(n)}, X^{(0)}\bigr)\,q\bigl(X^{(n)}\,|\,X^{(0)}\bigr),$$

so $q(X^{(n)}\,|\,X^{(n-1)})$ can be replaced with the $X^{(0)}$-conditional form.

Three-term decomposition

After the Markov rewrite the bound becomes

$$\mathbb{E}_q\!\left[-\log\frac{p(X^{(N)})}{q(X^{(N)}\,|\,X^{(0)})} - \sum_{n=2}^{N}\log\frac{p_\theta(X^{(n-1)}\,|\,X^{(n)})}{q(X^{(n-1)}\,|\,X^{(n)},X^{(0)})} - \log p_\theta(X^{(0)}\,|\,X^{(1)})\right].$$

Name the three groups of terms:

$$L_N,\qquad \sum_{n=2}^{N} L_{n-1},\qquad L_0.$$

Each simplifies to a recognisable quantity.

$L_N$: match the prior

$$L_N \;=\; \mathbb{E}_{X^{(0)}\sim q}\Bigl[D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\bigl(q(X^{(N)}\,|\,X^{(0)})\,\Vert\,p(X^{(N)})\bigr)\Bigr].$$

This measures how close the forward endpoint is to the standard Gaussian prior. It has no $\theta$ dependence, so it is a constant from the optimizer's point of view.

We have already arranged for $L_N \to 0$: the VP schedule was designed so $X^{(N)}$ converges to $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$. $L_N$ is ignored during training.

$L_{n-1}$: match each reverse step

$$L_{n-1} \;=\; \mathbb{E}_q\Bigl[D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\bigl(q(X^{(n-1)}\,|\,X^{(n)}, X^{(0)})\,\Vert\,p_\theta(X^{(n-1)}\,|\,X^{(n)})\bigr)\Bigr].$$

Both sides are Gaussian (the target because of the next slide, the model by construction), so the KL is available in closed form.

$L_0 = \mathbb{E}_q[-\log p_\theta(X^{(0)}\,|\,X^{(1)})]$ is a boundary term. Its contribution to the total loss is negligible compared to the sum, and we drop it in the main loss.

Target posterior is Gaussian

Conditioned on $X^{(0)}$, all forward distributions are Gaussian. One can show

$$q\bigl(X^{(n-1)}\,|\,X^{(n)}, X^{(0)}\bigr) \;=\; \mathcal{N}\bigl(\mu_n(X^{(n)}, X^{(0)}),\; \beta_n\bigr),$$

where

$$\mu_n(X^{(n)}, X^{(0)}) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1-\beta_n}}\left(X^{(n)} + \beta_n\,\frac{\partial}{\partial X^{(n)}}\log q(X^{(n)}\,|\,X^{(0)})\right).$$

The conditional score $\partial_x \log q(X^{(n)}\,|\,X^{(0)})$ is known in closed form because $q(X^{(n)}\,|\,X^{(0)})$ is the Gaussian $\mathcal{N}(\sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_n} X^{(0)}, 1-\bar{\alpha}_n)$.

KL between matched-variance Gaussians

When both distributions have the same variance $\beta_n$, the KL reduces to the squared-mean mismatch:

$$L_{n-1} = \frac{1}{2\beta_n}\,\bigl\|\mu_n(X^{(n)}, X^{(0)}) - \mu_\theta(X^{(n)}, n)\bigr\|^2.$$

Plugging in the two mean formulas and using $\partial_x \log q(X^{(n)}\,|\,X^{(0)}) = -\frac{X^{(n)} - \sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_n}X^{(0)}}{1-\bar{\alpha}_n}$:

$$L_{n-1} = \frac{\beta_n}{2(1-\beta_n)}\left\|\frac{\partial}{\partial X^{(n)}}\log q(X^{(n)}\,|\,X^{(0)}) - s_\theta(X^{(n)}, n)\right\|^2.$$

$\epsilon$-prediction reparameterization

Write $X^{(n)} \stackrel{(d)}{=} \sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_n}\,X^{(0)} + \sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_n}\,\epsilon_n$ and reparameterize the score as

$$s_\theta(X^{(n)}, n) = -\,\frac{\epsilon_\theta(X^{(n)}, n)}{\sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_n}}.$$

Then the per-step loss becomes noise matching:

$$L_{n-1} = \frac{\beta_n}{2(1-\beta_n)(1-\bar{\alpha}_n)}\left\|\epsilon_n - \epsilon_\theta\!\bigl(\sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_n}\,X^{(0)} + \sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_n}\,\epsilon_n,\; n\bigr)\right\|^2.$$

Final objective. The network $\epsilon_\theta$ predicts the Gaussian noise that was mixed into $X^{(0)}$. Training is a plain regression.

04

Training and Sampling

The $\epsilon$-prediction loss in practice

Training algorithm

DDPM Training
  1. Sample $X^{(0)}$ from the data.
  2. Sample $n \sim \text{Unif}\{1, \ldots, N\}$.
  3. Sample $\epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$.
  4. Form the noisy input $X^{(n)} = \sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_n}\,X^{(0)} + \sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_n}\,\epsilon$.
  5. Take a gradient step on $\tilde{\lambda}_n\,\bigl\|\epsilon - \epsilon_\theta(X^{(n)}, n)\bigr\|^2$.

In the original DDPM paper, $\tilde{\lambda}_n$ is set to 1 (unweighted). The weight that drops out of the VLB is absorbed into the tuning.

Sampling algorithm

DDPM Sampling
  1. Sample $X^{(N)} \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$.
  2. For $n = N, N-1, \ldots, 1$:
    1. Sample $Z^{(n)} \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$.
    2. Compute $X^{(n-1)} = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{1-\beta_n}}\left(X^{(n)} - \dfrac{\beta_n}{\sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_n}}\,\epsilon_\theta(X^{(n)}, n)\right) + \sqrt{\beta_n}\,Z^{(n)}$.

The update is exactly $\mu_\theta(X^{(n)}, n)$ plus the injected Gaussian noise that matches the reverse-conditional variance $\beta_n$.

Where the approximation can break

Reverse is only approximate

The Gaussian form of $p_\theta(X^{(n-1)}\,|\,X^{(n)})$ relies on $\beta_n$ being small. Large step sizes break the approximation.

Score estimate is imperfect

$\epsilon_\theta$ is a neural network with finite capacity trained on finite data. Errors in the score carry through every sampling step.

Terminal is only approximately Gaussian

$X^{(N)}$ converges to $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$ only as $\bar{\alpha}_N \to 0$. We start sampling from a mismatched prior at finite $N$.

Lecture recap

  • VP forward: $X^{(n)} = \sqrt{1-\beta_n}\,X^{(n-1)} + \sqrt{\beta_n}\,Z^{(n)}$. Unit variance in, unit variance out.
  • Terminal distribution converges to $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$ whenever $\bar{\alpha}_n \to 0$ (MGF proof).
  • Reverse conditional is approximately Gaussian with mean given by the score function.
  • Variational lower bound decomposes into $L_N$ (constant) $+\ \sum L_{n-1}$ (matching) $+\ L_0$ (boundary, negligible).
  • The matching term collapses to $\epsilon$-prediction regression.

Lecture 3 recasts the whole construction as a stochastic differential equation.
