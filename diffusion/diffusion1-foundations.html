01

Generative Models

Diversity, distribution learning, why sampling is hard

Realistic Is Not Enough

One-photo generator: realistic, useless.

Good models need both:

  • Realism: plausible samples
  • Diversity: match data spread (race, age, pose, $\ldots$)

Learn the distribution $P_X$, not isolated points.

Sampling Is Non-Trivial

Closed-form density does not imply easy sampling.

Cauchy with scale $\gamma > 0$:

$$f(x) = \frac{1}{\pi}\,\frac{\gamma}{x^2+\gamma^2}.$$

How do we draw $X \sim f$ from this formula alone?

Inverse Transform Sampling (1D)

Let $F_X$ be the CDF, $U \sim \text{Unif}[0,1]$. Then:

$$\Pr\bigl(F_X^{-1}(U)\leq x\bigr) = \Pr\bigl(U \leq F_X(x)\bigr) = F_X(x).$$

So $F_X^{-1}(U) \sim f_X$.

Generate $U$ via fair coin flips:

$$U \approx 0.U_1 U_2 \cdots U_n\,{}_{(2)},\quad U_i \sim \text{Bernoulli}(\tfrac{1}{2}).$$

High Dimension Breaks This

For $\mathbf{X} = (X_1, \ldots, X_d)$: no scalar CDF to invert. Rejection and MCMC scale poorly with $d$.

Most generative models do not learn density. They learn a transformation from easy noise to data.

Diffusion: gradually corrupt data into noise, then reverse at generation time.

02

VE Forward Process

Gradually adding Gaussian noise (1D)

The Forward Process

$X \in \mathbb{R}$ scalar with unknown density $P_X$. Define:

$$X^{(n)} = X^{(n-1)} + Z^{(n)},\quad X^{(0)} = X,\quad Z^{(n)} \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\sigma^2)\ \text{i.i.d.}$$

Variance grows each step: variance exploding (VE).

$n$ Steps in One Shot

Sum of independent Gaussians is Gaussian:

$$X^{(n)} \stackrel{(d)}{=} X^{(0)} + \sqrt{n}\,Z,\quad Z \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\sigma^2).$$

Pick $n$, sample $Z$, jump directly to $X^{(n)}$.

Training trick: simulate one transition at a time, never the full chain.

Terminal Distribution

Let $\mu_0, \sigma_0^2$ be mean and variance of $X^{(0)}$. By linearity:

$$\mathbb{E}[X^{(N)}] = \mu_0,\qquad \mathrm{Var}(X^{(N)}) = \sigma_0^2 + N\sigma^2.$$

For large $N$, $N\sigma^2$ dominates:

$$X^{(N)} \;\approx\; \mathcal{N}(\mu_0,\; \sigma_0^2 + N\sigma^2).$$

(Informal for VE; formal MGF proof in Lecture 2 for VP.)

Goal: Reverse the Chain

Sampling from $\mathcal{N}(\mu_0, \sigma_0^2 + N\sigma^2)$ is trivial. Call it $\tilde{X}^{(N)}$.

Want a reverse rule:

$$\tilde{X}^{(N)} \to \tilde{X}^{(N-1)} \to \cdots \to \tilde{X}^{(0)},\qquad \tilde{X}^{(0)} \stackrel{(d)}{\approx} X.$$

Need reverse conditional $P_{X^{(n-1)}\,|\,X^{(n)}}$ at every step.

03

Reverse via Bayes

Taylor, complete the square, read off Gaussian

Setup

Drop indices: $X = \tilde{X}^{(n-1)}$, $Z = \tilde{Z}^{(n)}$, $Y = \tilde{X}^{(n)}$.

$$Y = X + Z,\qquad Y\,|\,X \sim \mathcal{N}(X, \sigma^2).$$

Want $X\,|\,Y$. By Bayes:

$$P_{X|Y}(x\,|\,y) = \frac{P_{Y|X}(y\,|\,x)\,P_X(x)}{P_Y(y)}.$$

Likelihood is Gaussian. Expand $P_X, P_Y$ when $\sigma^2 \ll 1$.

Step 1: Taylor Expand $P_X$

Likelihood mass concentrates within $O(\sigma)$ of $y$, so $x$ is close to $y$:

$$P_X(x) \;\approx\; P_X(y) + P'_X(y)(x-y).$$

Zeroth order: $P_X(y)$. Linear correction: $P'_X(y)(x-y)$.

Step 2: Approximate $P_Y(y)$

Integrate Taylor expansion against the Gaussian likelihood:

$$\begin{aligned} P_Y(y) &\approx \int \tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma^2}}\,e^{-\frac{(x-y)^2}{2\sigma^2}}\bigl(P_X(y) + P'_X(y)(x-y)\bigr)\,dx \\ &= P_X(y) + P'_X(y)\,\mathbb{E}_{X\sim\mathcal{N}(y,\sigma^2)}[X-y] \\ &= P_X(y). \end{aligned}$$

Linear term vanishes by Gaussian symmetry.

Step 3: Substitute Back

Using $P_Y(y) \approx P_X(y)$:

$$\begin{aligned} P_{X|Y}(x\,|\,y) &\approx P_{Y|X}(y\,|\,x)\!\left(1 + \tfrac{P'_X(y)}{P_X(y)}(x-y)\right) \\ &\approx \tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma^2}}\,e^{-\frac{(x-y)^2}{2\sigma^2}}\,\exp\!\left(\tfrac{P'_X(y)}{P_X(y)}(x-y)\right). \end{aligned}$$

Replace $1+a$ by $e^a$ (first-order anyway).

Step 4: Complete the Square

Merge exponentials. The quadratic becomes:

$$(x-y)^2 - 2\sigma^2\tfrac{P'_X(y)}{P_X(y)}(x-y) + \!\left(\sigma^2\tfrac{P'_X(y)}{P_X(y)}\right)^{\!2} = \!\left(x - y - \sigma^2\tfrac{P'_X(y)}{P_X(y)}\right)^{\!2}.$$

Recover a Gaussian in $x$:

$$P_{X|Y}(x\,|\,y) \approx \tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma^2}}\exp\!\left(-\tfrac{1}{2\sigma^2}\!\left(x-y-\sigma^2\tfrac{P'_X(y)}{P_X(y)}\right)^{\!2}\right).$$

Reverse Conditional, Compactly

Recognise $\dfrac{P'_X(y)}{P_X(y)} = \dfrac{\partial}{\partial y}\log P_X(y)$:

$$X\,|\,Y \;\stackrel{\text{approx.}}{\sim}\; \mathcal{N}\!\left(Y + \sigma^2\,\tfrac{\partial}{\partial y}\log P_X(Y),\; \sigma^2\right).$$

Small $\sigma^2$: reverse conditional is Gaussian, mean shifted by the score $\partial_y \log P_X$.

Reverse Sampling Rule

At each step, draw:

$$\tilde{x}^{(n-1)} = \tilde{x}^{(n)} + \sigma^2\,\tfrac{\partial}{\partial \tilde{x}^{(n)}} \log P_{X^{(n)}}\!\bigl(\tilde{x}^{(n)}\bigr) + \tilde{z}^{(n)},\quad \tilde{z}^{(n)} \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\sigma^2).$$

Two ingredients:

  • Score $\partial_x \log P_{X^{(n)}}(x)$ at every level $n$
  • Fresh Gaussian noise at every step

Two Natural Questions

Why not subtract noise?

Gaussian is symmetric: $-Z \stackrel{(d)}{=} Z$. Subtracting corrupts further, not denoise.

Why not MMSE?

$\hat{X}(Y) = \mathbb{E}[X\,|\,Y]$ is deterministic; variance shrinks below $\mathrm{Var}(X)$. Reverse process is distribution matching, not denoising.

Gaussian MMSE Mismatch

$X \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\sigma_x^2)$, $Y = X+Z$, $Z \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\sigma_z^2)$:

$$\hat{X}(Y) = \tfrac{\sigma_x^2}{\sigma_x^2 + \sigma_z^2}\,Y \;\sim\;\mathcal{N}\!\left(0,\,\tfrac{\sigma_x^4}{\sigma_x^2+\sigma_z^2}\right).$$

Variance too small. Add fresh noise to match:

$$\tilde{X} = \mathbb{E}[X\,|\,Y] + \tilde{Z},\quad \tilde{Z} \sim \mathcal{N}\!\left(0,\,\tfrac{\sigma_x^2\,\sigma_z^2}{\sigma_x^2 + \sigma_z^2}\right) \;\Rightarrow\; \tilde{X}\sim\mathcal{N}(0,\sigma_x^2).$$
04

Score & Tweedie

Learning the score via denoising

The Score Function

For each noise level $n$:

$$s(x;n) = \tfrac{\partial}{\partial x}\log P_{X^{(n)}}(x).$$

Depends on $n$ since marginal $P_{X^{(n)}}$ changes with noise level.

Exact $s(x;n)$ for all $n$: no neural network needed for the reverse process. Bottleneck is estimating $s$ from data.

Train a Denoiser, Not a Score

Network $f_\theta(x^{(n)};n)$ reconstructs $x^{(0)}$ from $x^{(n)}$:

$$\mathcal{L}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}\bigl[\,\|f_\theta(X^{(n)};n) - X^{(0)}\|^2\,\bigr].$$

Bayes-optimal squared-loss regressor is the conditional mean:

$$f_\theta(x^{(n)};n) \;\approx\; \mathbb{E}[X^{(0)}\,|\,X^{(n)}=x^{(n)}].$$

Objective never references $P_X$ or the score.

Tweedie's Formula

Theorem (Robbins, 1956 / Tweedie).

$X$ random, $Z \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\sigma^2)$ independent, $Y = X+Z$:

$$\mathbb{E}[X\,|\,Y=y] \;=\; y + \sigma^2\,\tfrac{\partial}{\partial y}\log P_Y(y).$$

Posterior mean of clean $X$ = noisy $y$ + scaled score of the noisy marginal.

Robbins, "An empirical Bayes approach to statistics," Berkeley Symp. 1956. Proof sketched in Q3.

Denoiser to Score

Apply Tweedie to VE at level $n$ (effective noise variance $n\sigma^2$):

$$f_\theta(x;n) \;\approx\; x + n\sigma^2\,\tfrac{\partial}{\partial x}\log P_{X^{(n)}}(x).$$

Solve for the score:

$$s_\theta(x;n) \;=\; \tfrac{f_\theta(x;n) - x}{n\sigma^2}.$$

A denoising network is a score network. Training reduces to regression.

What We Have

  • Forward: data into Gaussian noise
  • Reverse: needs only the score at each level
  • Training: denoiser + Tweedie

Missing: exact Gaussian terminal, ML-based loss, continuous-time SDE view.
