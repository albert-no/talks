01

What is a Generative Model?

Diversity, distribution learning, and why sampling is hard

Realistic is not enough

A face generator that always outputs the same photo of Albert is technically realistic, but nobody wants it.

A good generative model must

  • produce realistic samples, and
  • match the diversity of the data (race, age, sex, pose, $\ldots$).

In short, it must learn the distribution of the dataset, not just individual points.

Generative model: a mechanism that produces samples whose joint law approximates the data distribution $P_X$.

Sampling is non-trivial

Even with a closed-form density, drawing samples can be hard. Consider the Cauchy distribution with scale $\gamma > 0$:

$$f(x) = \frac{1}{\pi}\,\frac{\gamma}{x^2+\gamma^2}.$$

How do we produce $X \sim f$ given only this formula?

A closed-form density tells us how dense the probability is at each point, but it doesn't directly tell us how to draw samples.

Inverse transform sampling (1D)

Let $F_X$ be the CDF of $X$ and $U \sim \text{Unif}[0,1]$. Because $F_X$ is non-decreasing and $\Pr(U\leq u)=u$:

$$\Pr\bigl(F_X^{-1}(U)\leq x\bigr) = \Pr\bigl(U \leq F_X(x)\bigr) = F_X(x),$$

so $F_X^{-1}(U) \sim f_X$.

How do we generate $U$? Binary expansion of fair coin flips:

$$U \approx 0.U_1 U_2 \cdots U_n\,{}_{(2)},\quad U_i \sim \text{Bernoulli}(\tfrac{1}{2}).$$

Takeaway

In 1D we can sample from any distribution we can invert. The question is what breaks when we move to high dimension.

High dimension: even exact densities are hard

For a random vector $\mathbf{X} = (X_1, \ldots, X_d)$, we cannot simply invert a CDF. Rejection sampling and MCMC scale poorly with $d$.

Most generative models do not explicitly learn the density $P_{\mathbf{X}}$. Instead they learn a transformation from an easy-to-sample source (Gaussian, uniform) to the target distribution.

Diffusion models take this further: they learn a sequence of transformations that gradually turn data into noise, and then reverse the process at generation time.

02

Variance Exploding (VE) Forward Process

1D diffusion: gradually adding Gaussian noise

The forward process

Let $X \in \mathbb{R}$ be a scalar random variable we want to learn, with unknown density $P_X$. Define the forward process:

$$X^{(n)} = X^{(n-1)} + Z^{(n)},\quad X^{(0)} = X,\quad Z^{(n)} \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\sigma^2)\ \text{i.i.d.}$$

We gradually corrupt $X$ by Gaussian noise. Variance grows at every step, hence the name variance exploding (VE).

$n$ steps in a single shot

Because each $Z^{(n)}$ is independent Gaussian, the sum is Gaussian:

$$X^{(n)} \stackrel{(d)}{=} X^{(0)} + \sqrt{n}\,Z,\quad Z \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\sigma^2).$$

This lets us train the denoiser at any noise level without running the full chain: pick $n$, sample $Z$, and jump directly to $X^{(n)}$.

Training trick: the forward process collapses to a single sampling step. Diffusion training only ever simulates one transition at a time.

Terminal distribution

Let $\mu_0, \sigma_0^2$ be the mean and variance of $X^{(0)}$. By linearity,

$$\mathbb{E}[X^{(N)}] = \mu_0,\qquad \mathrm{Var}(X^{(N)}) = \sigma_0^2 + N\sigma^2.$$

For large $N$ the $N\sigma^2$ term dominates, and intuitively

$$X^{(N)} \;\approx\; \mathcal{N}(\tilde{\mu},\tilde{\sigma}^2),\quad \tilde{\mu} = \mu_0,\ \tilde{\sigma}^2 = \sigma_0^2 + N\sigma^2.$$

This argument is informal for VE. For variance-preserving (VP) diffusion in the next lecture we will give a formal MGF proof.

The goal: reverse the chain

Sampling from $\mathcal{N}(\tilde{\mu},\tilde{\sigma}^2)$ is trivial. Let $\tilde{X}^{(N)}$ be such a draw.

We want a reverse rule

$$\tilde{X}^{(N)} \to \tilde{X}^{(N-1)} \to \cdots \to \tilde{X}^{(0)}$$

such that $\tilde{X}^{(0)} \stackrel{(d)}{\approx} X$.

The task

For each step $n$, characterize the reverse conditional

$P_{X^{(n-1)}\,|\,X^{(n)}}.$

If we know it, we can sample the chain backwards.

03

Reverse Process via Bayes

Taylor expand, complete the square, read off a Gaussian

Setup

Drop indices for clarity. Write $X = \tilde{X}^{(n-1)}$, $Z = \tilde{Z}^{(n)}$, $Y = \tilde{X}^{(n)}$, so

$$Y = X + Z,\qquad Y\,|\,X \sim \mathcal{N}(X, \sigma^2).$$

We want $X\,|\,Y$. By Bayes:

$$P_{X|Y}(x\,|\,y) = \frac{P_{Y|X}(y\,|\,x)\,P_X(x)}{P_Y(y)}.$$

We know $P_{Y|X}$ (it is Gaussian by construction). We do not know $P_X$ or $P_Y$, but both can be expanded around $y$ when $\sigma^2 \ll 1$.

Step 1: Taylor expand $P_X(x)$ around $y$

Because $\sigma^2 \ll 1$, most of the mass of $P_{Y|X}$ sits within $O(\sigma)$ of $y$, so $x$ is close to $y$:

$$P_X(x) = P_X(y) + P'_X(y)(x-y) + O\bigl(\|x-y\|^2\bigr) \;\approx\; P_X(y) + P'_X(y)(x-y).$$

We treat $P_X(y)$ as the zeroth-order value and $P'_X(y)(x-y)$ as a small linear correction.

Step 2: Approximate $P_Y(y)$

Integrate the Taylor expansion against the Gaussian likelihood:

$$\begin{aligned} P_Y(y) &= \int P_{Y|X}(y\,|\,x)\,P_X(x)\,dx \\ &\approx \int \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma^2}}\,e^{-\frac{(x-y)^2}{2\sigma^2}}\,\bigl(P_X(y) + P'_X(y)(x-y)\bigr)\,dx \\ &= P_X(y) + P'_X(y)\,\mathbb{E}_{X\sim\mathcal{N}(y,\sigma^2)}[X-y] \\ &= P_X(y). \end{aligned}$$

The linear term integrates to zero because the Gaussian is symmetric about $y$.

Step 3: Substitute back

Using $P_Y(y) \approx P_X(y)$ and the Taylor expansion of $P_X(x)$:

$$\begin{aligned} P_{X|Y}(x\,|\,y) &\approx \frac{P_{Y|X}(y\,|\,x)\,\bigl(P_X(y) + P'_X(y)(x-y)\bigr)}{P_X(y)} \\ &= P_{Y|X}(y\,|\,x)\left(1 + \frac{P'_X(y)}{P_X(y)}(x-y)\right) \\ &\approx \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma^2}}\,e^{-\frac{(x-y)^2}{2\sigma^2}}\,\exp\!\left(\frac{P'_X(y)}{P_X(y)}(x-y)\right). \end{aligned}$$

Step 3: we replaced $1 + a$ by $e^{a}$ for small $a$, because we only kept first-order terms anyway.

Step 4: Complete the square

Merge the two exponentials. The quadratic in the exponent becomes

$$\begin{aligned} &(x-y)^2 \;-\; 2\sigma^2\,\frac{P'_X(y)}{P_X(y)}(x-y) \;+\; \left(\sigma^2\,\frac{P'_X(y)}{P_X(y)}\right)^2 \\ &\qquad=\; \left(x - y - \sigma^2\,\frac{P'_X(y)}{P_X(y)}\right)^{\!2}. \end{aligned}$$

So we recover a Gaussian in $x$:

$$P_{X|Y}(x\,|\,y) \approx \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma^2}}\exp\!\left(-\frac{1}{2\sigma^2}\left(x-y-\sigma^2\,\frac{P'_X(y)}{P_X(y)}\right)^{\!2}\right).$$

Reverse conditional, compactly

Recognising $\dfrac{P'_X(y)}{P_X(y)} = \dfrac{\partial}{\partial y}\log P_X(y)$:

$$X\,|\,Y \;\stackrel{\text{approx.}}{\sim}\; \mathcal{N}\!\left(Y + \sigma^2\,\frac{\partial}{\partial y}\log P_X(Y),\; \sigma^2\right).$$

Key insight: when $\sigma^2$ is small, the reverse conditional is again Gaussian, with a mean shifted by the score $\partial_y \log P_X$.

Reverse sampling, step by step

For each step, draw the next reverse sample by

$$\tilde{x}^{(n-1)} = \tilde{x}^{(n)} + \sigma^2\,\frac{\partial}{\partial \tilde{x}^{(n)}} \log P_{X^{(n)}}\!\bigl(\tilde{x}^{(n)}\bigr) + \tilde{z}^{(n)},$$

where $\tilde{z}^{(n)} \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\sigma^2)$.

Two ingredients:

  • the score $\partial_x \log P_{X^{(n)}}(x)$ at every noise level $n$, and
  • fresh Gaussian noise at every step.

Two natural questions

Why not subtract the noise?

The noise distribution is symmetric, $-Z \stackrel{(d)}{=} Z$. Subtracting is distributionally the same as adding: you corrupt further, not denoise.

Why not use the MMSE estimator?

MMSE gives $\hat{X}(Y) = \mathbb{E}[X\,|\,Y]$, which is deterministic. Its distribution has smaller variance than $X$, so it cannot match the target. The reverse process is not optimal denoising, it is distribution matching.

Gaussian example: MMSE vs. distribution matching

Let $X \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\sigma_x^2)$, $Y = X + Z$, $Z \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\sigma_z^2)$. Then

$$\hat{X}(Y) = \mathbb{E}[X\,|\,Y] = \frac{\sigma_x^2}{\sigma_x^2 + \sigma_z^2}\,Y\;\sim\;\mathcal{N}\!\left(0,\,\frac{\sigma_x^4}{\sigma_x^2+\sigma_z^2}\right).$$

Variance is too small. To match $\mathrm{Var}(X) = \sigma_x^2$ we add fresh noise:

$$\tilde{X} = \mathbb{E}[X\,|\,Y] + \tilde{Z},\quad \tilde{Z} \sim \mathcal{N}\!\left(0,\,\frac{\sigma_x^2\,\sigma_z^2}{\sigma_x^2 + \sigma_z^2}\right)\;\Rightarrow\;\tilde{X}\sim\mathcal{N}(0,\sigma_x^2).$$

The reverse-process update $\tilde{x}+\sigma^2 s(\tilde{x},n)+\tilde{z}$ is doing exactly this in the small-$\sigma$ regime.

04

Score Function & Tweedie's Formula

Learning the score via denoising

The score function

For each noise level $n$, define the score function

$$s(x;n) = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}\log P_{X^{(n)}}(x).$$

It depends on $n$ because the marginal $P_{X^{(n)}}$ changes with noise level.

Observation: if we had exact access to $s(x;n)$ for every $n$ and every $x$, no neural network is needed for the reverse process. The bottleneck is estimating $s$ from data.

Train a denoiser, not the score directly

Given only samples, train a network $f_\theta(x^{(n)};n)$ to reconstruct $x^{(0)}$ from its noisy version $x^{(n)}$. With squared loss:

$$\mathcal{L}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}\bigl[\,\|f_\theta(X^{(n)};n) - X^{(0)}\|^2\,\bigr].$$

The Bayes-optimal regressor for squared loss is the conditional expectation, so

$$f_\theta(x^{(n)};n) \;\approx\; \mathbb{E}[X^{(0)}\,|\,X^{(n)}=x^{(n)}].$$

Crucially, this training objective never references the unknown $P_X$ or the score function.

Tweedie's formula

Let $X$ be any random variable, $Z \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\sigma^2)$ independent, and $Y = X+Z$. Then

$$\mathbb{E}[X\,|\,Y=y] \;=\; y + \sigma^2\,\frac{\partial}{\partial y}\log P_Y(y).$$

The formula is remarkable: the posterior mean of the clean variable is the noisy observation plus a term governed by the score of the noisy marginal.

Proof sketch: start from $P_Y(y) = \int P_X(x)\,P_Z(y-x)\,dx$, compute $\partial_y P_Y$, and recognize $(y-x)/\sigma^2$ from the Gaussian. Details left as Q3.

From denoiser to score

Apply Tweedie to the VE process at noise level $n$, where the effective noise variance is $n\sigma^2$:

$$f_\theta(x;n) \;\approx\; \mathbb{E}[X^{(0)}\,|\,X^{(n)}=x] \;=\; x + n\sigma^2\,\frac{\partial}{\partial x}\log P_{X^{(n)}}(x).$$

Solving for the score:

$$s_\theta(x;n) \;=\; \frac{f_\theta(x;n) - x}{n\sigma^2}.$$

Summary: a denoising network is a score network in disguise. Training reduces to regression against the clean sample.

What we have so far

  • A forward process that smoothly turns data into Gaussian noise.
  • A reverse rule that needs only the score function at each noise level.
  • A training recipe: learn a denoiser, convert to score via Tweedie.

What is missing:

  • A version where the terminal distribution is exactly Gaussian, not just approximately.
  • A principled loss derived from maximum likelihood.
  • A continuous-time view connecting diffusion to SDEs.
