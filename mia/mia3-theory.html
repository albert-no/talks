Yeom et al. 2018

Generalization gap and overfitting bound

Yeom et al. 2018

Overfitting and Privacy Risk

First rigorous theory: overfitting as privacy vulnerability.

  • Bound: MIA advantage $\leq$ generalization gap
  • Threshold attack with guarantees
  • Connects membership and attribute inference
Yeom, Giacomelli, Fredrikson, Jha, "Privacy Risk in ML: Analyzing the Connection to Overfitting", CSF 2018
Yeom et al. 2018

The Generalization Gap

Population risk and empirical risk:

$$\begin{align*}R_{\text{pop}}(f_\theta) &= \mathbb{E}_{z \sim \mathcal{D}}[\ell(f_\theta, z)] \\ R_{\text{train}}(f_\theta) &= \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n \ell(f_\theta, z_i)\end{align*}$$

Generalization gap:

$$\Delta = R_{\text{pop}}(f_\theta) - R_{\text{train}}(f_\theta)$$

$\Delta > 0$: model overfits.

This gap is the MIA signal.

Yeom et al. 2018

The Threshold Attack

Simplest principled MIA:

$$\mathcal{A}(z) = \mathbf{1}\left[\ell(f_\theta, z) \leq \tau\right]$$

with $\tau = R_{\text{pop}}(f_\theta)$.

  • Members: $R_{\text{train}} = R_{\text{pop}} - \Delta < \tau$ → member
  • Non-members: loss $R_{\text{pop}} = \tau$ → non-member (on average)

No shadows, no classifier: single loss threshold.

Yeom et al. 2018

Yeom Theorem: Advantage Bound

Theorem (Yeom et al. 2018)

Threshold attack with $\tau = R_{\text{pop}}(f_\theta)$:

$$\text{Adv}_{\text{MI}}(\mathcal{A}) \leq \Delta = R_{\text{pop}}(f_\theta) - R_{\text{train}}(f_\theta)$$

MIA advantage upper-bounded by generalization gap.

Corollary: $\Delta = 0$ → zero leakage to threshold attack.

Yeom et al. 2018

Proof Sketch (1 of 3)

Advantage $= |\text{TPR} - \text{FPR}|$:

  1. Markov on TPR: $\text{TPR} \geq 1 - R_{\text{train}}/\tau$ ← here
  2. Markov on FPR: $\text{FPR} \leq R_{\text{pop}}/\tau$
  3. Subtract to get the gap $\Delta/\tau$

Training data has lower loss; gap is $\Delta$.

Yeom et al. 2018

Proof Sketch (2 of 3)

Advantage $= |\text{TPR} - \text{FPR}|$:

  1. Markov on TPR: $\text{TPR} \geq 1 - R_{\text{train}}/\tau$
  2. Markov on FPR: $\text{FPR} \leq R_{\text{pop}}/\tau$ ← here
  3. Subtract to get the gap $\Delta/\tau$

Training data has lower loss; gap is $\Delta$.

Yeom et al. 2018

Proof Sketch (3 of 3)

Advantage $= |\text{TPR} - \text{FPR}|$:

  1. Markov on TPR: $\text{TPR} \geq 1 - R_{\text{train}}/\tau$
  2. Markov on FPR: $\text{FPR} \leq R_{\text{pop}}/\tau$
  3. Subtract: $\text{TPR} - \text{FPR} \leq \Delta/\tau$ ← closes the chain

Training data has lower loss; gap is $\Delta$.

Yeom et al. 2018

Loss Distributions: Intuition

Loss distributions: members vs. non-members.

Well-Generalized

Distributions overlap: $\Delta \approx 0$, MIA fails.

← low loss     high loss →

Overfitted

Distributions separated: $\Delta \gg 0$, MIA succeeds.

← low loss     high loss →

■ Members    ■ Non-members

Yeom et al. 2018

Threshold on the Loss Axis

Threshold $\tau$ partitions the loss axis:

$$\underbrace{\ell < \tau}_{\text{Member}} \;\Big|\; \underbrace{\ell \geq \tau}_{\text{Non-member}}$$

True Positive

Members with $\ell < \tau$: area left of $\tau$ under the member curve.

False Positive

Non-members with $\ell < \tau$: area left of $\tau$ under the non-member curve.

$\tau$ trades TPR vs. FPR. Lower $\tau$: both fall.

Yeom et al. 2018

Connection to Attribute Inference

Yeom links MIA to attribute inference:

$$\text{Attribute Inference:} \quad \mathcal{A}(f_\theta, x_{\setminus s}) \to s$$

Infer sensitive $s$ from all other features.

  • Overfitting on sensitive attributes: higher member accuracy
  • Advantage bounded by generalization gap
  • MIA as prerequisite: membership first
Yeom et al. 2018

Implications

Regularization Helps

L2, dropout, early stopping shrink $\Delta$.

No Overfitting, No MIA

$\Delta = 0$: threshold attack has zero advantage.

Privacy-Utility Tension

Some overfitting may be needed for accuracy.

Simple Baseline

Loss threshold: principled, zero-cost.

Yeom et al. 2018

Overfitting View: Limitations

  • Average gap, not per-sample: outliers still vulnerable at $\Delta \approx 0$.
  • Loose bound: actual advantage often $\ll \Delta$.
  • Threshold only: LR attacks succeed where thresholding fails.

Open question: the optimal attack and its power.

The Bayes-optimal attack

Toward Bayes-Optimal Attack

Yeom: principled, but limited. Open question:

What is the Bayes-optimal MIA?

Does white-box access help?

Sablayrolles et al. (2019): loss is (nearly) sufficient.

Sablayrolles et al. 2019

Sablayrolles: BB vs. WB Optimality

Optimal MIA: surprising BB = WB equivalence.

  • Optimal attack: likelihood ratio (Neyman-Pearson)
  • Gradients, activations: negligible beyond loss
  • Loss: near-sufficient statistic for membership
Sablayrolles et al., "White-box vs Black-box: Bayes Optimal Strategies for Membership Inference", ICML 2019
Sablayrolles et al. 2019

The Bayes Optimal MIA

Signal $\phi(z)$ (loss, gradients).

Optimal attack: likelihood ratio test:

$$\Lambda(z) = \frac{p(\phi(z) \mid z \in D)}{p(\phi(z) \mid z \notin D)} \quad \gtrless \quad \tau$$

By Neyman-Pearson: LR maximizes power at fixed significance.

Which signal $\phi(z)$? Loss $\ell$? Gradients $\nabla_\theta \ell$? Full internal state?

Sablayrolles et al. 2019

Optimal Statistic: Loss Ratio

Loss-based attack: $\phi(z) = \ell(f_\theta, z)$:

$$\Lambda(z) = \frac{p(\ell(f_\theta, z) \mid z \in D)}{p(\ell(f_\theta, z) \mid z \notin D)}$$

Estimate two distributions:

$p(\ell \mid \text{member})$

Low loss, concentrated.

$p(\ell \mid \text{non-member})$

Higher loss, more spread.

Ratio: loss surprise under member vs. non-member hypothesis.

Sablayrolles et al. 2019

White-Box Features

White-box: adversary sees much richer signals.

FeatureNotation
Loss$\ell(f_\theta, z)$
Per-layer gradients$\nabla_{W_l} \ell$
Intermediate activations$h_l(x)$
Loss gradient w.r.t. input$\nabla_x \ell$

White-box signal (concatenation):

$$\phi_{\text{WB}}(z) = \left[\ell, \; \|\nabla_{W_1} \ell\|, \ldots, \|\nabla_{W_L} \ell\|, \; h_1, \ldots, h_L\right]$$
Sablayrolles et al. 2019

Why BB Should Approach WB

White-box features: gradients, activations, loss.

All depend on: how the model fits $z$.

Loss captures: the scalar summary of that fit.

Large model: parameters $\gg$ data.

Each training point uniquely imprints on the loss surface.

Gradient information becomes redundant.

Sablayrolles et al. 2019

Theorem: BB Converges to WB

Theorem (Sablayrolles et al. 2019)

Asymptotic regime (large models, standard training): LR on loss alone converges to LR on all white-box features:

$$\Lambda_{\text{BB}}(z) \;\xrightarrow{\text{large models}}\; \Lambda_{\text{WB}}(z)$$

Implication: loss is near-sufficient.

Gradients, activations: diminishing returns.

Sablayrolles et al. 2019

Loss Sufficiency: Intuition

  • Loss: compresses how the model treats $z$
  • Gradients: determined by loss; no extra membership info
  • Activations: summarized by loss

Loss answers: "how well does the model fit $z$?" — the MIA question.

Caveat: asymptotic. Small models may give white-box gain.

Sablayrolles et al. 2019

Practical Implications

For Deployers

Restricting API: negligible gain. Label-only still leaks.

For Attackers

Skip white-box. Calibrate the loss instead.

Shifts MIA research focus: which signal → how to calibrate.

Core challenge of modern MIA.

Sablayrolles et al. 2019

Salem et al. 2019

ML-Leaks: Relaxed Assumptions

How much does the attacker need to know?

Progressively removed Shokri's assumptions. Attack still effective.

Salem, Zhang, Humbert, Berrang, Fritz, Backes, "ML-Leaks: Model and Data Independent MIA and Defenses", NDSS 2019
Salem et al. 2019

Three Progressive Relaxations

  • Relaxation 1: shadows with different architecture
  • Relaxation 2: shadow data from different distribution (even unrelated)
  • Relaxation 3: no shadows; threshold on max confidence

→

Salem et al. 2019

Relaxation 1: Different Architecture

Any shadow architecture, regardless of target:

Example

Target: ResNet-50

Shadow: 3-layer MLP

Why It Works

Confidence gap is architecture-agnostic.

Result: ~1-2% drop vs matched architecture.

Salem et al. 2019

Relaxation 2: Different Data Distribution

Train shadows on unrelated data:

Example

Target: CIFAR-10

Shadows: MNIST

Why It Works

Trained vs untrained confidence pattern is universal.

Result: ~2-4% drop vs same-distribution shadows.

Salem et al. 2019

Relaxation 3: No Shadow Models

Extreme case: no shadow models.

$$\mathcal{A}(f_\theta, x) = \mathbf{1}\left[\max_c f_\theta(x)_c > \tau\right]$$

Three ways to set $\tau$:

Fixed

Constant $\tau$.

Percentile

$\tau$ at a percentile of reference confidences.

Entropy

Threshold on entropy $H(f_\theta(x))$.

Salem et al. 2019

Results: Graceful Degradation

Attack VariantKnowledge RequiredAvg. AccuracyDrop
Shokri (full)Architecture + data + shadows~79%—
Threshold onlyNothing~74%−5%

Only ~5% drop from full Shokri to zero-knowledge threshold.

Salem et al. 2019

ML-Leaks: Takeaway

Minimal adversary knowledge sufficient.

Shadows helpful, not necessary.

Signal: property of overfitting, not access level.

  • Assume a strong, unconstrained attacker.
  • Label-only access still leaks membership.

Why Gradients as Features?

Intuition

Members: small, structured gradients.

Non-members: larger, noisier gradients.

In Practice

Gradients give a modest gain for small models.

Gain diminishes with model size.

Nasr et al. 2019

Federated Learning Vulnerability

Federated learning: clients share gradient updates, exposing white-box info.

Client A
→
$\Delta\theta_A$
→
Server (agg.)
←
$\Delta\theta_B$

Client B

Any participant can MIA shared gradients. FL: white-box access for all parties.

Nasr et al. 2019

Passive vs. Active Attacks in FL

Passive

Observe gradient updates; no manipulation.

Uses the gradient attack model.

Active

Manipulate the global model to amplify the membership signal.

Accuracy gain: +15-20%.

Active: model tuned to be sensitive to target points.

Amplifies the member/non-member gradient gap.
