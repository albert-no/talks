Yeom et al. 2018

Generalization gap and the overfitting bound

Yeom et al. 2018

Privacy Risk in ML: Connection to Overfitting

First rigorous theoretical analysis connecting overfitting to privacy vulnerability.

  • Formal bound: MIA advantage $\leq$ generalization gap
  • Simple threshold attack with theoretical guarantees
  • Connects membership and attribute inference

"Privacy Risk in Machine Learning: Analyzing the Connection to Overfitting"

CSF 2018 — Yeom, Giacomelli, Fredrikson, Jha

Yeom et al. 2018

The Generalization Gap

Define the population risk and empirical risk:

$$R_{\text{pop}}(f_\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{z \sim \mathcal{D}}[\ell(f_\theta, z)] \qquad R_{\text{train}}(f_\theta) = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n \ell(f_\theta, z_i)$$

The generalization gap is:

$$\Delta = R_{\text{pop}}(f_\theta) - R_{\text{train}}(f_\theta)$$

$\Delta > 0$ means the model fits training data better than unseen data — it has overfit. This gap is exactly the signal that MIA exploits.

Yeom et al. 2018

The Threshold Attack

The simplest MIA with theoretical backing:

$$\mathcal{A}(z) = \mathbf{1}\left[\ell(f_\theta, z) \leq \tau\right]$$

where $\tau = R_{\text{pop}}(f_\theta)$ (the expected population loss).

  • Members have average loss $R_{\text{train}} = R_{\text{pop}} - \Delta < \tau$ → classified as members
  • Non-members have average loss $R_{\text{pop}} = \tau$ → classified as non-members (on average)

No shadow models, no neural network attack classifier — just a single threshold on the loss.

Yeom et al. 2018

Theorem: MIA Advantage ≤ Generalization Gap

Theorem (Yeom et al. 2018)

For the threshold attack with $\tau = R_{\text{pop}}(f_\theta)$:

$$\text{Adv}_{\text{MI}}(\mathcal{A}) \leq \Delta = R_{\text{pop}}(f_\theta) - R_{\text{train}}(f_\theta)$$

The membership inference advantage is upper-bounded by the generalization gap.

Corollary: A perfectly generalizing model ($\Delta = 0$) leaks zero membership information to the threshold attack.

Yeom et al. 2018

Proof Sketch

The advantage is $|\text{TPR} - \text{FPR}|$:

  • TPR = $\Pr[\ell(f_\theta, z) \leq \tau \mid z \in D_{\text{train}}]$ — probability that a member has loss below threshold
  • FPR = $\Pr[\ell(f_\theta, z) \leq \tau \mid z \sim \mathcal{D}]$ — probability that a random point has loss below threshold
  • By Markov's inequality, $\text{TPR} \geq 1 - R_{\text{train}}/\tau$ and $\text{FPR} \leq R_{\text{pop}}/\tau$
  • The gap: $\text{TPR} - \text{FPR} \leq (R_{\text{pop}} - R_{\text{train}})/\tau = \Delta/\tau$

The key step: training data has systematically lower loss than unseen data, and the gap is exactly $\Delta$.

Yeom et al. 2018

$$\underbrace{\ell < \tau}_{\text{Predict: Member}} \qquad | \qquad \underbrace{\ell \geq \tau}_{\text{Predict: Non-member}}$$

Choosing $\tau$ trades off TPR and FPR. A lower $\tau$ reduces FPR but also reduces TPR.

Yeom et al. 2018

Connection to Attribute Inference

Yeom et al. also connect membership inference to attribute inference:

$$\text{Attribute Inference:} \quad \mathcal{A}(f_\theta, x_{\setminus s}) \to s$$

Given all features except the sensitive attribute $s$, infer $s$ from the model's behavior.

  • If a model overfits on sensitive attributes, its predictions are more accurate for training members
  • Attribute inference advantage is also bounded by the generalization gap
  • MIA is a prerequisite: you need to know someone is a member before attribute inference is meaningful
Yeom et al. 2018

Implications

Regularization Helps Privacy

Anything that reduces the generalization gap (L2, dropout, early stopping, data augmentation) also reduces MIA vulnerability.

No Overfitting → No MIA

If $\Delta = 0$, the threshold attack has zero advantage. Perfect generalization implies the model treats members and non-members identically.

Privacy-Utility Tension

Some overfitting may be necessary for good performance. Reducing $\Delta$ to zero may require sacrificing accuracy.

Simple Baseline

The loss threshold is a principled, zero-cost baseline that should always be compared against.

Yeom et al. 2018

Limitations of the Overfitting View

  • Well-generalized models can still leak. The generalization gap is an average measure — individual samples can be highly vulnerable even when $\Delta$ is small.
  • Per-sample risk varies enormously. Outliers, rare examples, and mislabeled data are much more vulnerable than typical examples.
  • The bound is loose. The actual MIA advantage can be much smaller than $\Delta$ — the theorem gives a worst case.
  • Only covers the threshold attack. More sophisticated attacks (likelihood ratios) can succeed even when the threshold attack fails.

Key question: What is the optimal attack, and how powerful can it be?

Sablayrolles et al. 2019

The Bayes-optimal attack

Toward the Optimal Attack

Yeom showed that the threshold attack is principled but limited. The natural next question:

What is the Bayes optimal membership inference attack?

And does white-box access (full model parameters) help?

Sablayrolles et al. (2019) answered both questions — with a surprising result about the sufficiency of the loss.

Sablayrolles et al. 2019

White-Box vs. Black-Box: Bayes Optimal Strategies

Derived the theoretically optimal MIA and proved a surprising equivalence between black-box and white-box attacks.

  • Optimal attack = likelihood ratio test (as expected from Neyman-Pearson)
  • White-box features (gradients, activations) add negligible information beyond the loss
  • The loss is a near-sufficient statistic for membership

"White-box vs Black-box: Bayes Optimal Strategies for Membership Inference"

ICML 2019

Sablayrolles et al. 2019

The Bayes Optimal MIA

Given an observable signal $\phi(z)$ (e.g., loss, gradients), the optimal attack is the likelihood ratio test:

$$\Lambda(z) = \frac{p(\phi(z) \mid z \in D)}{p(\phi(z) \mid z \notin D)} \quad \gtrless \quad \tau$$

This follows directly from the Neyman-Pearson lemma (Slide 26) — the likelihood ratio maximizes power at any given significance level.

The question is: what signal $\phi(z)$ should we use? The loss $\ell(f_\theta, z)$? The gradients $\nabla_\theta \ell$? The full internal state?

Sablayrolles et al. 2019

Deriving the Optimal Test Statistic

For loss-based attack, the signal is $\phi(z) = \ell(f_\theta, z)$:

$$\Lambda(z) = \frac{p(\ell(f_\theta, z) \mid z \in D)}{p(\ell(f_\theta, z) \mid z \notin D)}$$

We need to estimate two distributions:

$p(\ell \mid \text{member})$

Distribution of loss on training points — typically low loss, concentrated

$p(\ell \mid \text{non-member})$

Distribution of loss on unseen points — typically higher loss, more spread

The ratio tells us: how "surprised" should we be to see this loss value, given each hypothesis?

Sablayrolles et al. 2019

White-Box Features

In a white-box setting, the adversary has access to much richer signals:

FeatureNotationDimension
Loss$\ell(f_\theta, z)$Scalar
Per-layer gradients$\nabla_{W_l} \ell$$\sum_l |W_l|$
Intermediate activations$h_l(x)$$\sum_l d_l$
Loss gradient w.r.t. input$\nabla_x \ell$$d_{\text{input}}$

The white-box signal is the concatenation:

$$\phi_{\text{WB}}(z) = \left[\ell, \; \|\nabla_{W_1} \ell\|, \ldots, \|\nabla_{W_L} \ell\|, \; h_1, \ldots, h_L\right]$$
Sablayrolles et al. 2019

Key Theorem: Black-Box ≈ White-Box

Theorem (Sablayrolles et al. 2019)

In the asymptotic regime (large models, standard training), the likelihood ratio based on the loss alone converges to the likelihood ratio based on all white-box features:

$$\Lambda_{\text{BB}}(z) \;\xrightarrow{\text{large models}}\; \Lambda_{\text{WB}}(z)$$

Implication: The loss is a near-sufficient statistic for membership. Gradients and activations add diminishing information.

Sablayrolles et al. 2019

Why the Loss Is (Nearly) Sufficient

  • The loss $\ell(f_\theta, z)$ compresses all information about how the model treats $z$
  • Gradients $\nabla_\theta \ell$ are functionally determined by the loss landscape at $z$ — they don't add independent information about membership
  • Activations $h_l(x)$ feed into the loss through the forward pass — the loss is their aggregate summary

Intuition: The loss answers the question "how well does the model fit this point?" That's exactly the question membership inference asks. Everything else is redundant.

Caveat: this is an asymptotic result. For small models or specific architectures, white-box features can provide a modest improvement.

Sablayrolles et al. 2019

Practical Implications

For Model Deployers

Restricting API access to labels or confidence scores doesn't meaningfully reduce privacy risk. Even label-only access leaks membership.

For Attackers

Don't bother with white-box attacks when the loss is available. Focus effort on better calibration of the loss distribution.

This result shifts the focus of MIA research from what signal to use to how to calibrate the signal — the central challenge of modern MIA.

Sablayrolles et al. 2019

Salem et al. 2019

ML-Leaks: Relaxing All Assumptions

Salem et al. systematically tested: how much does the attacker really need to know?

They progressively removed Shokri's assumptions and found the attack remains effective.

"ML-Leaks: Model and Data Independent MIA and Defenses"

NDSS 2019 — Salem, Zhang, Humbert, Berrang, Fritz, Backes

Salem et al. 2019

Three Progressive Relaxations

  • Relaxation 1: Shadow models can use a different architecture than the target
  • Relaxation 2: Shadow training data can come from a different distribution (even unrelated)
  • Relaxation 3: No shadow models at all — just threshold on the max confidence

Salem et al. 2019

Relaxation 1: Different Architecture

Use any model architecture for shadows, regardless of the target's architecture:

Example

Target: ResNet-50

Shadow: Simple 3-layer MLP

Why It Works

The confidence distribution gap between members and non-members is an architecture-agnostic property of overfitting — not specific to any model type.

Result: ~1–2% accuracy drop compared to matched architecture shadows.

Salem et al. 2019

Relaxation 2: Different Data Distribution

Train shadow models on completely unrelated data:

Example

Target trained on: CIFAR-10

Shadows trained on: MNIST

Why It Works

The attack model learns "what membership looks like in general" — the pattern of confidence on trained vs. untrained data is universal across datasets.

Result: ~2–4% accuracy drop compared to same-distribution shadows. Still significantly above random.

Salem et al. 2019

Relaxation 3: No Shadow Models

The most extreme simplification — no shadow models at all:

$$\mathcal{A}(f_\theta, x) = \mathbf{1}\left[\max_c f_\theta(x)_c > \tau\right]$$

Three ways to set the threshold $\tau$:

Fixed

$\tau = 0.5$ or some constant. Simple but crude.

Percentile

Set $\tau$ at a percentile of confidence scores on reference data.

Entropy

Threshold on entropy $H(f_\theta(x))$ instead of max confidence.

Salem et al. 2019

ML-Leaks: Takeaway

The adversary needs far less than previously assumed.

Shadow models are helpful but not necessary. The membership signal is a fundamental property of overfitting, not an artifact of specific attack procedures.

This has important implications for defenses: you cannot assume the attacker will be limited. Even minimal information (just the predicted label) can enable membership inference.

Why Gradients as Features?

Intuition

For members, the model is near a local minimum of the loss for $z$. Gradients are small and structured.

For non-members, the loss surface is less explored. Gradients are larger and more random.

In Practice

Despite the theoretical result (BB ≈ WB), gradients provide a modest advantage for small models and specific architectures.

The advantage diminishes as model size increases (confirming Sablayrolles).

Nasr et al. 2019

The server (or any participant) can perform MIA on the shared gradient updates. FL doesn't inherently protect membership privacy — it actually makes white-box information available to more parties.

Nasr et al. 2019

Passive vs. Active Attacks in FL

Passive Attack

The attacker observes gradient updates without modifying the training process.

Uses the gradient-based attack model from Slide 90.

Active Attack

The attacker manipulates the global model or aggregation to amplify the membership signal in victims' gradients.

Increases MIA accuracy by 15–20%.

Active attacks modify the model to make it "sensitive" to specific data points — amplifying the difference in gradient behavior between members and non-members.
