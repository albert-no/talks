01

Yeom et al. 2018

Generalization gap and overfitting bound

Yeom et al. 2018

Privacy Risk in ML: Connection to Overfitting

First rigorous theory: overfitting drives privacy vulnerability.

  • Bound: MIA advantage $\leq$ generalization gap
  • Threshold attack with guarantees
  • Connects membership and attribute inference
Yeom, Giacomelli, Fredrikson, Jha, "Privacy Risk in ML: Analyzing the Connection to Overfitting", CSF 2018
Yeom et al. 2018

The Generalization Gap

Population risk and empirical risk:

$$R_{\text{pop}}(f_\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{z \sim \mathcal{D}}[\ell(f_\theta, z)] \qquad R_{\text{train}}(f_\theta) = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n \ell(f_\theta, z_i)$$

Generalization gap:

$$\Delta = R_{\text{pop}}(f_\theta) - R_{\text{train}}(f_\theta)$$

$\Delta > 0$: model overfits.

This gap is the MIA signal.

Yeom et al. 2018

The Threshold Attack

Simplest principled MIA:

$$\mathcal{A}(z) = \mathbf{1}\left[\ell(f_\theta, z) \leq \tau\right]$$

with $\tau = R_{\text{pop}}(f_\theta)$.

  • Members: $R_{\text{train}} = R_{\text{pop}} - \Delta < \tau$ → member
  • Non-members: loss $R_{\text{pop}} = \tau$ → non-member (on average)

No shadow models, no attack classifier: single loss threshold.

Yeom et al. 2018

Theorem: MIA Advantage ≤ Generalization Gap

Theorem (Yeom et al. 2018)

Threshold attack with $\tau = R_{\text{pop}}(f_\theta)$:

$$\text{Adv}_{\text{MI}}(\mathcal{A}) \leq \Delta = R_{\text{pop}}(f_\theta) - R_{\text{train}}(f_\theta)$$

MIA advantage upper-bounded by generalization gap.

Corollary: $\Delta = 0$ → zero leakage to threshold attack.

Yeom et al. 2018

Proof Sketch

Advantage $= |\text{TPR} - \text{FPR}|$:

  • Markov: $\text{TPR} \geq 1 - R_{\text{train}}/\tau$
  • Markov: $\text{FPR} \leq R_{\text{pop}}/\tau$
  • Subtract: $\text{TPR} - \text{FPR} \leq \Delta/\tau$

Training data has lower loss; gap is $\Delta$.

Yeom et al. 2018

Overfitting Intuition: Loss Distributions

Loss distributions: members vs. non-members.

Well-Generalized

Distributions overlap: $\Delta \approx 0$, MIA fails.

← low loss     high loss →

Overfitted

Distributions separated: $\Delta \gg 0$, MIA succeeds.

← low loss     high loss →

■ Members    ■ Non-members

Yeom et al. 2018

Loss Distribution & Decision Threshold

Threshold $\tau$ partitions the loss axis:

$$\underbrace{\ell < \tau}_{\text{Predict: Member}} \qquad | \qquad \underbrace{\ell \geq \tau}_{\text{Predict: Non-member}}$$

True Positive

Members with $\ell < \tau$: area left of $\tau$ under the member curve.

False Positive

Non-members with $\ell < \tau$: area left of $\tau$ under the non-member curve.

$\tau$ trades TPR vs. FPR.

Lower $\tau$: both fall.

Yeom et al. 2018

Connection to Attribute Inference

Yeom also links MIA to attribute inference:

$$\text{Attribute Inference:} \quad \mathcal{A}(f_\theta, x_{\setminus s}) \to s$$

Infer sensitive attribute $s$ from all other features.

  • Overfitting on sensitive attributes: higher accuracy on members
  • Advantage also bounded by generalization gap
  • MIA is a prerequisite: know membership first
Yeom et al. 2018

Implications

Regularization Helps

L2, dropout, early stopping shrink $\Delta$.

No Overfitting, No MIA

$\Delta = 0$: threshold attack has zero advantage.

Privacy-Utility Tension

Some overfitting may be needed for accuracy.

Simple Baseline

Loss threshold: principled, zero-cost.

Yeom et al. 2018

Limitations of the Overfitting View

  • Well-generalized models still leak: $\Delta$ is average; individual samples can be vulnerable.
  • Per-sample risk varies: outliers and rare examples far more vulnerable.
  • Loose bound: actual advantage can be much smaller than $\Delta$.
  • Threshold only: likelihood-ratio attacks succeed where thresholding fails.

Key question: what is the optimal attack, and how powerful?

The Bayes-optimal attack

Toward the Optimal Attack

Yeom's threshold attack: principled but limited.

Next question:

What is the Bayes optimal MIA?

Sablayrolles et al. (2019): surprising result on loss sufficiency.

Sablayrolles et al. 2019

White-Box vs. Black-Box: Bayes Optimal Strategies

Derived the optimal MIA: surprising BB = WB equivalence.

  • Optimal attack = likelihood ratio (Neyman-Pearson)
  • White-box features (gradients, activations): negligible beyond loss
  • Loss: near-sufficient statistic for membership
Sablayrolles et al., "White-box vs Black-box: Bayes Optimal Strategies for Membership Inference", ICML 2019
Sablayrolles et al. 2019

The Bayes Optimal MIA

Signal $\phi(z)$ (loss, gradients).

Optimal attack: likelihood ratio test:

$$\Lambda(z) = \frac{p(\phi(z) \mid z \in D)}{p(\phi(z) \mid z \notin D)} \quad \gtrless \quad \tau$$

Direct from Neyman-Pearson (Slide 26): LR maximizes power at fixed significance.

Which signal $\phi(z)$? Loss $\ell$? Gradients $\nabla_\theta \ell$? Full internal state?

Sablayrolles et al. 2019

Deriving the Optimal Test Statistic

Loss-based attack: $\phi(z) = \ell(f_\theta, z)$:

$$\Lambda(z) = \frac{p(\ell(f_\theta, z) \mid z \in D)}{p(\ell(f_\theta, z) \mid z \notin D)}$$

Estimate two distributions:

$p(\ell \mid \text{member})$

Low loss, concentrated.

$p(\ell \mid \text{non-member})$

Higher loss, more spread.

Ratio: how "surprising" is this loss under each hypothesis?

Sablayrolles et al. 2019

White-Box Features

White-box: adversary sees much richer signals.

FeatureNotationDimension

Per-layer gradients$\nabla_{W_l} \ell$$\sum_l |W_l|$
Intermediate activations$h_l(x)$$\sum_l d_l$
Loss gradient w.r.t. input$\nabla_x \ell$$d_{\text{input}}$

White-box signal (concatenation):

$$\phi_{\text{WB}}(z) = \left[\ell, \; \|\nabla_{W_1} \ell\|, \ldots, \|\nabla_{W_L} \ell\|, \; h_1, \ldots, h_L\right]$$
Sablayrolles et al. 2019

Key Theorem: Black-Box ≈ White-Box

Theorem (Sablayrolles et al. 2019)

Asymptotic regime (large models, standard training): LR on loss alone converges to LR on all white-box features:

$$\Lambda_{\text{BB}}(z) \;\xrightarrow{\text{large models}}\; \Lambda_{\text{WB}}(z)$$

Implication: loss is near-sufficient.

Gradients, activations: diminishing returns.

Sablayrolles et al. 2019

Why the Loss Is (Nearly) Sufficient

  • Loss $\ell(f_\theta, z)$ compresses how the model treats $z$
  • Gradients $\nabla_\theta \ell$: determined by loss landscape; no independent membership info
  • Activations $h_l(x)$: summarized by the loss

Intuition: loss answers "how well does the model fit $z$?" That is the MIA question.

Caveat: asymptotic.

Small models, specific architectures: white-box gives modest gain.

Sablayrolles et al. 2019

Practical Implications

For Deployers

Restricting API: negligible gain. Label-only still leaks.

For Attackers

Skip white-box. Calibrate the loss instead.

Shifts MIA research focus: which signal → how to calibrate.

Core challenge of modern MIA.

Sablayrolles et al. 2019

Salem et al. 2019

ML-Leaks: Relaxing All Assumptions

Tested systematically: how much does the attacker need to know?

Progressively removed Shokri's assumptions.

Attack still effective.

Salem et al. 2019

  • Relaxation 3: no shadows; threshold on max confidence

Relaxation 1: Different Architecture

Any shadow architecture, regardless of target:

Example

Target: ResNet-50

Shadow: 3-layer MLP

Why It Works

Confidence gap is architecture-agnostic.

Result: ~1-2% drop vs matched architecture.

Salem et al. 2019

Relaxation 2: Different Data Distribution

Train shadows on unrelated data:

Example

Target: CIFAR-10

Shadows: MNIST

Why It Works

Trained vs untrained confidence pattern is universal.

Result: ~2-4% drop vs same-distribution shadows.

Salem et al. 2019

Relaxation 3: No Shadow Models

Extreme case: no shadow models.

$$\mathcal{A}(f_\theta, x) = \mathbf{1}\left[\max_c f_\theta(x)_c > \tau\right]$$

Three ways to set $\tau$:

Fixed

Constant $\tau$.

Percentile

$\tau$ at a percentile of reference confidences.

Entropy

Threshold on entropy $H(f_\theta(x))$.

Salem et al. 2019

Salem et al. 2019

ML-Leaks: Takeaway

Adversary needs far less than assumed.

Shadows help, but aren't necessary.

Membership signal is a property of overfitting.

Defense implication: do not assume a limited attacker.

Even a predicted label enables MIA.
