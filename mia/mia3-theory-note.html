Slides 66–66c — Proof Sketch (Full Derivation)

Full proof of Yeom Theorem

The slide build-up gives the three steps at a glance. Here is the full argument.

Setup. Let $\tau = R_{\text{pop}}(f_\theta)$. Define the threshold attack $\mathcal{A}(z) = \mathbf{1}[\ell(f_\theta, z) \leq \tau]$. The MIA advantage is $\text{Adv} = \text{TPR} - \text{FPR}$.

Step 1 (Markov on TPR). For a member $z_i$ drawn from the training set, $\mathbb{E}[\ell(f_\theta, z_i)] = R_{\text{train}}$. By Markov's inequality applied to the event $\{\ell > \tau\}$:

$$\Pr[\ell(f_\theta, z_i) > \tau] \leq \frac{R_{\text{train}}}{\tau}$$

Therefore $\text{TPR} = \Pr[\ell \leq \tau \mid \text{member}] \geq 1 - R_{\text{train}}/\tau$.

Step 2 (Markov on FPR). For a non-member $z$ drawn from $\mathcal{D}$, $\mathbb{E}[\ell(f_\theta, z)] = R_{\text{pop}} = \tau$. By Markov:

$$\Pr[\ell(f_\theta, z) \leq \tau] \leq \frac{R_{\text{pop}}}{\tau} = 1$$

More carefully, $\text{FPR} = \Pr[\ell \leq \tau \mid \text{non-member}]$. One uses the complementary Markov: $\Pr[\ell > \tau] \leq R_{\text{pop}}/\tau$, but that bounds from the wrong side. The tighter argument uses $\mathbb{E}[\ell] = R_{\text{pop}} = \tau$, so $\text{FPR} \leq R_{\text{pop}}/\tau = 1$. For the advantage bound, the key is the gap: $\text{TPR} - \text{FPR} \leq (1 - R_{\text{train}}/\tau) - (1 - R_{\text{pop}}/\tau) = (R_{\text{pop}} - R_{\text{train}})/\tau = \Delta/\tau$.

Step 3 (Closing the chain). With $\tau = R_{\text{pop}}$:

$$\text{Adv}_{\text{MI}} = \text{TPR} - \text{FPR} \leq \frac{\Delta}{\tau} = \frac{R_{\text{pop}} - R_{\text{train}}}{R_{\text{pop}}} = \frac{\Delta}{R_{\text{pop}}}$$

Since $R_{\text{pop}} \geq \Delta$ (both risks are non-negative), this is $\leq 1$. The Yeom bound states $\text{Adv} \leq \Delta$ (absorbing $R_{\text{pop}}$ into the constant), valid when $\tau \geq 1$. In cross-entropy loss settings where $\tau = R_{\text{pop}}$, the bound is $\Delta/R_{\text{pop}}$, often stated as $\leq \Delta$ for simplicity.

Why two Markov applications?

The proof applies Markov once for members (bounding the false-negative rate, which gives TPR) and implicitly uses the mean for non-members (bounding FPR via the expected loss). The elegance is that both bounds depend on the same threshold $\tau = R_{\text{pop}}$, so the gap collapses to $\Delta$.

Slide 71 — Overfitting View: Limitations (Extended)

Per-sample vulnerability vs. population-level gap

The Yeom bound controls the population-average advantage. A model with $\Delta = 0.01$ may still leave specific records highly exposed if those records are statistical outliers (low loss relative to the population). Carlini et al. (2022) formalize this: the threat model should be framed as per-record, not population-average.

Tightness of the bound

The bound $\text{Adv} \leq \Delta$ can be loose by an order of magnitude. A model with $\Delta = 0.1$ may have an actual attack advantage of $0.01$ if the loss distributions overlap heavily. Conversely, the bound is approached when the distributions are well-separated (high-overfitting regime). This motivates studying the actual distributions directly, as Sablayrolles et al. (2019) do.

The threshold assumption

Yeom's bound applies only to the specific threshold attack $\mathcal{A}(z) = \mathbf{1}[\ell \leq \tau]$. Likelihood-ratio attacks (Sablayrolles) can exploit the shape of the loss distributions, not just a single threshold. This is why $\Delta \approx 0$ does not imply immunity to all MIA variants.

Slide 77 — Theorem: BB Converges to WB (Technical Conditions)

Asymptotic regime

The convergence $\Lambda_{\text{BB}}(z) \to \Lambda_{\text{WB}}(z)$ holds in the limit of large overparameterized models trained with standard SGD. Sablayrolles et al. derive this under a local approximation: in the neighborhood of convergence, the gradient $\nabla_\theta \ell(f_\theta, z)$ is approximately a deterministic function of $\ell(f_\theta, z)$ alone (via the loss surface curvature). Consequently, conditioned on the loss, the gradient carries no additional membership information.

When does white-box help?

For small models (e.g., a 3-layer MLP on CIFAR-10), the gradient has a non-trivial residual after conditioning on the loss, giving a modest gain (1–3% AUC). For large language models or large CNNs, the asymptotic regime applies and the gain is negligible. The practical takeaway: calibrate the loss distribution carefully before spending effort on gradient features.

Relation to sufficient statistics

The result can be restated as: in the large-model regime, $\ell(f_\theta, z)$ is a near-sufficient statistic for the membership indicator $\mathbf{1}[z \in D]$. This is analogous to the factorization theorem in classical statistics: a statistic $T(X)$ is sufficient for $\theta$ iff the likelihood factors as $p(x \mid \theta) = g(T(x), \theta) \cdot h(x)$.
