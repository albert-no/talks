Defenses: A Brief Recap

Regularization, DP-SGD, and the privacy-utility tradeoff

Defense Taxonomy

Regularization

L2, dropout, early stopping, data augmentation

Reduces generalization gap → Yeom bound

Output Perturbation

MemGuard, label smoothing, temperature scaling

Modify outputs to mask membership signal

Training-Time

DP-SGD, mixup, adversarial regularization

Modify training to reduce memorization

Architecture Design

Knowledge distillation, PATE

Structural approaches to limit leakage

DP-SGD: The Formal Defense (Brief Reminder)

The only defense with formal guarantees against any adversary:

$$\tilde{g}_t = \frac{1}{B}\left(\sum_i \text{clip}(g_i, C) + \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2 C^2 I)\right)$$

Strengths

Bounds MI advantage via $(\varepsilon, \delta)$. Composable. Auditable.

Cost

Significant accuracy drop at small $\varepsilon$. Computational overhead.

The Arms Race

Every empirical defense is eventually broken by adaptive attacks:

Lesson: Post-hoc output perturbation is an arms race. Only training-time defenses (DP-SGD) modify the fundamental information content.

02

Why Early Evaluations Misled Us

Average accuracy hides per-sample risk

The Problem with Early Evaluations

Prior work reported MIA results using balanced accuracy at a 50% threshold:

  • Inflated by easy examples that any attack can classify
  • Hides performance on hard (but important) cases
  • Not meaningful at low FPR — the practical regime
  • Doesn't capture per-sample heterogeneity

The modern era of MIA begins with a re-evaluation of what it means for an attack to succeed — driven by Carlini et al.'s insistence on TPR at low FPR.

Choquette-Choo et al. 2020

Label-Only Membership Inference

What if the API only returns the predicted label — no confidence scores?

Challenge: A binary signal (correct/incorrect label) seems too weak for MIA. But it turns out to be sufficient.

Data Augmentation Attack

Query with augmented versions of $x$. Count how often the label stays correct:

$$s(x) = \frac{1}{K}\sum_{k=1}^K \mathbf{1}[f_\theta(T_k(x)) = y]$$

Members are more robust to augmentation.

Boundary Attack

Estimate distance to decision boundary:

$$d(x) = \min_\delta \|\delta\| \text{ s.t. } f_\theta(x+\delta) \neq y$$

Members are further from the boundary.

Choquette-Choo et al. 2020

Label-Only: Surprisingly Effective

  • Performance surprisingly close to confidence-based attacks
  • Data augmentation attack requires only $K \sim 100$ queries per point
  • Boundary attack is more expensive but more precise

Even the minimal signal of a predicted label leaks membership.

This confirms Sablayrolles' theoretical result: restricting API access provides minimal privacy benefit. The membership signal is fundamental to how the model treats data.

Carlini et al. 2022

Membership Inference Attacks From First Principles

A landmark paper that redefined both the attack methodology and evaluation standards for MIA.

  • Introduced LiRA (Likelihood Ratio Attack)
  • Per-example calibration via shadow models
  • Established TPR at low FPR as the evaluation standard
  • Dramatically outperforms all prior attacks

"Membership Inference Attacks From First Principles"

IEEE S&P 2022

Carlini, Chien, Nasr, Song, Terzis, Tramèr

Carlini et al. 2022

LiRA: The Likelihood Ratio Attack

Core idea: compute the likelihood ratio of the loss under two hypotheses:

$$\Lambda(z) = \frac{p(\ell(f_\theta, z) \mid z \in D)}{p(\ell(f_\theta, z) \mid z \notin D)}$$

This is exactly the Neyman-Pearson optimal test (Slide 26), instantiated with the loss as the test statistic (justified by Sablayrolles).

Key question: How do we estimate $p(\ell \mid \text{IN})$ and $p(\ell \mid \text{OUT})$ for a specific data point $z$?

Carlini et al. 2022

Estimating the Distributions

Train $N$ shadow models with controlled membership of $z$:

IN Models ($N/2$)

Include $z$ in training data

Collect losses: $\{\ell_{\text{in}}^{(1)}, \ldots, \ell_{\text{in}}^{(N/2)}\}$

OUT Models ($N/2$)

Exclude $z$ from training data

Collect losses: $\{\ell_{\text{out}}^{(1)}, \ldots, \ell_{\text{out}}^{(N/2)}\}$

Fit Gaussian distributions to each set:

$$\ell_{\text{in}} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_{\text{in}}, \sigma_{\text{in}}^2) \qquad \ell_{\text{out}} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_{\text{out}}, \sigma_{\text{out}}^2)$$
Carlini et al. 2022

LiRA: Mathematical Formulation

The likelihood ratio becomes a ratio of Gaussian PDFs:

$$\Lambda(z) = \frac{\phi(\ell; \mu_{\text{in}}, \sigma_{\text{in}})}{\phi(\ell; \mu_{\text{out}}, \sigma_{\text{out}})}$$

where $\phi(\ell; \mu, \sigma) = \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}} \exp\left(-\frac{(\ell - \mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right)$ and $\ell = \ell(f_\theta, z)$ is the target model's loss.

Taking the log:

$$\log \Lambda(z) = \frac{(\ell - \mu_{\text{out}})^2}{2\sigma_{\text{out}}^2} - \frac{(\ell - \mu_{\text{in}})^2}{2\sigma_{\text{in}}^2} + \log\frac{\sigma_{\text{out}}}{\sigma_{\text{in}}}$$
Carlini et al. 2022

Per-Example Calibration

Key innovation: The distributions are computed per example $z$. Each data point gets its own $(\mu_{\text{in}}, \sigma_{\text{in}}, \mu_{\text{out}}, \sigma_{\text{out}})$.

Why this matters:

  • An "easy" example has low loss regardless of membership → needs tight thresholds
  • A "hard" example has high loss even as a member → needs different thresholds
  • A global threshold (Yeom) treats all examples the same — suboptimal
  • Per-example calibration captures the difficulty of each data point

Analogy: "Not all questions on an exam are equally hard — you need per-question grading."

Carlini et al. 2022

Online vs. Offline LiRA

Online LiRA

For each target $z$: train $N/2$ models with $z$ and $N/2$ without.

Pro: Exact per-example distributions

Con: $O(N \cdot |D|)$ total models needed

Offline LiRA

Train $N$ models on random subsets. Classify membership of $z$ post-hoc based on whether $z$ happened to be included.

Pro: Only $N$ models total

Con: Noisier estimates

In practice, offline LiRA with $N = 64$–$256$ models is standard.

Carlini et al. 2022

Computational Cost

$$\text{Total cost} = N \times \text{(cost of training one model)}$$

For $N = 256$ shadow models on a CIFAR-10 classifier:

  • ~256× the compute of training one model
  • Each shadow model must be fully trained to convergence
  • All models train on overlapping but different subsets

The price of optimality: LiRA is the most accurate attack but also the most expensive. Later work (RMIA) will dramatically reduce this cost.

Carlini et al. 2022

LiRA dramatically outperforms all prior attacks in the low-FPR regime:

Carlini et al. 2022

The Evaluation Revolution

Carlini's argument: "An attack that achieves 60% balanced accuracy but 0% TPR at 0.1% FPR is useless in practice."

New evaluation standards established by this paper:

  • Always report TPR at low FPR (0.1%, 0.01%)
  • Plot ROC on log-log scale to reveal low-FPR behavior
  • Use per-example calibration — global thresholds are suboptimal
  • Report on vulnerable subgroups, not just average
Carlini et al. 2022

Fixed vs. Per-Example Thresholds

Old: Global Threshold

Single $\tau$ for all examples.

$$\mathcal{A}(z) = \mathbf{1}[\ell(f_\theta, z) \leq \tau]$$

Treats all data points equally. Misses easy examples and over-alerts on hard ones.

New: Per-Example LR

Calibrated threshold per $z$.

$$\mathcal{A}(z) = \mathbf{1}\left[\frac{\phi(\ell; \mu_z^{\text{in}}, \sigma_z^{\text{in}})}{\phi(\ell; \mu_z^{\text{out}}, \sigma_z^{\text{out}})} > \tau\right]$$

Accounts for each point's difficulty. Dramatically better at low FPR.

Carlini et al. 2022

LiRA: Pseudocode (Offline)

// Phase 1: Train shadow models on random subsets for i = 1 to N: S[i] ← random_subset(D_pool, size=n) f[i] ← train_model(S[i]) // Phase 2: For each target point z, compute LiRA score def lira_score(target_model, z): losses_in ← [loss(f[i], z) for i where z ∈ S[i]] losses_out ← [loss(f[i], z) for i where z ∉ S[i]] μ_in, σ_in ← fit_gaussian(losses_in) μ_out, σ_out ← fit_gaussian(losses_out) ℓ ← loss(target_model, z) return log_ratio(ℓ, μ_in, σ_in, μ_out, σ_out)

Population vs. Reference Attacks

Population Attack

Compare loss against population distribution $p(\ell \mid z \sim \mathcal{D})$:

$$\Lambda(z) = \frac{p(\ell(f_\theta, z) \mid z \in D)}{p(\ell \mid z \sim \mathcal{D})}$$

Cheap but requires population data.

Reference Attack

Compare against reference models (like LiRA):

$$\Lambda(z) = \frac{p(\ell \mid z \in D_{\text{ref}})}{p(\ell \mid z \notin D_{\text{ref}})}$$

More expensive, more powerful.

RMIA: Zarifzadeh et al. 2023

Reference-free relative comparison

Zarifzadeh et al. 2023

RMIA: Low-Cost High-Power MIA

Achieves LiRA-level performance with dramatically fewer reference models.

  • As few as 1–8 reference models (vs. 256 for LiRA)
  • Uses population data for additional calibration
  • 10–100× computational reduction

"Low-Cost High-Power Membership Inference Attacks"

ICML 2024 — Zarifzadeh, Liu, Shokri

Zarifzadeh et al. 2023

RMIA: Core Idea

Instead of per-example shadow models, use a small set of reference models + population data:

$$\Lambda(z) = \frac{p_\theta(z)}{\frac{1}{R}\sum_{r=1}^R p_{\theta_r}(z)}$$

Numerator: target model's likelihood of $z$. Denominator: average likelihood across $R$ reference models.

If the target model assigns much higher likelihood to $z$ than reference models do, $z$ was likely in the target's training data.

Zarifzadeh et al. 2023

RMIA: Relative Comparison

Alternative formulation — rank the target's loss on $z$ among reference points:

$$s(z) = \frac{1}{|Z_{\text{ref}}|}\sum_{z' \in Z_{\text{ref}}} \mathbf{1}\left[\frac{p_\theta(z)}{p_{\theta_r}(z)} > \frac{p_\theta(z')}{p_{\theta_r}(z')}\right]$$

"How does $z$'s likelihood ratio rank among reference data points?"

  • Members have higher relative ranking than non-members
  • The ranking is more robust than absolute loss values
  • Resilient to distribution shift between reference and target
Zarifzadeh et al. 2023

Zarifzadeh et al. 2023

RMIA: Why It Works

  • Reference points provide calibration. Instead of per-example shadow models, use population data to establish what "normal" looks like.
  • Relative ranking is stable. Comparing ratios is more robust than comparing absolute losses.
  • Population data is cheap. You don't need to train models on specific subsets — any data from the domain works.

RMIA represents the current best efficiency-accuracy tradeoff for MIA.

Beyond Classification

Diffusion models and the LR paradigm

Matsumoto et al. 2023

MIA Against Diffusion Models

Extending MIA to diffusion models — a generative architecture with unique challenges:

Challenge

Diffusion models have $T$ timesteps. No single forward pass — the denoising process is iterative.

Approach

Use reconstruction loss at specific timesteps as the membership signal.

$$\ell_t = \|\epsilon - \epsilon_\theta(x_t, t)\|^2 \qquad \bar{\ell} = \frac{1}{T}\sum_{t=1}^T \ell_t$$
Matsumoto et al. 2023

Timestep Analysis

Not all timesteps are equally informative for MIA:

Early Timesteps (low noise)

Small perturbation from original image. Model's prediction is close to the original. Most informative for MIA.

Late Timesteps (high noise)

Image is nearly pure noise. Model can't distinguish members from non-members. Least informative.

Optimal strategy: weight timesteps by informativeness — focus on early timesteps where the model "remembers" training details.

Matsumoto et al. 2023

Diffusion MIA: Results & Connection

  • Diffusion models memorize more than expected, especially at high capacity
  • Smaller training sets → higher vulnerability (same as classification)
  • The loss-based paradigm transfers from classification to generation

Connection to LLMs: Both diffusion models and LLMs are likelihood-based generative models. Both process structured data. The MIA paradigm — compare losses against a calibrated baseline — applies to both.

Open Questions Before the LLM Era

  • How does MIA change for pre-trained models (seen once among trillions of tokens)?
  • Does fine-tuning create new vulnerabilities?
  • How to handle text data and autoregressive generation?
  • Can we attack without any reference models at all?
  • How to calibrate when "text difficulty" varies enormously?

All of these questions are addressed by LLM MIA research in Section VI.
