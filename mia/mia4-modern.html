Composable.

Why Early Evaluations Misled Us

Average accuracy hides per-sample risk

The Problem with Early Evaluations

Prior work: balanced accuracy at 50% threshold:

  • Inflated by easy examples
  • Hides hard (important) cases
  • Meaningless at low FPR (the practical regime)
  • Misses per-sample heterogeneity

Modern MIA begins with re-evaluation: Carlini's call for TPR at low FPR.

Choquette-Choo et al. 2020

Label-Only Membership Inference

API returns only the predicted label, no scores?

Challenge: binary signal seems too weak.

Turns out sufficient.

Choquette-Choo et al. 2020

Label-Only: Two Attack Forms

Data Augmentation

Query augmented $x$.

Count correct labels:

$$s(x) = \frac{1}{K}\sum_{k=1}^K \mathbf{1}[f_\theta(T_k(x)) = y]$$

Members: more robust.

Boundary

Distance to decision boundary:

$$d(x) = \min_\delta \|\delta\| \text{ s.t. } f_\theta(x+\delta) \neq y$$

Members: farther from boundary.

Choquette-Choo et al. 2020

Label-Only: Surprisingly Effective

  • Surprisingly close to confidence-based attacks
  • Data aug: only $K \sim 100$ queries per point
  • Boundary: costlier, more precise

Even predicted labels leak membership.

Confirms Sablayrolles: API restriction gives little privacy.

Membership signal is intrinsic.

LiRA: The Likelihood Ratio Attack

Core: likelihood ratio of loss under two hypotheses:

$$\Lambda(z) = \frac{p(\ell(f_\theta, z) \mid z \in D)}{p(\ell(f_\theta, z) \mid z \notin D)}$$

Neyman-Pearson optimal test (Slide 26) with loss as statistic (Sablayrolles).

Key question: estimate $p(\ell \mid \text{IN})$ and $p(\ell \mid \text{OUT})$ for $z$?

Carlini et al. 2022

Estimating the Distributions

Train $N$ shadow models with controlled $z$ membership:

IN Models ($N/2$)

Include $z$ in training.

Losses $\{\ell_{\text{in}}^{(i)}\}$

OUT Models ($N/2$)

Exclude $z$ from training.

Losses $\{\ell_{\text{out}}^{(i)}\}$

Fit Gaussians to each set:

$$\ell_{\text{in}} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_{\text{in}}, \sigma_{\text{in}}^2) \qquad \ell_{\text{out}} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_{\text{out}}, \sigma_{\text{out}}^2)$$
Carlini et al. 2022

LiRA: Mathematical Formulation

Ratio of Gaussian PDFs:

$$\Lambda(z) = \frac{\phi(\ell; \mu_{\text{in}}, \sigma_{\text{in}})}{\phi(\ell; \mu_{\text{out}}, \sigma_{\text{out}})}$$

with $\phi(\ell; \mu, \sigma) = \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}} \exp\left(-\frac{(\ell - \mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right)$, $\ell = \ell(f_\theta, z)$ target loss.

Log:

$$\log \Lambda(z) = \frac{(\ell - \mu_{\text{out}})^2}{2\sigma_{\text{out}}^2} - \frac{(\ell - \mu_{\text{in}})^2}{2\sigma_{\text{in}}^2} + \log\frac{\sigma_{\text{out}}}{\sigma_{\text{in}}}$$
Carlini et al. 2022

Per-Example Calibration

Key innovation: distributions are computed per example $z$.

Each point has its own $(\mu_{\text{in}}, \sigma_{\text{in}}, \mu_{\text{out}}, \sigma_{\text{out}})$.

Why it matters:

  • "Easy" example: low loss either way, needs tight thresholds
  • "Hard" example: high loss as member, needs different thresholds
  • Global threshold (Yeom): one-size-fits-all, suboptimal
  • Per-example calibration captures point difficulty

Analogy: exam questions vary in difficulty, use per-question grading.

Carlini et al. 2022

Online vs. Offline LiRA

Online

Per $z$: $N/2$ with, $N/2$ without.

Pro: exact distributions

Con: $O(N \cdot |D|)$ models

Offline

$N$ random subsets, split post-hoc.

Pro: only $N$ models

Con: noisier estimates

Standard: offline with $N = 64$ to $256$.

Carlini et al. 2022

Computational Cost

$$\text{Total cost} = N \times \text{(cost of training one model)}$$

$N = 256$ shadow models on CIFAR-10:

  • ~256× compute of one model
  • Each shadow fully trained to convergence
  • Overlapping but distinct subsets

Price of optimality: most accurate, most expensive.

RMIA will cut cost.

Carlini et al. 2022

Carlini et al. 2022

The Evaluation Revolution

Carlini's argument: 60% balanced accuracy but 0% TPR at 0.1% FPR is useless in practice.

New evaluation standards:

  • Report TPR at low FPR (0.1%, 0.01%)
  • Log-log ROC reveals low-FPR behavior
  • Per-example calibration: global thresholds suboptimal
  • Report vulnerable subgroups, not only averages
Carlini et al. 2022

Fixed vs. Per-Example Thresholds

Old: Global Threshold

One $\tau$ for all.

$$\mathcal{A}(z) = \mathbf{1}[\ell(f_\theta, z) \leq \tau]$$

Misses easy points, over-alerts on hard ones.

New: Per-Example LR

Calibrated threshold per $z$.

$$\mathcal{A}(z) = \mathbf{1}\!\left[\frac{\phi(\ell; \mu_z^{\text{in}}, \sigma_z^{\text{in}})}{\phi(\ell; \mu_z^{\text{out}}, \sigma_z^{\text{out}})} > \tau\right]$$

Accounts for point difficulty: much better at low FPR.

Carlini et al. 2022

LiRA: Pseudocode (Offline)

// Phase 1: Train shadow models on random subsets for i = 1 to N: S[i] ← random_subset(D_pool, size=n) f[i] ← train_model(S[i]) // Phase 2: For each target point z, compute LiRA score def lira_score(target_model, z): losses_in ← [loss(f[i], z) for i where z ∈ S[i]] losses_out ← [loss(f[i], z) for i where z ∉ S[i]] μ_in, σ_in ← fit_gaussian(losses_in) μ_out, σ_out ← fit_gaussian(losses_out) ℓ ← loss(target_model, z) return log_ratio(ℓ, μ_in, σ_in, μ_out, σ_out)

Costlier; more powerful.

RMIA: Zarifzadeh et al. 2023

Reference-free relative comparison

Zarifzadeh et al. 2023

RMIA: Low-Cost High-Power MIA

LiRA-level performance, far fewer reference models.

  • 1 to 8 reference models (vs 256 for LiRA)
  • Population data for calibration
  • 10 to 100× compute reduction
Zarifzadeh, Liu, Shokri, "Low-Cost High-Power Membership Inference Attacks", ICML 2024
Zarifzadeh et al. 2023

RMIA: Core Idea

Not per-example shadows: small reference set + population data:

$$\Lambda(z) = \frac{p_\theta(z)}{\frac{1}{R}\sum_{r=1}^R p_{\theta_r}(z)}$$

Numerator: target likelihood of $z$. Denominator: avg over $R$ references.

Target $\gg$ references on $z$: likely trained on $z$.

Zarifzadeh et al. 2023

RMIA: Relative Comparison

Alternative: rank target's $z$ loss among reference points:

$$s(z) = \frac{1}{|Z_{\text{ref}}|}\sum_{z' \in Z_{\text{ref}}} \mathbf{1}\left[\frac{p_\theta(z)}{p_{\theta_r}(z)} > \frac{p_\theta(z')}{p_{\theta_r}(z')}\right]$$

Rank of $z$'s likelihood ratio vs reference points.

  • Members rank higher than non-members
  • Ranking more robust than absolute loss
  • Survives reference/target distribution shift
Zarifzadeh et al. 2023

RMIA: Why It Works

  • Reference points calibrate. Population data defines "normal" without per-example shadows.
  • Relative ranking is stable. Ratios beat absolute losses.
  • Population data is cheap. Any domain data works, no special subsets.

RMIA: current best efficiency-accuracy tradeoff.

Beyond Classification

Diffusion models, the LR paradigm

Matsumoto et al. 2023

MIA Against Diffusion Models

MIA on diffusion models, a generative architecture with unique challenges:

Challenge

$T$ timesteps; denoising is iterative.

Approach

Reconstruction loss at chosen timesteps.

$$\ell_t = \|\epsilon - \epsilon_\theta(x_t, t)\|^2 \qquad \bar{\ell} = \frac{1}{T}\sum_{t=1}^T \ell_t$$
Matsumoto et al. 2023

Timestep Analysis

Timesteps, not equally informative:

Early (low noise)

Prediction close to original. Most informative.

Late (high noise)

Nearly pure noise. Least informative.

Weight by informativeness: focus on early timesteps.

Matsumoto et al. 2023

Diffusion MIA: Results & Connection

  • Diffusion models memorize more than expected at high capacity
  • Smaller training sets, higher vulnerability (as in classification)
  • Loss-based paradigm transfers: classification to generation

LLM connection: same paradigm.

Hands-On: Threshold and LiRA

// Threshold Attack (simplest) def threshold_attack(model, z, τ): return loss(model, z) < τ // LiRA (most powerful) def lira_attack(target, z, shadows): ℓ_in ← [loss(s, z) for s in shadows if z ∈ s.train] ℓ_out ← [loss(s, z) for s in shadows if z ∉ s.train] μ_in, σ_in ← gaussian_fit(ℓ_in) μ_out, σ_out ← gaussian_fit(ℓ_out) return log_likelihood_ratio(loss(target, z), μ_in, σ_in, μ_out, σ_out)

Simple vs. optimal: one threshold vs. per-example calibration with shadow models.

Hands-On: RMIA

// RMIA (best cost-accuracy tradeoff) def rmia_attack(target, z, ref_models, ref_data): ratio_z ← mean([loss(target,z) / loss(r,z) for r in ref_models]) ratio_zp ← [mean([loss(target,z') / loss(r,z') ...]) for z' in ref_data] return percentile_rank(ratio_z, ratio_zp)

Near-LiRA power at 30 to 100× lower cost (2 to 8 reference models).
