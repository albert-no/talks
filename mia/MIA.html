The Central Question

Given a mechanism $M$ trained on dataset $D$, and a data point $x$:

Was $x$ used to produce $M$'s output?

This question — membership inference — is the most fundamental privacy attack against machine learning systems.

It predates deep learning, applies across domains (genomics, statistics, ML), and serves as the foundation for stronger attacks like data extraction and model inversion.

Why Should You Care?

Legal & Regulatory

GDPR grants the right to know if your data was used. MIA provides an empirical test for this.

Privacy Auditing

MIA gives empirical lower bounds on privacy leakage — if MIA succeeds, the model is not private.

Gateway Attack

MIA is the foundation for stronger attacks: data extraction, attribute inference, and model inversion.

Membership inference is to ML privacy what IND-CPA is to cryptographic security — the minimal attack that any private system must resist.

Foundations — From Genomic Data to ML Privacy

Homer et al. 2008

Identifying Individuals in Aggregate Genomic Data

Homer et al. demonstrated that individuals can be identified from aggregate allele frequency statistics, even in complex mixtures of 40+ people.

  • Input: a person's genotype + published allele frequencies from a study
  • Output: was this person in the study?
  • Detected individuals contributing as little as 0.1% of total DNA

"Resolving Individuals Contributing Trace Amounts of DNA to Highly Complex Mixtures Using High-Density SNP Genotyping Microarrays"

PLoS Genetics, 2008

This paper is the conceptual foundation for all membership inference work.

Homer et al. 2008

The Attack Setup

Three ingredients for the attack:

Individual Genotype

$Y_{i,j}$ = allele frequency of individual $i$ at SNP $j$

Known to the attacker (e.g., from a different study)

Mixture Statistics

$M_j$ = allele frequency at SNP $j$ in the study mixture

Published as part of the study's results

Population Reference

$\text{Pop}_j$ = allele frequency at SNP $j$ in the general population

Publicly available from reference databases

If the individual is in the mixture, their genotype should be closer to the mixture profile than to the general population.

Homer et al. 2008

The Distance Statistic

For each individual $i$ and each SNP $j$, compute:

$$D(Y_{i,j}) = |Y_{i,j} - M_j| - |Y_{i,j} - \text{Pop}_j|$$

If individual is a member:

  • $Y_{i,j}$ is close to $M_j$ (they contributed)
  • $|Y_{i,j} - M_j|$ is small
  • $D(Y_{i,j}) < 0$ on average

If individual is not a member:

  • $Y_{i,j}$ is close to $\text{Pop}_j$ (population baseline)
  • $|Y_{i,j} - \text{Pop}_j|$ is small
  • $D(Y_{i,j}) \approx 0$ on average
Homer et al. 2008

Aggregation: Weak Signals, Strong Test

Each individual SNP gives a tiny signal. Aggregate across ~500,000 SNPs using a one-sample $t$-test:

$$T(Y_i) = \frac{\overline{D}(Y_i) - 0}{s_D / \sqrt{n_{\text{SNP}}}}$$

Under $H_0$ (not a member): $\overline{D} \approx 0$. Under $H_1$ (member): $\overline{D} < 0$.

Key principle: Aggregating hundreds of thousands of weak, per-SNP signals yields $p$-values below $10^{-6}$. This is the same principle that makes MIA work in ML — aggregate many small per-parameter or per-prediction signals.

Homer et al. 2008

Key Results

  • Detected individuals contributing 0.1% of mixture DNA
  • Zero false positives across 3 microarray platforms
  • Works in mixtures of 40+ individuals
  • 10,000–50,000 SNPs suffice for $p < 10^{-6}$
  • Can even identify via parental genotypes as proxies

Real-World Impact: NIH Database Closure

In direct response to this paper, the NIH issued an emergency closure of publicly available Genome-Wide Association (GWA) study databases.

  • Summary statistics (allele frequencies, genotype counts) were withdrawn from public access

This was the first major policy change driven by a membership inference-style attack.

Recap: Reconstruction Attacks

You've already seen reconstruction attacks — the adversary tries to recover training data from the mechanism's output:

$$\text{Reconstruction:} \quad \mathcal{A}(M(D)) \to \hat{D} \approx D$$
  • Strong attack: recover actual training records
  • Natural threat model for aggregate queries (census data, histograms)
  • The Dinur-Nissim attack shows reconstruction is possible from $O(n)$ linear queries

Question: Is defeating reconstruction attacks sufficient for privacy?

Why Reconstruction Is Not Enough

Consider a mechanism that samples a random training record and outputs it:

$$M(D) = D_i \quad \text{where } i \sim \text{Uniform}(\{1, \ldots, n\})$$

Defeats Reconstruction

Outputting one random record reveals essentially nothing about the other $n-1$ records. The adversary cannot reconstruct $D$.

Fails Privacy

If the adversary sees their own record in the output, they know with certainty they were in $D$. Membership is leaked.

Defeating reconstruction ≠ preserving privacy. We need a stronger notion of security.

Cryptographic Analogy: IND-CPA

In cryptography, the security notion is not "can you recover the plaintext?" but rather:

IND-CPA: Can you distinguish between encryptions of two messages $m_0$ and $m_1$?

Plaintext Recovery

Adversary must recover the actual message. Too weak — the system can leak partial information and still pass.

Indistinguishability

Adversary cannot tell which of two messages was encrypted. Correct notion — nothing is leaked, even partial information.

The same shift applies to privacy: from reconstruction (recovery) to indistinguishability (distinguishing).

The Sampling Mechanism Failure

Consider two neighboring databases that differ in one record $x$:

$$D = \{x_1, \ldots, x_n\} \qquad D' = D \setminus \{x\}$$

The sampling mechanism $M(D) = D_i$ for random $i$:

  • $M(D)$ outputs $x$ with probability $\frac{1}{n}$
  • $M(D')$ outputs $x$ with probability $0$
  • These distributions are perfectly distinguishable — just check if the output is $x$

The right question is: can an adversary distinguish $M(D)$ from $M(D')$? This is exactly what differential privacy formalizes — and what membership inference attacks attempt.

Privacy = Indistinguishability → MIA

Reconstruction Attack

Recover training data from output

Too strong — defeating it doesn't guarantee privacy

Membership Inference Attack

Distinguish whether $x$ was in training data

Right notion — aligns with DP's indistinguishability

Key insight: MIA is the fundamental privacy attack. If a mechanism resists MIA, it provides meaningful privacy. If MIA succeeds, the mechanism leaks individual information.

The Membership Inference Game

Formalize MIA as a game between a challenger and an adversary:

  • Challenger holds database $D$ and a target point $x$
  • Challenger flips a coin: $b \xleftarrow{\$} \{0, 1\}$
  • If $b = 1$: run mechanism on $D_1 = D \cup \{x\}$. If $b = 0$: run on $D_0 = D \setminus \{x\}$
  • Adversary $\mathcal{A}$ observes $M(D_b)$ and outputs a guess $\hat{b}$
  • Adversary wins if $\hat{b} = b$

This is a binary hypothesis test: the adversary must distinguish between $M(D_0)$ and $M(D_1)$.

Neighboring Databases

The MI game operates on neighboring databases — databases that differ in the presence of a single individual:

$$D_1 = D \cup \{x\} \qquad D_0 = D \setminus \{x\}$$

This is the same notion of adjacency used in differential privacy:

$$(\varepsilon, \delta)\text{-DP:} \quad \Pr[M(D_1) \in S] \leq e^\varepsilon \Pr[M(D_0) \in S] + \delta \quad \forall S, \forall x$$

DP says the outputs must be statistically close regardless of which database was used. MIA tests whether this closeness holds in practice.

Adversary's Advantage

The adversary's advantage in the MI game is:

$$\text{Adv}_{\text{MI}}(\mathcal{A}) = \left|\Pr[\mathcal{A}(M(D_1)) = 1] - \Pr[\mathcal{A}(M(D_0)) = 1]\right|$$

Random guessing

$\text{Adv} = 0$

No information gained

Perfect attack

$\text{Adv} = 1$

Complete membership leakage

Typical successful attack

$\text{Adv} \in (0, 1)$

Partial information leaked

Equivalent to the total variation distance between $M(D_1)$ and $M(D_0)$ as seen by $\mathcal{A}$.

Connection to Hypothesis Testing

MIA is a binary hypothesis test:

$H_0$: Not a Member

$x \notin D$

Observed output $\sim M(D_0)$

$H_1$: Member

$x \in D$

Observed output $\sim M(D_1)$

The adversary observes a signal $s = g(M(D_b))$ — for example, the model's loss on $x$ — and must decide which hypothesis generated it.

Standard hypothesis testing framework:

  • Type I error (FPR): falsely declaring membership — $\Pr[\hat{b}=1 \mid b=0] = \alpha$
  • Type II error (FNR): missing a member — $\Pr[\hat{b}=0 \mid b=1] = \beta$
  • Power (TPR): correctly identifying a member — $1 - \beta$

Test Statistic

The adversary needs a test statistic — any function of the observable signal:

$$T(x) = g(M(D), x) \quad \text{e.g., } T(x) = \ell(f_\theta, x) \text{ (the model's loss on } x\text{)}$$

Common test statistics for ML models:

Neyman-Pearson Lemma

What is the most powerful test at any given significance level $\alpha$?

Neyman-Pearson Lemma: The most powerful test is the likelihood ratio test:

$$\Lambda(x) = \frac{p(T(x) \mid H_1)}{p(T(x) \mid H_0)} = \frac{p(T(x) \mid x \in D)}{p(T(x) \mid x \notin D)} \quad \gtrless \quad \tau$$
  • No test can achieve higher TPR at the same FPR
  • The optimal attack is always a likelihood ratio — this will recur throughout the entire tutorial
  • The practical challenge: we don't know $p(T \mid H_1)$ and $p(T \mid H_0)$ exactly

Error Types in MIA Context

Type I Error (False Positive)

Declaring $x \in D$ when $x \notin D$

Consequence: False accusation of data use

Rate: FPR = $\alpha$

Type II Error (False Negative)

Declaring $x \notin D$ when $x \in D$

Consequence: Missed privacy violation

Rate: FNR = $\beta$

In practice, low FPR matters most. A 1% FPR means 1 false accusation per 100 queries. For legal or auditing applications, we need TPR at FPR $\leq 0.1\%$.

This will become the central evaluation criterion in Carlini et al. 2022.

DP Implies Hardness of MI

If mechanism $M$ satisfies $(\varepsilon, \delta)$-differential privacy, then for any adversary $\mathcal{A}$:

$$\text{Adv}_{\text{MI}}(\mathcal{A}) \leq e^\varepsilon - 1 + \delta$$

Proof sketch: By DP, $\Pr[\mathcal{A}(M(D_1))=1] \leq e^\varepsilon \Pr[\mathcal{A}(M(D_0))=1] + \delta$. Rearranging gives the bound on the difference.

$\varepsilon$$\delta$Max MI Advantage
0.1$10^{-5}$$\leq 0.105$
1.0$10^{-5}$$\leq 1.718$
8.0$10^{-5}$$\leq 2980$ (vacuous)

For small $\varepsilon$, DP provides strong MI protection. For large $\varepsilon$, the bound becomes vacuous.

MI Attacks Yield DP Lower Bounds

The converse direction is equally important:

If an MI attack achieves advantage $\alpha$ against mechanism $M$, then $M$ is not $(\varepsilon, \delta)$-DP for any $(\varepsilon, \delta)$ satisfying $e^\varepsilon - 1 + \delta < \alpha$.

This makes MI attacks a privacy auditing tool:

  • Train a model with your pipeline
  • Run the best available MI attack
  • The attack's success gives an empirical lower bound on privacy leakage
  • If the bound exceeds your target $\varepsilon$, the pipeline is not private enough

We will revisit this in Section IV when discussing privacy auditing.

Evaluation Metrics: ROC & AUC

The ROC curve plots TPR vs FPR as the threshold varies:

  • AUC = 0.5: random guessing (no attack)
  • AUC = 1.0: perfect separation
  • AUC = 0.6: typical weak MIA

Problem: AUC averages over all thresholds, including the high-FPR regime where attacks are impractical.

Misleading Accuracy

A model with 55% balanced accuracy might seem "barely above random" — but could achieve:

  • 10% TPR at 0.1% FPR for some samples
  • 0% TPR at 0.1% FPR for others

The average hides the vulnerable subpopulation.

The Right Metric: TPR at Low FPR

Carlini et al. (2022): "An attack that achieves 60% balanced accuracy but 0% TPR at 0.1% FPR is useless in practice."

Shokri et al. 2017

Membership Inference Attacks Against ML Models

The first systematic black-box MIA against machine learning models.

  • Attacked commercial ML-as-a-service: Google Prediction API and Amazon ML
  • Black-box access: only observe prediction confidence vectors
  • Introduced the shadow model methodology

IEEE S&P 2017

Shokri et al. 2017

Key Insight

Train shadow models that mimic the target model's behavior. Since you control the shadow models' training data, you can create a labeled dataset of (prediction, member/non-member) pairs to train an attack classifier.

The shadow model approach transforms a difficult unsupervised problem (does the model behave differently on members?) into a standard supervised classification problem.

Detailed Pipeline

  • Shadow training data: Generate datasets $D_1^s, \ldots, D_k^s$ from a distribution similar to the target's training data
  • Train shadow models: Train $k$ models $f_{\theta_1}, \ldots, f_{\theta_k}$ on these datasets
  • Collect predictions: For each shadow model, query it on its training data (label: "in") and on held-out data (label: "out")
  • Build attack dataset: $\{(\sigma(f_{\theta_j}(x)), \text{in/out})\}$ for all shadow models and queries
  • Train attack model: Binary classifier $\mathcal{A}_c$ (one per class $c$) on the attack dataset
  • Inference: Query target model on $x$, feed confidence to $\mathcal{A}_c$ for class $c = f_\theta(x)$
Shokri et al. 2017

Step 1: Shadow Model Training

Train $k$ shadow models that approximate the target's behavior:

Requirements

  • Data from a similar distribution as the target's training data
  • Same or similar model architecture
  • Same training procedure (epochs, optimizer, etc.)

Why It Works

  • Shadow models exhibit similar overfitting patterns
  • The gap between member and non-member confidence is a generalizable signal
  • Attack model learns "what membership looks like" across models
Shokri et al. 2017

Generating Shadow Training Data

Three strategies when the target's exact training distribution is unknown:

Model-Based

Query the target model to synthesize data that it classifies confidently. Use hill-climbing on the input space.

Statistics-Based

Use marginal statistics (feature distributions) from public data to sample synthetic records.

Noisy Real Data

Use data from a related domain or add noise to publicly available data from the same distribution.

Later work (Salem et al. 2019) will show that even unrelated data can work — the signal is more robust than initially thought.

Shokri et al. 2017

Step 2: Collecting Attack Training Data

For each shadow model $f_{\theta_j}$:

Member Queries

Query on training data $x \in D_j^s$

Record: $(\sigma(f_{\theta_j}(x)), \text{label}=\texttt{IN})$

Non-Member Queries

Query on held-out data $x \notin D_j^s$

Record: $(\sigma(f_{\theta_j}(x)), \text{label}=\texttt{OUT})$

The resulting attack dataset contains labeled examples of how shadow models behave on members vs. non-members.

Key assumption: the target model exhibits the same behavioral difference between members and non-members as the shadow models do.

Shokri et al. 2017

Step 3: Per-Class Attack Models

Train a separate binary classifier for each class $c$:

$$\mathcal{A}_c : \mathbb{R}^{|\mathcal{C}|} \to \{0, 1\}$$

Input: confidence vector $\sigma(f_\theta(x))$ for points predicted as class $c$

Output: member (1) or non-member (0)

Why per-class? Different classes have different confidence distributions. A model may be very confident on class "cat" but uncertain on class "bird" — the attack model needs class-specific calibration.

Shokri et al. 2017

Attack Inference

Given a target point $(x, y)$ and the target model $f_\theta$:

  • Query target: $\mathbf{p} = \sigma(f_\theta(x))$
  • Identify predicted class: $c = \arg\max_i \mathbf{p}_i$
  • Apply attack model: $\hat{m} = \mathcal{A}_c(\mathbf{p})$
  • If $\hat{m} = 1$: declare $x$ as a member

The attack classifier has learned to recognize the subtle differences in confidence patterns between members and non-members for each class.

Shokri et al. 2017

Shokri et al. 2017

Why More Classes = More Vulnerable

The output is a point on the probability simplex $\Delta^{C-1}$:

$$\sigma(f_\theta(x)) \in \Delta^{C-1} = \left\{\mathbf{p} \in \mathbb{R}^C : p_i \geq 0, \sum_i p_i = 1\right\}$$

Few classes (C=2)

Output is a single scalar (probability of class 1). Limited signal for the attacker.

Many classes (C=100)

Output is a 99-dimensional vector. The distribution of probability mass across classes reveals much more about how the model treats $x$.

More dimensions → richer behavioral fingerprint → easier to distinguish members from non-members.

Shokri et al. 2017

Limitations of Shadow Models

  • Data distribution assumption: Need access to data from a similar distribution as the target's training data
  • Architecture knowledge: Best results when shadow model architecture matches the target
  • Computational cost: Training $k$ shadow models is expensive ($k \times$ the cost of one model)
  • Per-class classifiers: Need separate attack models for each class — doesn't scale easily
  • No theoretical justification: Works empirically but lacks formal optimality guarantees

Open question: Can we do as well with fewer assumptions? (Spoiler: Yes — Salem et al., Sablayrolles et al.)

Shokri et al. 2017

The Simplest Attack: Threshold on Confidence

Before the full shadow model pipeline, consider the simplest possible attack:

$$\mathcal{A}(f_\theta, x) = \mathbf{1}\left[\max_c f_\theta(x)_c > \tau\right]$$

If the model is very confident on $x$, it was probably a training member.

Advantages

  • No shadow models needed
  • No training data needed
  • Single threshold to tune
  • Computationally free

Limitations

  • Global threshold ignores class differences
  • Doesn't use the full confidence vector
  • Sensitive to threshold choice
  • Weaker for well-calibrated models

Surprisingly, this simple baseline is often competitive with the full shadow model approach (Salem et al. 2019).

Hayes et al. 2017

LOGAN: MIA Against Generative Models

Shokri attacked classification models. Hayes et al. extended MIA to generative adversarial networks (GANs).

Challenge: GANs don't output prediction vectors. There's no natural "confidence score" to threshold on. How do we define membership for a generative model?

  • A GAN memorizes training images if it generates outputs very similar to specific training examples
  • The discriminator score can serve as a membership signal
  • Alternatively, reconstruction error can indicate membership
Hayes et al. 2017

LOGAN: Attack Architecture

Discriminator-Based

Use the GAN's discriminator $D(x)$ as a membership signal:

$$\mathcal{A}(x) = \mathbf{1}[D(x) > \tau]$$

The discriminator was trained to distinguish real (training) data from fake — it implicitly encodes membership.

Reconstruction-Based

Find the closest generated image to $x$:

$$\mathcal{A}(x) = \mathbf{1}[\min_z \|x - G(z)\| < \tau]$$

If the GAN can closely reconstruct $x$, it likely memorized it.

Hayes et al. 2017

Shadow GAN Approach

For more sophisticated attacks, train shadow GANs:

  • Train $k$ shadow GANs on known datasets
  • Extract discriminator features for members and non-members of each shadow
  • Train a binary attack classifier on these features
  • Apply to the target GAN's discriminator output

Key insight: mode collapse increases vulnerability. When a GAN fails to cover the full data distribution, it memorizes specific examples more strongly.

Hayes et al. 2017

Hisamoto et al. 2019

MIA on Sequence-to-Sequence Models

Hisamoto et al. extended MIA to machine translation models — the bridge from vision to NLP.

Challenge

Seq2seq models don't output a fixed-size confidence vector. Instead, they generate variable-length sequences with per-token probabilities.

Solution

Use perplexity — the model's average surprise at the source-target pair — as the membership signal.

$$\text{PPL}(x, y) = \exp\left(-\frac{1}{T}\sum_{t=1}^T \log p_\theta(y_t \mid y_{<t}, x)\right)$$
Hisamoto et al. 2019

Perplexity as Membership Signal

Members (training pairs)

  • Model has seen this (source, target) pair
  • Assigns high probability to the correct translation

Privacy Risk in ML: Connection to Overfitting

First rigorous theoretical analysis connecting overfitting to privacy vulnerability.

  • Formal bound: MIA advantage $\leq$ generalization gap
  • Simple threshold attack with theoretical guarantees
  • Connects membership and attribute inference

"Privacy Risk in Machine Learning: Analyzing the Connection to Overfitting"

CSF 2018 — Yeom, Giacomelli, Fredrikson, Jha

Yeom et al. 2018

The Generalization Gap

Define the population risk and empirical risk:

$$R_{\text{pop}}(f_\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{z \sim \mathcal{D}}[\ell(f_\theta, z)] \qquad R_{\text{train}}(f_\theta) = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n \ell(f_\theta, z_i)$$

The generalization gap is:

$$\Delta = R_{\text{pop}}(f_\theta) - R_{\text{train}}(f_\theta)$$

$\Delta > 0$ means the model fits training data better than unseen data — it has overfit. This gap is exactly the signal that MIA exploits.

Yeom et al. 2018

The Threshold Attack

The simplest MIA with theoretical backing:

$$\mathcal{A}(z) = \mathbf{1}\left[\ell(f_\theta, z) \leq \tau\right]$$

where $\tau = R_{\text{pop}}(f_\theta)$ (the expected population loss).

  • Members have average loss $R_{\text{train}} = R_{\text{pop}} - \Delta < \tau$ → classified as members
  • Non-members have average loss $R_{\text{pop}} = \tau$ → classified as non-members (on average)

No shadow models, no neural network attack classifier — just a single threshold on the loss.

Yeom et al. 2018

Theorem: MIA Advantage ≤ Generalization Gap

Theorem (Yeom et al. 2018)

For the threshold attack with $\tau = R_{\text{pop}}(f_\theta)$:

$$\text{Adv}_{\text{MI}}(\mathcal{A}) \leq \Delta = R_{\text{pop}}(f_\theta) - R_{\text{train}}(f_\theta)$$

The membership inference advantage is upper-bounded by the generalization gap.

Corollary: A perfectly generalizing model ($\Delta = 0$) leaks zero membership information to the threshold attack.

Yeom et al. 2018

Proof Sketch

The advantage is $|\text{TPR} - \text{FPR}|$:

  • TPR = $\Pr[\ell(f_\theta, z) \leq \tau \mid z \in D_{\text{train}}]$ — probability that a member has loss below threshold
  • FPR = $\Pr[\ell(f_\theta, z) \leq \tau \mid z \sim \mathcal{D}]$ — probability that a random point has loss below threshold
  • By Markov's inequality, $\text{TPR} \geq 1 - R_{\text{train}}/\tau$ and $\text{FPR} \leq R_{\text{pop}}/\tau$
  • The gap: $\text{TPR} - \text{FPR} \leq (R_{\text{pop}} - R_{\text{train}})/\tau = \Delta/\tau$

The key step: training data has systematically lower loss than unseen data, and the gap is exactly $\Delta$.

Yeom et al. 2018

Connection to Attribute Inference

Yeom et al. also connect membership inference to attribute inference:

$$\text{Attribute Inference:} \quad \mathcal{A}(f_\theta, x_{\setminus s}) \to s$$

Given all features except the sensitive attribute $s$, infer $s$ from the model's behavior.

  • If a model overfits on sensitive attributes, its predictions are more accurate for training members
  • Attribute inference advantage is also bounded by the generalization gap
  • MIA is a prerequisite: you need to know someone is a member before attribute inference is meaningful
Yeom et al. 2018

Implications

Regularization Helps Privacy

Anything that reduces the generalization gap (L2, dropout, early stopping, data augmentation) also reduces MIA vulnerability.

No Overfitting → No MIA

If $\Delta = 0$, the threshold attack has zero advantage. Perfect generalization implies the model treats members and non-members identically.

Privacy-Utility Tension

Some overfitting may be necessary for good performance. Reducing $\Delta$ to zero may require sacrificing accuracy.

Simple Baseline

The loss threshold is a principled, zero-cost baseline that should always be compared against.

Yeom et al. 2018

Limitations of the Overfitting View

  • Well-generalized models can still leak. The generalization gap is an average measure — individual samples can be highly vulnerable even when $\Delta$ is small.
  • Per-sample risk varies enormously. Outliers, rare examples, and mislabeled data are much more vulnerable than typical examples.
  • The bound is loose. The actual MIA advantage can be much smaller than $\Delta$ — the theorem gives a worst case.
  • Only covers the threshold attack. More sophisticated attacks (likelihood ratios) can succeed even when the threshold attack fails.

Key question: What is the optimal attack, and how powerful can it be?

The Bayes Optimal MIA

Given an observable signal $\phi(z)$ (e.g., loss, gradients), the optimal attack is the likelihood ratio test:

$$\Lambda(z) = \frac{p(\phi(z) \mid z \in D)}{p(\phi(z) \mid z \notin D)} \quad \gtrless \quad \tau$$

This follows directly from the Neyman-Pearson lemma (Slide 26) — the likelihood ratio maximizes power at any given significance level.

The question is: what signal $\phi(z)$ should we use? The loss $\ell(f_\theta, z)$? The gradients $\nabla_\theta \ell$? The full internal state?

Sablayrolles et al. 2019

Deriving the Optimal Test Statistic

For loss-based attack, the signal is $\phi(z) = \ell(f_\theta, z)$:

$$\Lambda(z) = \frac{p(\ell(f_\theta, z) \mid z \in D)}{p(\ell(f_\theta, z) \mid z \notin D)}$$

We need to estimate two distributions:

$p(\ell \mid \text{member})$

Distribution of loss on training points — typically low loss, concentrated

$p(\ell \mid \text{non-member})$

Distribution of loss on unseen points — typically higher loss, more spread

The ratio tells us: how "surprised" should we be to see this loss value, given each hypothesis?

Sablayrolles et al. 2019

In a white-box setting, the adversary has access to much richer signals:

$$\phi_{\text{WB}}(z) = \left[\ell, \; \|\nabla_{W_1} \ell\|, \ldots, \|\nabla_{W_L} \ell\|, \; h_1, \ldots, h_L\right]$$

Key Theorem: Black-Box ≈ White-Box

Theorem (Sablayrolles et al. 2019)

In the asymptotic regime (large models, standard training), the likelihood ratio based on the loss alone converges to the likelihood ratio based on all white-box features:

$$\Lambda_{\text{BB}}(z) \;\xrightarrow{\text{large models}}\; \Lambda_{\text{WB}}(z)$$

Implication: The loss is a near-sufficient statistic for membership. Gradients and activations add diminishing information.

Sablayrolles et al. 2019

Why the Loss Is (Nearly) Sufficient

  • The loss $\ell(f_\theta, z)$ compresses all information about how the model treats $z$
  • Gradients $\nabla_\theta \ell$ are functionally determined by the loss landscape at $z$ — they don't add independent information about membership
  • Activations $h_l(x)$ feed into the loss through the forward pass — the loss is their aggregate summary

Intuition: The loss answers the question "how well does the model fit this point?" That's exactly the question membership inference asks. Everything else is redundant.

Caveat: this is an asymptotic result. For small models or specific architectures, white-box features can provide a modest improvement.

Sablayrolles et al. 2019

Practical Implications

For Model Deployers

Restricting API access to labels or confidence scores doesn't meaningfully reduce privacy risk. Even label-only access leaks membership.

For Attackers

Don't bother with white-box attacks when the loss is available. Focus effort on better calibration of the loss distribution.

This result shifts the focus of MIA research from what signal to use to how to calibrate the signal — the central challenge of modern MIA.

Per-Sample Privacy Risk

Not all samples are equally vulnerable. Per-sample risk:

$$r(z) = \Pr[\mathcal{A}(z) = 1 \mid z \in D] - \Pr[\mathcal{A}(z) = 1 \mid z \notin D]$$

High Risk

  • Outliers and rare examples
  • Underrepresented classes
  • Mislabeled data

Low Risk

  • Common, typical examples
  • Well-represented classes
  • Easy-to-classify instances

The Problem with Early Evaluations

Prior work reported MIA results using balanced accuracy at a 50% threshold:

  • Hides performance on hard (but important) cases

What if the API only returns the predicted label — no confidence scores?

Query with augmented versions of $x$. Count how often the label stays correct:

Members are more robust to augmentation.

Members are further from the boundary.

Core idea: compute the likelihood ratio of the loss under two hypotheses:

IN Models ($N/2$)

Include $z$ in training data

Collect losses: $\{\ell_{\text{in}}^{(1)}, \ldots, \ell_{\text{in}}^{(N/2)}\}$

OUT Models ($N/2$)

Exclude $z$ from training data

Collect losses: $\{\ell_{\text{out}}^{(1)}, \ldots, \ell_{\text{out}}^{(N/2)}\}$

Fit Gaussian distributions to each set:

$$\ell_{\text{in}} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_{\text{in}}, \sigma_{\text{in}}^2) \qquad \ell_{\text{out}} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_{\text{out}}, \sigma_{\text{out}}^2)$$
Carlini et al. 2022

LiRA: Mathematical Formulation

The likelihood ratio becomes a ratio of Gaussian PDFs:

$$\Lambda(z) = \frac{\phi(\ell; \mu_{\text{in}}, \sigma_{\text{in}})}{\phi(\ell; \mu_{\text{out}}, \sigma_{\text{out}})}$$

where $\phi(\ell; \mu, \sigma) = \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}} \exp\left(-\frac{(\ell - \mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right)$ and $\ell = \ell(f_\theta, z)$ is the target model's loss.

Taking the log:

$$\log \Lambda(z) = \frac{(\ell - \mu_{\text{out}})^2}{2\sigma_{\text{out}}^2} - \frac{(\ell - \mu_{\text{in}})^2}{2\sigma_{\text{in}}^2} + \log\frac{\sigma_{\text{out}}}{\sigma_{\text{in}}}$$
Carlini et al. 2022

Per-Example Calibration

Key innovation: The distributions are computed per example $z$. Each data point gets its own $(\mu_{\text{in}}, \sigma_{\text{in}}, \mu_{\text{out}}, \sigma_{\text{out}})$.

Why this matters:

  • An "easy" example has low loss regardless of membership → needs tight thresholds
  • A "hard" example has high loss even as a member → needs different thresholds
  • A global threshold (Yeom) treats all examples the same — suboptimal
  • Per-example calibration captures the difficulty of each data point

Analogy: "Not all questions on an exam are equally hard — you need per-question grading."

Carlini et al. 2022

Online vs. Offline LiRA

Online LiRA

For each target $z$: train $N/2$ models with $z$ and $N/2$ without.

Pro: Exact per-example distributions

Con: $O(N \cdot |D|)$ total models needed

Offline LiRA

Train $N$ models on random subsets. Classify membership of $z$ post-hoc based on whether $z$ happened to be included.

Pro: Only $N$ models total

Con: Noisier estimates

In practice, offline LiRA with $N = 64$–$256$ models is standard.

Carlini et al. 2022

$$\text{Total cost} = N \times \text{(cost of training one model)}$$

RMIA: Core Idea

Instead of per-example shadow models, use a small set of reference models + population data:

$$\Lambda(z) = \frac{p_\theta(z)}{\frac{1}{R}\sum_{r=1}^R p_{\theta_r}(z)}$$

Numerator: target model's likelihood of $z$. Denominator: average likelihood across $R$ reference models.

If the target model assigns much higher likelihood to $z$ than reference models do, $z$ was likely in the target's training data.

Zarifzadeh et al. 2023

RMIA: Relative Comparison

Alternative formulation — rank the target's loss on $z$ among reference points:

$$s(z) = \frac{1}{|Z_{\text{ref}}|}\sum_{z' \in Z_{\text{ref}}} \mathbf{1}\left[\frac{p_\theta(z)}{p_{\theta_r}(z)} > \frac{p_\theta(z')}{p_{\theta_r}(z')}\right]$$

"How does $z$'s likelihood ratio rank among reference data points?"

  • Members have higher relative ranking than non-members
  • The ranking is more robust than absolute loss values
  • Resilient to distribution shift between reference and target

MIA Against Diffusion Models

Extending MIA to diffusion models — a generative architecture with unique challenges:

Challenge

Diffusion models have $T$ timesteps. No single forward pass — the denoising process is iterative.

Approach

Use reconstruction loss at specific timesteps as the membership signal.

$$\ell_t = \|\epsilon - \epsilon_\theta(x_t, t)\|^2 \qquad \bar{\ell} = \frac{1}{T}\sum_{t=1}^T \ell_t$$
Matsumoto et al. 2023

Timestep Analysis

Not all timesteps are equally informative for MIA:

Early Timesteps (low noise)

Small perturbation from original image. Model's prediction is close to the original. Most informative for MIA.

Late Timesteps (high noise)

Image is nearly pure noise. Model can't distinguish members from non-members. Least informative.

Optimal strategy: weight timesteps by informativeness — focus on early timesteps where the model "remembers" training details.

The Perplexity Baseline

The simplest LLM MIA — threshold on perplexity (loss):

$$\text{PPL}(x) = \exp\left(-\frac{1}{T}\sum_{t=1}^T \log p_\theta(x_t \mid x_{<t})\right)$$

Attack: $\mathcal{A}(x) = \mathbf{1}[\text{PPL}(x) < \tau]$ — low perplexity implies membership.

Problem: "The cat sat on the mat" has low perplexity regardless of membership. Common text is always low-perplexity. Perplexity conflates memorization with general fluency.

Why Raw Perplexity Fails

Low PPL ≠ Member

  • "Hello, how are you?" — PPL ≈ 5, never in training
  • Common phrases, boilerplate text
  • Simple factual statements

High PPL ≠ Non-member

  • Technical jargon, rare names — PPL is high even for training text
  • Code, mathematical notation
  • Domain-specific terminology

The core challenge: Separate "this text is intrinsically easy/hard" from "this text was memorized." This is the calibration problem for LLM MIA.

Mattern et al. 2023

Neighbourhood MIA

Compare target text against synthetic neighbours (paraphrases):

If the text is memorized: PPL(original) $\ll$ PPL(neighbours).

If the text is just easy: PPL(original) $\approx$ PPL(neighbours).

The gap between original and neighbours isolates the memorization signal.

Mattern et al. 2023

Generating Neighbours

Use another language model to create paraphrases via mask-and-fill:

  • Take target text $x$
  • Randomly mask spans of tokens
  • Fill masked spans with another LM (e.g., T5)
  • Result: $\tilde{x}^{(k)} = \text{fill}(\text{mask}(x))$ for $k = 1, \ldots, K$

The neighbours preserve the meaning and structure but change specific words — creating counterfactual versions of the text.

Mattern et al. 2023

Neighbourhood Score

$$s(x) = \log p_\theta(x) - \frac{1}{K}\sum_{k=1}^K \log p_\theta(\tilde{x}^{(k)})$$

$s(x) \gg 0$ → Member

Original is suspiciously more likely than its paraphrases. The model has specific memory of this exact text.

$s(x) \approx 0$ → Non-member

Original is about as likely as paraphrases. The model treats all versions similarly — no specific memorization.

The difference cancels out intrinsic difficulty: easy text has low PPL for both original and neighbours.

Mattern et al. 2023

Neighbourhood: Results & Limitations

Results

  • Significant improvement over raw perplexity
  • Works on GPT-2, GPT-Neo
  • No reference models needed
  • Connection to counterfactual reasoning

Limitations

  • Quality of neighbours matters
  • Expensive: $K$ generations per target
  • Mask-and-fill may not preserve meaning for technical text
  • Requires access to a separate generation model
Fu et al. 2023

SPV-MIA: Self-Prompt Calibration

Use the target LLM itself to generate calibration data — no external models needed.

Key insight: If the model was fine-tuned on text $x$, then prompting the model to generate text "like $x$" will produce output very similar to $x$ — because $x$ is memorized.

For non-members, the self-generated text will be less similar to the target.

Fu et al. 2023

Self-Prompting Strategies

Prefix Completion

Give the first few tokens of $x$ as prompt. Compare the model's continuation with the actual text.

Paraphrase Generation

Ask the model to paraphrase $x$. Members produce paraphrases closer to the original.

Topic-Conditioned

Prompt with the topic of $x$. Members generate text in the same style/content as $x$.

$$\text{Score}(x) = \frac{p_\theta(x)}{p_\theta(\text{self-prompt}(x))}$$

The self-prompt acts as a personalized reference — no external reference model needed.

Context-Aware MIA

Memorization in LLMs is context-dependent:

The same text is memorized differently depending on the surrounding context in which it appeared during training.

  • "To be or not to be" from Shakespeare's training data → memorized in Hamlet context
  • The same phrase in a random blog post → not memorized in that context
  • Previous MIA methods ignore context — they treat text as isolated
Chang et al. 2024

Context-Aware Attack Design

Provide different prefixes (contexts) and measure loss variation:

$$s(x) = \text{Var}_{c \in \mathcal{C}}\left[\log p_\theta(x \mid c)\right]$$

Members

With the right context, loss drops dramatically. With wrong context, it's higher. High variance.

Non-members

Loss is similar regardless of context — the model has no specific memory to activate. Low variance.

Context extraction: use beginning of document as prefix, topic-related prompts, or random contexts as baseline.

Chang et al. 2024

Context-Aware: Results & Implications

  • Significant improvement for pre-training data detection
  • Especially effective for longer documents with distinctive contexts
  • Complements other methods — can be combined with neighbourhood or token-level

Key implication: Memorization is not a property of text alone — it's a property of text-in-context. This has profound implications for data deletion and machine unlearning.

Token-Level Surprise

Define the surprise (information content) at each token position:

$$I_t = -\log p_\theta(x_t \mid x_{<t})$$

Low Surprise Tokens

"The", "is", "a" — predictable from context alone. Uninformative for MIA.

High Surprise Tokens

Named entities, specific numbers, rare words — only predictable if memorized. Highly informative.

Tao & Shokri 2025

InfoRMIA: Formulation

$$s(x) = \sum_{t \in \mathcal{T}_{\text{info}}} w_t \cdot \left(\log p_\theta(x_t \mid x_{<t}) - \log p_{\text{ref}}(x_t \mid x_{<t})\right)$$
  • $\mathcal{T}_{\text{info}}$ = set of informative tokens (high surprise under reference)
  • $w_t$ = weight reflecting token informativeness
  • $p_\theta$ = target model, $p_{\text{ref}}$ = reference model
  • The difference per token cancels intrinsic difficulty (like neighbourhood, but at token level)

Scale Effects on MIA Vulnerability

How does model size affect MIA vulnerability?

  • Larger models generalize better → smaller generalization gap → less MIA by Yeom's bound
  • But larger models also memorize more → more data is extractable (Carlini et al.)
  • Net effect: larger models may have lower average vulnerability but higher worst-case vulnerability

Scaling law of memorization: The amount of memorized content grows with model size and training repetitions. More parameters = more capacity to store specific training examples.

Extractable vs. Inferable Memorization

Extractable

The model can be prompted to generate verbatim training text. Directly produces the memorized content.

Stronger attack, harder to achieve.

Inferable

Given a text, the model's behavior reveals whether it was trained on it. Doesn't produce the text itself.

Weaker attack (MIA), but more broadly applicable.

Inferable memorization is a superset of extractable — everything extractable is inferable, but not vice versa.

LLM Defenses: DP Fine-Tuning

Apply DP-SGD during fine-tuning to limit memorization:

  • DP-SGD for fine-tuning is more practical than for pre-training (smaller dataset, fewer steps)
  • Per-example gradient clipping + noise injection
  • Utility cost is significant but manageable for fine-tuning
  • Libraries: Opacus (PyTorch), TensorFlow Privacy

DP for pre-training at LLM scale remains an open challenge — the utility-privacy tradeoff is too severe for trillion-token training.

Deduplication & Data Curation

Reducing memorization through training data quality:

Deduplication

Remove repeated text from the training corpus. Repeated text is memorized much more strongly.

Carlini et al. showed: memorization scales with repetition count.

Data Curation

Filter out PII, unique identifiers, and sensitive content before training.

Prevents the most harmful memorization but doesn't eliminate MIA vulnerability.

Key Themes Across the Decade

1. Overfitting Is the Root Cause

From Yeom's bound to LLM memorization: the gap between training and population behavior is what MIA exploits.

2. Black-Box ≈ White-Box

The loss contains nearly all membership information. Gradients and activations add negligible signal.

3. Per-Example Calibration

Global thresholds are suboptimal. The best attacks calibrate for each data point's difficulty.

4. Finer Granularity

From sequence-level → document-level → token-level. The trend is toward more precise localization of memorization.

Open Problem: Pre-Training Detection

Detecting membership in pre-training data remains the hardest open problem:

  • Weak signal: text seen once among trillions of tokens
  • Current best: ~70–75% AUC (far from perfect)
  • Unknown training data makes ground truth approximate
  • Scaling to larger models hasn't clearly improved detection

Needs: Better calibration, multi-signal fusion, and improved benchmarks with verified ground truth.

Open Problem: Multi-Modal MIA

MIA for vision-language models (CLIP, GPT-4V, Gemini):

  • Image-text pairs create richer membership signal than either modality alone
  • Cross-modal leakage: can you infer text membership from image behavior?
  • Multimodal models are trained on web-scale image-text pairs — massive attack surface
  • Almost entirely unexplored in the literature

Open Problem: Reference-Free MIA

Can we achieve LiRA-level performance with zero reference models?

  • SPV-MIA uses self-generation — a step toward reference-free
  • Neighbourhood uses an auxiliary model for paraphrasing
  • True zero-resource MIA (only the target model) remains limited to threshold attacks
  • Information-theoretic bounds on reference-free attack power are unknown

Open Problem: MIA for Agents & RAG

New threat models for compound AI systems:

RAG Systems

Is a retrieved document from the training set or the retrieval corpus? MIA must distinguish pre-trained knowledge from retrieved context.

LLM Agents

Agents with memory and tool use. Membership in what? Training data, conversation history, or tool outputs?

These systems blur the line between "training data" and "operational data" — new threat models are needed.
