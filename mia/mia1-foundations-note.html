Slide 34 + 34b — TPR at Low FPR

Why average accuracy is misleading

Two attacks with the same balanced accuracy can have wildly different real-world impact. An attack achieving 60% balanced accuracy by being slightly better than chance everywhere is useless when an auditor needs high-confidence positives — i.e. the regime where FPR is well below 1%. Carlini et al. (2022) reframed evaluation around this regime: TPR at low FPR is the only metric that aligns with the threat model.

Why log-log ROC matters

On a linear ROC plot, the low-FPR regime is compressed into a sliver near the y-axis — most of the visual area is wasted on the high-FPR end that nobody cares about for privacy. A log-log axis stretches the low-FPR regime, making it possible to compare attacks at FPR = $10^{-3}$ vs. $10^{-4}$ vs. $10^{-5}$ at a glance.

Pitfalls when reading metric tables

  • AUC ≠ low-FPR power. Two attacks can share an AUC of 0.6 while one has 5% TPR @ 0.1% FPR and the other has 0%.
  • Per-example variance. Population-level numbers hide the vulnerable subpopulation. Always report a histogram or per-example calibration check.
  • TPR @ 0.01% FPR vs. 0.1% FPR. Stronger privacy claims require the lower bound; the gap between the two columns is itself diagnostic.
