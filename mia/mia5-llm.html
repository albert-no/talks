Why LLMs Are Different

Perplexity and its failures

Why LLMs Are Different

Massive Pre-training

Trillions of web tokens.

Data unknown.

Autoregressive

$p(x) = \prod_t p(x_t \mid x_{<t})$.

Variable length.

Layered Memorization

Pre-training + fine-tuning + RLHF.

Scale

Billions of parameters.

Shadow models infeasible.

Three LLM Threat Models

Pre-training

"In Pile / C4 / web crawl?"

Hardest.

Weak signal.

Fine-tuning

"In RLHF / tuning set?"

Moderate signal.

Context

"In prompt context?"

Easiest.

Strong signal.

Research focus: pre-training (hardest) and fine-tuning.

Perplexity Baseline

Simplest LLM MIA: threshold on perplexity (loss).

$$\text{PPL}(x) = \exp\left(-\frac{1}{T}\sum_{t=1}^T \log p_\theta(x_t \mid x_{<t})\right)$$

Attack: $\mathcal{A}(x) = \mathbf{1}[\text{PPL}(x) < \tau]$.

Low PPL implies member.

Problem: common text always low-PPL.

Conflates memorization with fluency.

Why Raw Perplexity Fails

Low PPL ≠ Member

  • "Hello, how are you?": low PPL, untrained
  • Common boilerplate

High PPL ≠ Non-member

  • Jargon, rare names: high PPL even if trained
  • Code, math notation

Core challenge: intrinsic difficulty vs. memorization.

The calibration problem.

02

Neighbourhood Attack

Mattern et al. 2023

Mattern et al. 2023

Neighbourhood MIA

Target vs. synthetic neighbours (paraphrases).

Memorized: PPL(original) $\ll$ PPL(neighbours).

Easy: PPL(original) $\approx$ PPL(neighbours).

Gap isolates memorization signal.

Mattern et al. 2023

Generating Neighbours

Another LM generates paraphrases via mask-and-fill.

  • Take target text $x$
  • Randomly mask token spans
  • Fill masks with another LM (e.g., T5)
  • Result: $\tilde{x}^{(k)} = \text{fill}(\text{mask}(x))$

Meaning and structure preserved; surface words vary.

Mattern et al. 2023

Neighbourhood Score

$$s(x) = \log p_\theta(x) - \frac{1}{K}\sum_{k=1}^K \log p_\theta(\tilde{x}^{(k)})$$

$s(x) \gg 0$ → Member

Original suspiciously more likely than paraphrases.

$s(x) \approx 0$ → Non-member

Original as likely as paraphrases.

Difference cancels intrinsic difficulty.

Mattern et al. 2023

Neighbourhood: Results & Limitations

Results

  • Large gain over raw PPL
  • No reference models needed
  • Counterfactual reasoning link

Limitations

  • Neighbour quality matters
  • Expensive: $K$ generations
  • Needs generation model

SPV-MIA and Context-Aware MIA

Reference-model strategies for LLMs

Fu et al. 2023

SPV-MIA: Self-Prompt Calibration

Target LLM generates its own calibration data. No external models.

Key insight: fine-tuned on $x$ → self-prompt output stays close to $x$.

Non-members: self-generated output less similar.

Fu et al. 2023

Self-Prompting Strategies

Prefix Completion

First tokens of $x$ as prompt.

Compare continuation.

Paraphrase

Paraphrase $x$.

Members stay close to original.

Topic-Conditioned

Prompt with topic.

Members: same style.

$$\text{Score}(x) = \frac{p_\theta(x)}{p_\theta(\text{self-prompt}(x))}$$

Self-prompt: personalized reference, no external model needed.

Fu et al. 2023

  • Best for fine-tuning detection (strong signal)

Chang et al. 2024

Context-Aware MIA

LLM memorization is context-dependent.

Same text memorized differently by surrounding context at training.

  • "To be or not to be" in Hamlet context: memorized
  • Same phrase in random blog: not
  • Prior MIA: text treated in isolation
Chang et al. 2024

Context-Aware Attack Design

Provide different prefixes, measure loss variation.

$$s(x) = \text{Var}_{c \in \mathcal{C}}\left[\log p_\theta(x \mid c)\right]$$

Members

Right context: loss drops. High variance.

Non-members

Loss similar across contexts. Low variance.

Context sources: document start, topic prompts, random baseline.

Chang et al. 2024

Context-Aware: Results & Implications

  • Significant gain on pre-training detection
  • Best on longer documents with distinctive contexts
  • Complementary: combine with neighbourhood or token-level

Key implication: memorization is text-in-context, not text alone.

Consequences for deletion and unlearning.

04

InfoRMIA

Information-theoretic token-level attack (2025)

Tao & Shokri 2025

Token-Level InfoRMIA

Not all tokens equally informative for MIA.

  • Common words ("the", "is", "and"): predictable regardless
  • Named entities, numbers, rare phrases: signal only if memorized
  • Informative tokens: stronger MIA signal
Tao & Shokri, "Stronger Membership Inference and Memorization Assessment for LLMs", 2025.
Tao & Shokri 2025

Token-Level Surprise

Surprise (information content) at each token position:

$$I_t = -\log p_\theta(x_t \mid x_{<t})$$

Low Surprise

"The", "is", "a": predictable. Uninformative.

High Surprise

Entities, numbers, rare words: predictable only if memorized. Highly informative.

Tao & Shokri 2025

InfoRMIA: Formulation

$$s(x) = \sum_{t \in \mathcal{T}_{\text{info}}} w_t\,\log\frac{p_\theta(x_t \mid x_{<t})}{p_{\text{ref}}(x_t \mid x_{<t})}$$
  • $\mathcal{T}_{\text{info}}$: informative tokens (high reference surprise)
  • $w_t$: informativeness weight
  • $p_\theta$: target, $p_{\text{ref}}$: reference
  • Per-token difference cancels intrinsic difficulty (token-level neighbourhood)
Tao & Shokri 2025

Identifying Informative Tokens

Strategies to find $\mathcal{T}_{\text{info}}$:

High Ref Surprise

Reference surprised, target not.

Memorization signal.

Named Entities

People, places, organizations.

Target-Ref Gap

Tokens where they disagree most.

Information-Theoretic Foundation

Connection to mutual information.

$$I(z \in D \;;\; x_t \mid x_{<t})$$

MI between membership and token prediction at position $t$.

  • High-MI tokens contribute most
  • Weighted sum maximizes total MI
  • Formal bound on attack power from token-level MI
Tao & Shokri 2025

Information-Theoretic Foundation (cont.)

InfoRMIA: token-level analogue of likelihood ratio.

Locates where the signal lives.

From MIA to Extraction

Stronger attacks on the same foundation

MIA to Extraction

MIA: precursor to data extraction.

MIA: $x$ a member?
→
Extraction: generate $x$
→
Verify: $x$ from training?
  • Carlini et al. (2021): 600+ verbatim GPT-2 sequences extracted
  • MIA as verification of generated text
  • Chain: generate candidates, rank by MIA, extract top

Extractable vs. Inferable Memorization

Extractable

Prompt to generate verbatim training text.

Stronger attack, harder.

Inferable

Behavior reveals membership without producing the text.

Weaker (MIA), broadly applicable.

Inferable is a superset of extractable (not vice versa).

LLM MIA: Perplexity + Neighbourhood

// Perplexity Baseline def ppl_attack(model, text, τ): ppl ← exp(-mean([log_prob(model, t, text[:i]) for i,t in text])) return ppl < τ // Neighbourhood Attack (no shadow models) def neighbourhood_attack(model, ref_model, text, K): neighbours ← [mask_and_fill(ref_model, text) for _ in range(K)] score ← log_prob(model, text) - mean([log_prob(model, n) for n in neighbours]) return score

Baseline: single threshold. Neighbourhood: local paraphrase calibration.

LLM MIA: Token-Level InfoRMIA

// Token-Level InfoRMIA def infomia_attack(target, ref, text): scores ← [] for t, token in enumerate(text): diff ← log_prob(target, token, text[:t]) - log_prob(ref, token, text[:t]) w ← informativeness(ref, token, text[:t]) scores.append(w * diff) return sum(scores)

Per-token weighted log-ratio: down-weight tokens the reference already explains.

LLM Defenses and Governance

DP fine-tuning, deduplication, unlearning, law

LLM Defenses: DP Fine-Tuning

DP-SGD during fine-tuning limits memorization.

  • DP-SGD fine-tuning: practical (small dataset, few steps)
  • Per-example gradient clipping + noise
  • Utility cost significant but manageable
  • Libraries: Opacus (PyTorch), TensorFlow Privacy

DP for pre-training at LLM scale: open challenge.

Tradeoff too severe at trillion-token scale

Deduplication & Data Curation

Reduced memorization via training data quality.

Deduplication

Repeated content is memorized far more.

Carlini: memorization scales with repetition.

Data Curation

Filter PII, IDs, sensitive content pre-training.

Helps, but MIA still possible.

Machine Unlearning for LLMs

Data point's influence removed without full retraining.

Unified Framework

Every MIA: a special case of the likelihood ratio.

$$\mathcal{A}(z) = \mathbf{1}\left[\frac{p(\text{signal} \mid z \in D)}{p(\text{signal} \mid z \notin D)} > \tau\right]$$

Methods differ in: choice of signal and calibration.

Key Themes

1. Overfitting

MIA exploits the train-population gap.

2. Black-Box ≈ White-Box

Loss holds nearly all membership info.

3. Per-Example Calibration

Global thresholds suboptimal.

Calibrate per point.

4. Finer Granularity

Sequence to document to token.

Open Problem: Pre-Training Detection

Pre-training membership: hardest open problem.

  • Signal: seen once among trillions
  • Best: ~70 to 75% AUC
  • Training data unknown; ground truth approximate
  • Scaling has not clearly improved detection

Needs: calibration, multi-signal fusion, verified benchmarks.

Open Problem: Multi-Modal MIA

MIA for vision-language models (CLIP, GPT-4V, Gemini).

  • Image-text pairs: richer signal than either alone
  • Cross-modal leakage: text membership from image behavior?
  • Web-scale image-text training: massive surface
  • Almost entirely unexplored

Open Problem: Reference-Free MIA

LiRA-level with zero reference models?

  • SPV-MIA: self-generation, step toward reference-free
  • Neighbourhood: auxiliary model for paraphrasing
  • Zero-resource MIA: threshold attacks only
  • Info-theoretic bounds on reference-free power: unknown

Open Problem: MIA for Agents & RAG

New threat models: compound AI systems.

RAG Systems

Training set or retrieval corpus? MIA must separate the two.

LLM Agents

Memory + tools.

Membership in what?

Training vs. operational data blurs; new threat models needed.
