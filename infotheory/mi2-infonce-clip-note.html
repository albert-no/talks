The InfoNCE proof in finer detail

The slide gives a Jensen-style proof. The complete argument (Poole et al. 2019, Oord et al. 2018):

\[ \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{InfoNCE}}(f^\star)=-\mathbb{E}\log\frac{r(x,y)}{r(x,y)+\sum_k r(x,y_k^-)}, \]

where $f^\star(x,y)=\log r(x,y)$. Add and subtract $\log K$ inside:

\[ =\mathbb{E}\log\frac{1}{K}\Bigl(r(x,y)+\sum_k r(x,y_k^-)\Bigr)-\mathbb{E}\log r(x,y)+\log K. \]

The first term is $\le \log\frac{1}{K}\bigl(\mathbb{E}r(x,y)+(K-1)\mathbb{E}_{p_X p_Y}r\bigr)=\log\frac{K}{K}=0$ (Jensen plus $\mathbb{E}_{p_X p_Y}r=1$). The second term is $-I(X;Y)$. So $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{InfoNCE}}(f^\star)\le \log K-I(X;Y)$, i.e. $I\ge\log K-\mathcal{L}$. The final inequality holds for any $f$ since $f^\star$ minimizes the loss.

Why exactly $\log K$ saturation, not just an upper bound

The slide states the bound saturates at $\log K$. Why is this exactly the ceiling? Because the InfoNCE loss is a softmax cross-entropy: its minimum value when the optimal classifier is perfect is $0$, and the maximum value is $\log K$ (uniform prediction). So $\log K-\mathcal{L}\in[0,\log K]$ regardless of MI.

For $I>\log K$, the bound is trivially $\log K$ — no information about how much larger $I$ is. The estimator is bias-low by exactly $I-\log K$ in the limit of perfect critic.

Temperature $\tau$ in CLIP

The temperature scales the inner product before the softmax. Smaller $\tau$ means sharper softmax: a few similarity scores dominate. Larger $\tau$ smooths the distribution.

CLIP learns $\tau$ as a parameter, clipped to a maximum of $100$ to prevent training instability. The learned value at convergence corresponds to a sharply peaked similarity matrix — in the contrastive regime, the diagonal needs to dominate by orders of magnitude.

This is connected to information bottleneck: tightening $\tau$ corresponds to demanding the encoder produce very confident matches. The exact tradeoff is studied in Wang & Isola (2020), "Understanding Contrastive Representation Learning through Alignment and Uniformity on the Hypersphere".

Why the symmetric loss

The asymmetric loss $\mathcal{L}_{x\to y}$ trains image embeddings to retrieve their captions. The reverse $\mathcal{L}_{y\to x}$ trains text embeddings to retrieve their images. Both are valid InfoNCE objectives on the same data, just with $X$ and $Y$ swapped.

Empirically, training only one direction creates a representation imbalance: the encoder corresponding to the "querier" learns sharper features than the "key". The symmetric average eliminates this asymmetry.

Theoretically, $\frac{1}{2}(\mathcal{L}_{x\to y}+\mathcal{L}_{y\to x})$ is also a valid InfoNCE bound on $I(X;Y)$, since MI is symmetric.

The CLIP zero-shot recipe in detail

Beyond the basic 4-step recipe on the slide:

  1. Prompt ensembling: use 80 different prompt templates ("a photo of a {label}", "a {label} in the wild", "a sketch of a {label}", etc.) and average the resulting text embeddings. Boosts ImageNet accuracy by $\sim 5$%.
  2. Class name engineering: ImageNet labels are sometimes ambiguous ("crane" — bird? machine?). The CLIP paper uses category-disambiguated names ("crane (bird)") for hard cases.
  3. Temperature at inference: the learned $\tau$ from training is reused; do not re-learn at evaluation time.

The full evaluation protocol: encode test images $\to$ compute cosine similarity with $\sim 80$-prompt-ensemble text embeddings $\to$ argmax over labels.

Robustness and other zero-shot capabilities

Zero-shot ImageNet is a single benchmark; CLIP's broader appeal is robustness. Trained on $400$M image–text pairs from the web, it transfers across domains: ImageNet-Sketch ($\sim 10$% accuracy from supervised ResNet-50, $\sim 20$% from CLIP), ImageNet-A (adversarial natural examples, $\sim 50$% gap), and so on.

Information-theoretically, this is because the text supervision is a much weaker but much broader signal than ImageNet's $1000$-way labels. The MI maximization is across any caption and image, not just labeled categories.

What CLIP does not do well

For full context:

  • Fine-grained categories: bird species, car models — underrepresented in web text.
  • Counting and spatial reasoning: "three cats on the left" doesn't reliably retrieve correct images.
  • OCR-heavy tasks: only weak text reading from images.
  • Compositional generalization: "purple cow" vs. "cow with purple background" can be confused.

These are limitations of the data and the contrastive objective, not of InfoNCE itself. Subsequent work (Flamingo, BLIP, generative image-text models) addresses some of these by adding a generative component on top of the CLIP-style backbone.
