01

The Master Reparameterization

One identity that drives every parameterization choice

Recall: $q(x_t\mid x_0)$ in reparameterized form

From Lecture 2:

$$x_t\;=\;\sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}\,x_0\;+\;\sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}\,\varepsilon,\qquad\varepsilon\sim\mathcal{N}(0,I).$$

This is an algebraic identity between three random variables: $(x_0,\varepsilon,t)\;\to\;x_t$. Knowing any two recovers the third:

$$x_0\;=\;\dfrac{x_t-\sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}\,\varepsilon}{\sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}},\qquad\varepsilon\;=\;\dfrac{x_t-\sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}\,x_0}{\sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}}.$$

Training data: sample $(x_0,t,\varepsilon)$, compute $x_t$, regress.

02

Three Equivalent Parameterizations

$x_0$, $\varepsilon$, and the score — pick your favorite

Parameterization 1 — predict $x_0$

Train a network $x_{0,\theta}(x_t,t)$ to predict the clean data:

$$\mathcal{L}_{x_0}=\mathbb{E}_{(x_0,t,\varepsilon)}\!\bigl[\,\|x_0-x_{0,\theta}(x_t,t)\|^2\,\bigr],\qquad x_t=\sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}x_0+\sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}\varepsilon.$$

Optimal predictor under squared error: $x_0^\star(x_t,t)=\mathbb{E}[X_0\mid X_t=x_t]$ — the posterior mean.

Pro: directly interpretable. Con: at large $t$ where $x_t$ is mostly noise, the target $x_0$ is hard to predict (high variance loss).

Parameterization 2 — predict the noise $\varepsilon$

Train $\varepsilon_\theta(x_t,t)$ to predict the noise that produced $x_t$:

$$\mathcal{L}_\varepsilon=\mathbb{E}_{(x_0,t,\varepsilon)}\!\bigl[\,\|\varepsilon-\varepsilon_\theta(x_t,t)\|^2\,\bigr].$$

Equivalent to the $x_0$ form by the master reparameterization — up to a $t$-dependent rescaling. The default choice in DDPM (Ho et al. 2020).

Pro: target has unit variance at every $t$, gradient signal is well-conditioned across noise levels.

Parameterization 3 — predict the score

Define the marginal density at noise level $t$ as $p_t(x)=q(x_t=x)$. Its score function is

$$\nabla_x\log p_t(x).$$

Train a score network $s_\theta(x_t,t)\approx\nabla_x\log p_t(x_t)$. Used in score-based generative modeling (Song & Ermon, Song et al.) and SDE diffusion.

The score is what determines the reverse-time SDE drift. We will derive its connection to the noise prediction next.

03

Tweedie's Formula

Posterior mean of clean data $=$ rescaled score

Tweedie's formula

For diffusion: $X_t=\sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}X_0+\sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}\varepsilon$. Then

$$\mathbb{E}[X_0\mid X_t=x_t]\;=\;\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}}\Bigl(x_t+(1-\bar\alpha_t)\,\nabla_{x_t}\log p_t(x_t)\Bigr).$$

The posterior mean of $X_0$ is the noisy observation $x_t$, plus a correction proportional to the score, all rescaled.

Tweedie's formula — proof

Proof

Marginal density of $x_t$:

$$p_t(x_t)=\int p_0(x_0)\,\mathcal{N}(x_t;\sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}x_0,(1-\bar\alpha_t)I)\,dx_0.$$

Differentiate $\log p_t$ with respect to $x_t$. The Gaussian's score in $x_t$ is $-\frac{x_t-\sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}x_0}{1-\bar\alpha_t}$. By exchanging differentiation and integration:

$$\nabla_{x_t}\log p_t(x_t)=\dfrac{1}{p_t(x_t)}\!\int\!-\dfrac{x_t-\sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}x_0}{1-\bar\alpha_t}\,p(x_t,x_0)\,dx_0=-\dfrac{x_t-\sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}\,\mathbb{E}[X_0\mid x_t]}{1-\bar\alpha_t}.$$

Solve for $\mathbb{E}[X_0\mid x_t]$ to get the formula. $\;\blacksquare$

Three-way conversion: $x_0$, $\varepsilon$, score

Combine Tweedie and the master reparameterization. From the latter,

$$\mathbb{E}[\varepsilon\mid x_t]\;=\;\dfrac{x_t-\sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}\,\mathbb{E}[X_0\mid x_t]}{\sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}}\;=\;-\sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}\,\nabla_{x_t}\log p_t(x_t).$$

So the optimal noise predictor is exactly the score, rescaled:

$$\varepsilon_\theta^\star(x_t,t)\;=\;-\sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}\,s_\theta^\star(x_t,t),\qquad x_{0,\theta}^\star=\dfrac{x_t-\sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}\,\varepsilon_\theta^\star}{\sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}}.$$

Three names for the same object. Pick the parameterization that conditions the loss best.

04

Information-Theoretic View

Each forward step is a noisy channel; reverse is approximate inversion

The forward chain as a sequence of channels

Each step $X_{t-1}\to X_t$ is an additive Gaussian channel:

$$X_t\;=\;\sqrt{\alpha_t}\,X_{t-1}\;+\;\sqrt{\beta_t}\,\eta_t,\qquad\eta_t\sim\mathcal{N}(0,I).$$

By the data-processing inequality, mutual information is monotone non-increasing along the chain:

$$I(X_0;X_T)\;\le\;I(X_0;X_{T-1})\;\le\;\cdots\;\le\;I(X_0;X_1)\;\le\;I(X_0;X_0).$$

As $\bar\alpha_T\to 0$, $I(X_0;X_T)\to 0$: the noise erases all information about the source.

Reverse process as approximate channel inversion

Each reverse step $p_\theta(x_{t-1}\mid x_t)$ approximates the true posterior $p(x_{t-1}\mid x_t)$ — the optimal inverter for the channel $X_{t-1}\to X_t$.

If perfect inversion were possible, the reverse chain would recover $X_0$ exactly. In practice each step incurs a per-letter KL gap, accumulating to the ELBO slack across the $T$ steps.

Diffusion training is approximate universal channel decoding at $T$ noise levels simultaneously.

Connection to successive refinement

Rate–distortion successive refinement: describe a source at decreasing distortion levels using a nested codebook structure. Each refinement uses the previous coarse description as a starting point.

Diffusion has the same flavor:

  • $X_T$ is the coarsest description — pure noise.
  • $X_{T-1},\ldots,X_1$ are intermediate refinements at decreasing noise levels.
  • $X_0$ is the finest reconstruction.

The reverse process learns these refinements simultaneously by sharing parameters across $t$.

The complete picture

Diffusion ties together:

  • Lecture 1 (VAE): ELBO and the posterior-KL gap.
  • Lecture 2 (hierarchical): the chain structure and closed-form reverse conditional.
  • Lecture 3 (this): three parameterizations linked by Tweedie and the channel view.
  • Lossy compression (Module 1): $X_t$ is essentially the source $X_0$ corrupted via a Gaussian noise channel; the reverse process is the encoder-decoder pair from rate–distortion theory.

One unifying lens: a diffusion model is a learned, hierarchical, near-optimal code for the data distribution under increasing distortion.

Recap of Lecture 3

  • Master reparameterization: $x_t=\sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}x_0+\sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}\varepsilon$ ties three views.
  • Predict $x_0$: direct, but high-variance at large $t$.
  • Predict $\varepsilon$: well-conditioned across noise levels — standard in practice.
  • Predict score: $\nabla_x\log p_t$ — the SDE perspective.
  • Tweedie: $\mathbb{E}[X_0\mid x_t]=\frac{1}{\sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}}(x_t+(1-\bar\alpha_t)\nabla\log p_t)$ links posterior mean and score.
  • Channel view: forward = noisy Gaussian channel; reverse = approximate inversion; full chain = successive refinement code.
