Why Gaussian achievability uses a "backward" channel

The natural way to write a test channel is forward: pick $X$, then sample $\widehat X\mid X$. For the Gaussian rate–distortion problem, this forward form is more complicated — the conditional law $\widehat X\mid X$ is a shrunken Gaussian with both a non-trivial mean and a non-trivial variance.

The backward form $X=\widehat X+Z$ trades that complication for a clean independence statement: $\widehat X\perp Z$. The mutual information drops out instantly because $h(X\mid\widehat X)=h(Z)$, and the distortion $\mathbb{E}(X-\widehat X)^2=\mathbb{E}Z^2$ is also immediate. Both forms describe the same joint distribution; the backward form is just easier to compute with.

What the Gaussian-vs-other-source comparison is really saying

The slide says "Gaussian is the hardest source at fixed variance." Two distinct ways to read this, both correct:

  • Upper bound on $R_X(D)$: any source with variance $\sigma^2$ admits a code at rate $\le\frac12\log(\sigma^2/D)$. So $\frac12\log(\sigma^2/D)$ is universally achievable.
  • Tightness for Gaussians: the bound is achieved by the Gaussian source. So the bound is the actual rate–distortion function only in that case.

For non-Gaussian sources, $R_X(D)<\frac12\log(\sigma^2/D)$ strictly — structure beyond variance helps. Skewed sources, multimodal sources, sparse sources are all easier than the variance-matched Gaussian.

The auxiliary-density trick in the Laplacian converse

The line "$I(X;\widehat X)\ge\mathbb{E}\log(q/p_X)$" looks like sleight of hand. Where does it come from?

By definition, $I(X;\widehat X)=\mathbb{E}\log\frac{p_{X\mid\widehat X}}{p_X}$. For any other conditional density $q_{X\mid\widehat X}$,

\[ \mathbb{E}\log\frac{p_{X\mid\widehat X}}{q_{X\mid\widehat X}} \;=\;\mathbb{E}\bigl[D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p_{X\mid\widehat X}\Vert q_{X\mid\widehat X})\bigr]\;\ge\;0, \]

so $\mathbb{E}\log p_{X\mid\widehat X}\ge\mathbb{E}\log q_{X\mid\widehat X}$, hence $I(X;\widehat X)\ge\mathbb{E}\log(q_{X\mid\widehat X}/p_X)$. The bound is tight when $q$ matches the true posterior, which is exactly what happens for the optimal mixture-based test channel.

Choosing $q$ to be a Laplace centered at $\widehat X$ with scale $D$ is engineering: that density makes the expectation collapse to a closed form involving only $\mathbb{E}|X|$ and $\mathbb{E}|X-\widehat X|$, both of which we have explicit bounds on.

Verifying the Laplacian mixture by characteristic functions

The mixture $V=\lambda^2D^2\,\delta_0+(1-\lambda^2D^2)\mathrm{Lap}(\lambda)$ has char. fn.

\[ \phi_V(t)=\lambda^2D^2\cdot 1+(1-\lambda^2D^2)\,\frac{\lambda^2}{\lambda^2+t^2}. \]

Multiply by $\phi_N(t)=1/(1+D^2t^2)$:

\[ \phi_V(t)\phi_N(t)\;=\;\frac{\lambda^2D^2(\lambda^2+t^2)+(1-\lambda^2D^2)\lambda^2}{(\lambda^2+t^2)(1+D^2t^2)}\;=\;\frac{\lambda^2(1+D^2t^2)}{(\lambda^2+t^2)(1+D^2t^2)}\;=\;\frac{\lambda^2}{\lambda^2+t^2}\;=\;\phi_X(t). \]

Numerator simplification: $\lambda^2D^2\lambda^2+\lambda^2D^2t^2+\lambda^2-\lambda^4D^2=\lambda^2+\lambda^2D^2t^2=\lambda^2(1+D^2t^2)$. So $V+N$ is exactly $\mathrm{Lap}(\lambda)$.

The atom at zero is not an artifact

The atom is forced by the converse. The auxiliary density $q_{X\mid\widehat X}$ is Laplace centered at $\widehat X$ with scale $D$, which means that conditional on $\widehat X=0$, the source has density $\frac{1}{2D}e^{-|X|/D}$ — a sharp peak at zero. For the converse equality to hold, the joint $P_{X,\widehat X}$ must have $\widehat X=0$ with non-trivial probability.

So the rate–distortion-optimal compressor, when reconstructing a Laplacian source under $\ell_1$ distortion, must zero out a fraction of coordinates. Not for engineering convenience — for information-theoretic optimality.

Why output bound uses $\ell_1$, not $\ell_2$

The proof telescopes a layerwise perturbation through 1-Lipschitz nonlinearities. The Lipschitz constant of $\sigma$ is 1 in $\ell_2$ and in $\ell_1$, but the matrix multiplication step is $\|W\Delta\|_1\le\|W\|_1\|\Delta\|_1$ where $\|W\|_1$ is the column-sum norm. That gives the clean product over layers.

An $\ell_2$ analogue exists via spectral norms: $\|W\Delta\|_2\le\|W\|_2\|\Delta\|_2$. The bound has the same structure, but the per-coordinate distortion problem then becomes squared-error rather than absolute-error, and the optimal reproduction is Gaussian rather than mixture-with-atom. No sparsity falls out. So the $\ell_1$ formulation is what makes pruning a theorem.

Why CROM rotations Gaussianize

For any fixed unit vector $r^n\in\mathbb{R}^n$, multiplying by a Haar-random orthogonal matrix produces $A r^n$ uniformly distributed on the sphere of radius $\|r^n\|$. In high dimension, a uniformly random point on the sphere has coordinates that are approximately independent Gaussian with variance $\|r^n\|^2/n$.

This is one of the more useful soft facts in high-dimensional probability: the sphere is so big that a uniform draw on it looks marginal-Gaussian on every coordinate, even though the joint constraint $\sum y_i^2=1$ couples them. Concentration tells us the coupling is weak in the relevant tail regime.

Once the residual coordinates look approximately Gaussian, the extreme-value behavior is predictable, so the next sparse-extreme step can be applied.
