01

Generative Modeling

Latent variable models, MLE, and the integral that won't go away

The generative goal

Given data $x_1,\ldots,x_N\sim p_{\text{data}}$, learn a parametric model $p_\theta(x)$ that:

  • assigns high likelihood to data, and
  • can be sampled from to generate new $x$.

Latent-variable model. Introduce a latent $Z$ with simple prior $p(z)$ (e.g. $\mathcal{N}(0,I)$) and a flexible decoder $p_\theta(x\mid z)$:

$$p_\theta(x,z)=p(z)\,p_\theta(x\mid z),\qquad p_\theta(x)=\int p_\theta(x,z)\,dz.$$

Maximum likelihood

Train by minimizing average negative log-likelihood:

$$\theta^\star\;\in\;\arg\min_\theta\;\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N -\log p_\theta(x_i).$$

To compute $\log p_\theta(x_i)$, we need $p_\theta(x_i)=\int p_\theta(x_i,z)\,dz$. For a flexible decoder, this integral is intractable.

Variational inference replaces the intractable integral with a tractable lower bound.

02

The Variational Bound (ELBO)

Two derivations of the same inequality

Introduce a variational posterior

Pick a parametric family of conditionals $q_\phi(z\mid x)$ — the encoder. Together with the decoder $p_\theta(x\mid z)$, this gives

$$p_\theta(x)\;=\;\int p_\theta(x,z)\,dz\;=\;\int q_\phi(z\mid x)\,\dfrac{p_\theta(x,z)}{q_\phi(z\mid x)}\,dz\;=\;\mathbb{E}_{z\sim q_\phi(\cdot\mid x)}\!\left[\dfrac{p_\theta(x,z)}{q_\phi(z\mid x)}\right].$$

So $\log p_\theta(x)=\log\mathbb{E}_{z\sim q_\phi}[p_\theta(x,z)/q_\phi(z\mid x)]$. Now apply Jensen.

Derivation 1 — Jensen's inequality

Derivation via concavity of $\log$

$\log$ is concave, so $\log\mathbb{E}[X]\ge\mathbb{E}[\log X]$. Apply to the previous identity:

$$\log p_\theta(x)\;=\;\log\,\mathbb{E}_{q_\phi}\!\left[\dfrac{p_\theta(x,z)}{q_\phi(z\mid x)}\right]\;\ge\;\mathbb{E}_{q_\phi}\!\left[\log\dfrac{p_\theta(x,z)}{q_\phi(z\mid x)}\right]\;=\;\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{ELBO}}(\theta,\phi;x).$$

The right-hand side is the evidence lower bound (ELBO). Equality iff the ratio $p_\theta(x,z)/q_\phi(z\mid x)$ is constant in $z$ — equivalently, $q_\phi(z\mid x)=p_\theta(z\mid x)$. $\;\blacksquare$

Derivation 2 — the KL identity

Derivation via $D_{\mathrm{KL}}\ge 0$

Bayes: $p_\theta(z\mid x)=p_\theta(x,z)/p_\theta(x)$. Take logs and rearrange:

$$\log p_\theta(x)\;=\;\mathbb{E}_{q_\phi}\!\left[\log\dfrac{p_\theta(x,z)}{q_\phi(z\mid x)}\right]\;+\;\underbrace{\mathbb{E}_{q_\phi}\!\left[\log\dfrac{q_\phi(z\mid x)}{p_\theta(z\mid x)}\right]}_{D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_\phi\Vert p_\theta(\cdot\mid x))}.$$

Since $D_{\mathrm{KL}}\ge 0$, the first term is a lower bound:

$$\log p_\theta(x)\;=\;\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{ELBO}}(\theta,\phi;x)\;+\;D_{\mathrm{KL}}\bigl(q_\phi(\cdot\mid x)\,\Vert\,p_\theta(\cdot\mid x)\bigr).\;\blacksquare$$

Why the two derivations are the same statement

Both give the same inequality:

$$\log p_\theta(x)\;=\;\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{ELBO}}+D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_\phi\Vert p_\theta(\cdot\mid x))\;\ge\;\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{ELBO}}.$$

Different roads, same destination:

  • Jensen view: the bound comes from concavity. The gap is opaque.
  • KL view: the gap is named — it is exactly $D_{\mathrm{KL}}\bigl(q_\phi\Vert p_\theta(\cdot\mid x)\bigr)$. Closing the gap means matching the variational posterior to the true one.
03

Reconstruction $+$ KL Decomposition

What does the bound ask the model to do?

Standard ELBO decomposition

Algebra

Expand $p_\theta(x,z)=p(z)p_\theta(x\mid z)$ inside the ELBO:

$$\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{ELBO}}=\mathbb{E}_{q_\phi}\!\left[\log\dfrac{p(z)p_\theta(x\mid z)}{q_\phi(z\mid x)}\right]=\underbrace{\mathbb{E}_{q_\phi}[\log p_\theta(x\mid z)]}_{\text{reconstruction}}-\underbrace{D_{\mathrm{KL}}\bigl(q_\phi(\cdot\mid x)\,\Vert\,p(\cdot)\bigr)}_{\text{regularizer}}.$$

Two opposing pressures: reconstruct $x$ from latent samples, but keep the encoder close to the prior. $\;\blacksquare$

Interpretation: rate and distortion

Reconstruction term

$\mathbb{E}_{q_\phi}[\log p_\theta(x\mid z)]$: assign high likelihood to $x$ when $z\sim q_\phi(z\mid x)$.

Acts as a negative distortion: bigger when the decoder reproduces $x$ well from the encoded latent.

KL regularizer

$D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_\phi(z\mid x)\,\Vert\,p(z))$: keep the encoder close to the prior.

Acts as a rate: information leaked from $x$ into $z$. Pure rate–distortion structure.

The ELBO is a (variational) rate–distortion functional — this is the information-theoretic angle that justifies its use.

04

VAE: Parameterization and Reparameterization

Gaussian encoder, the trick that makes gradients flow

The VAE: Gaussian encoder, neural decoder

Encoder. A neural network outputs $\mu_\phi(x)$, $\sigma_\phi(x)$:

$$q_\phi(z\mid x)=\mathcal{N}\bigl(\mu_\phi(x),\,\operatorname{diag}(\sigma_\phi(x)^2)\bigr).$$

Prior. $p(z)=\mathcal{N}(0,I)$.

Decoder. Another network parameterizes $p_\theta(x\mid z)$ (Gaussian for continuous data, Bernoulli for binary, etc.).

For Gaussian $q_\phi$ vs. $\mathcal{N}(0,I)$ prior, the KL has a closed form:

$$D_{\mathrm{KL}}\bigl(q_\phi\,\Vert\,p\bigr)=\tfrac12\sum_{j}\bigl(\mu_{\phi,j}^2+\sigma_{\phi,j}^2-1-\log\sigma_{\phi,j}^2\bigr).$$

The gradient problem

Training maximizes the ELBO. Reconstruction term:

$$\mathbb{E}_{z\sim q_\phi(z\mid x)}[\log p_\theta(x\mid z)].$$

Issue. The expectation is over a distribution that depends on $\phi$. Naively, $\nabla_\phi\mathbb{E}_{q_\phi}[f(z)]\ne\mathbb{E}_{q_\phi}[\nabla_\phi f(z)]$ — we cannot just differentiate inside the expectation.

Workaround: reparameterization. Express the random sample as a deterministic function of a parameter-independent noise variable.

The reparameterization trick

For $z\sim\mathcal{N}(\mu_\phi(x),\sigma_\phi(x)^2)$, write

$$z\;=\;\mu_\phi(x)\;+\;\sigma_\phi(x)\odot\varepsilon,\qquad \varepsilon\sim\mathcal{N}(0,I).$$

Now $\varepsilon$ is parameter-free. The expectation becomes

$$\mathbb{E}_{z\sim q_\phi(z\mid x)}[f(z)]\;=\;\mathbb{E}_{\varepsilon\sim\mathcal{N}(0,I)}\bigl[f\bigl(\mu_\phi(x)+\sigma_\phi(x)\odot\varepsilon\bigr)\bigr].$$

The expectation no longer depends on $\phi$ — gradients pass through.

Why reparameterization works for gradients

Lemma

Let $g_\phi:\mathcal{E}\to\mathcal{Z}$ be a differentiable map with $z=g_\phi(\varepsilon)$ pushing $p_\varepsilon$ to $q_\phi$. Then for any $f$,

$$\nabla_\phi\,\mathbb{E}_{z\sim q_\phi}[f(z)]\;=\;\nabla_\phi\,\mathbb{E}_{\varepsilon\sim p_\varepsilon}[f(g_\phi(\varepsilon))]\;=\;\mathbb{E}_{\varepsilon\sim p_\varepsilon}[\nabla_\phi f(g_\phi(\varepsilon))].$$

The first equality is change of variables; the second exchanges $\nabla_\phi$ and $\mathbb{E}_\varepsilon$ legitimately because $p_\varepsilon$ does not depend on $\phi$. $\;\blacksquare$

Monte Carlo estimate: a single sample $\varepsilon\sim\mathcal{N}(0,I)$ gives an unbiased gradient.

Putting it together: VAE training

Per data point $x$:

  1. Encoder produces $\mu_\phi(x),\sigma_\phi(x)$ from $x$.
  2. Sample $\varepsilon\sim\mathcal{N}(0,I)$ and form $z=\mu_\phi(x)+\sigma_\phi(x)\odot\varepsilon$.
  3. Decoder produces $p_\theta(x\mid z)$.
  4. Compute the loss: $\;-\log p_\theta(x\mid z)+D_{\mathrm{KL}}\bigl(q_\phi(\cdot\mid x)\,\Vert\,p\bigr)$.
  5. Backprop through both networks.

Sampling at inference: $z\sim p(z)$, output $x=\mu_\theta(z)$ or sample $x\sim p_\theta(x\mid z)$.

Failure mode: posterior collapse

If the decoder is powerful, the model can drive $q_\phi(z\mid x)\to p(z)$ — the latent carries no information. The KL term is exactly zero, and the model becomes an unconditional density model.

$$D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_\phi\Vert p)=0\;\Longleftrightarrow\;q_\phi(z\mid x)=p(z)\;\Longrightarrow\;z\perp x.$$

Symptom of an over-flexible decoder. Mitigations: KL annealing, free bits, weaker decoders.

Looking ahead: diffusion models avoid this by using a fixed forward process — the encoder is not trained, and the latent is forced to carry information about $x$.

Recap of Lecture 1

  • Latent-variable model: $p_\theta(x)=\int p(z)p_\theta(x\mid z)dz$. The integral is intractable.
  • Variational bound (ELBO): introduce $q_\phi(z\mid x)$, derive $\log p_\theta(x)\ge\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{ELBO}}$.
  • Two derivations: Jensen on the importance-sampling identity, or KL non-negativity.
  • Decomposition: $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{ELBO}}=\mathbb{E}_q[\log p_\theta(x\mid z)]-D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q\,\Vert\,p)$ — reconstruction minus rate.
  • VAE: Gaussian encoder, neural decoder, reparameterization trick to get pathwise gradients.
  • Posterior collapse: a powerful decoder can ignore the latent. Diffusion sidesteps this with a fixed forward process.
