Entropy 2 — Companion Notes

Long-form derivations and asides for the deck.

Joint Entropy — All Equivalent Formulas

Three forms of $H(X,Y)$:

$$\begin{aligned} H(X,Y) &= -\sum_{x,y} p_{X,Y}(x,y)\log p_{X,Y}(x,y) \\ &= H(X) + H(Y\mid X) \\ &= H(Y) + H(X\mid Y). \end{aligned}$$

The bottom two are the chain rule. They are useful in opposite directions: when computing $H(X,Y)$ from a parametric model where $p_{Y\mid X}$ is simple (e.g. a noisy-channel model), use the second form; when one marginal is simple (e.g. a coin flip), use the third.

Two Correlated Coins — Full Numbers

From the joint table, $H(X,Y) = -\tfrac{1}{2}\log\tfrac{1}{2} - \tfrac{1}{4}\log\tfrac{1}{4} - \tfrac{1}{4}\log\tfrac{1}{4} = \tfrac{1}{2} + \tfrac{1}{2} + \tfrac{1}{2} = \tfrac{3}{2}$.

Marginals: $p_X(0) = 3/4$, $p_X(1) = 1/4$, so $H(X) = H_b(1/4) \approx 0.811$. Similarly $p_Y(0) = p_Y(1) = 1/2$, so $H(Y) = 1$.

$H(X\mid Y=0) = 0$ (only $X=0$ possible), $H(X\mid Y=1) = H_b(1/2) = 1$. Average: $H(X\mid Y) = \tfrac{1}{2}(0) + \tfrac{1}{2}(1) = 0.5$.

$H(Y\mid X=0) = H_b(1/3) \approx 0.918$ (since $\Pr(Y=0\mid X=0) = (1/2)/(3/4) = 2/3$), $H(Y\mid X=1) = 0$ (only $Y=1$). Average: $H(Y\mid X) = (3/4)(0.918) + (1/4)(0) \approx 0.689$.

Check chain rule: $H(X) + H(Y\mid X) = 0.811 + 0.689 = 1.500 = H(X,Y)$. $\checkmark$

Mutual information: $I(X;Y) = H(X) - H(X\mid Y) = 0.811 - 0.5 = 0.311$ bits. Or $H(Y) - H(Y\mid X) = 1 - 0.689 = 0.311$. $\checkmark$

$H(X\mid Y=y) > H(X)$ — Why It Can Happen

The "obvious" reading "knowing $Y$ never increases uncertainty about $X$" applies only on average. A particular outcome $y$ can be uninformative or even misleading: if $Y$ tells us nothing in some realizations and everything in others, the high-uncertainty realizations can exceed the unconditional entropy because they are reweighted by $p_Y(y)$ on the average.

In the slide example, $Y=0$ tells us only that $B=0$ (which we did not condition on); $X$ remains uniform over $\{1,2,3,4\}$, so $H(X\mid Y=0) = 2 = H(X)$. But $Y=k$ for $k\ne 0$ pins $X = k$, so $H(X\mid Y=k) = 0 < H(X)$.

Subadditivity — Independence Bound

Equality in $H(X^n) \le \sum_i H(X_i)$ requires equality at every step of the chain $H(X_i \mid X^{i-1}) = H(X_i)$, which by Gibbs requires $X_i \perp X^{i-1}$ for every $i$ — equivalent to mutual independence.

The general inequality $H(X^n) - \sum_i H(X_i) = -\sum_i I(X_i; X^{i-1})$ is sometimes called the "total correlation" or "multi-information" of $X^n$; it measures how far $X^n$ is from being mutually independent.

Mutual Information — Three Faces

Three equivalent definitions are commonly seen in the literature:

  1. Reduction in entropy: $I(X;Y) = H(X) - H(X\mid Y)$.
  2. KL between joint and product of marginals: $I(X;Y) = D(p_{X,Y}\,\|\,p_X p_Y)$.
  3. Donsker–Varadhan variational form: $I(X;Y) = \sup_T \mathbb{E}_{p_{XY}}[T] - \log\mathbb{E}_{p_X p_Y}[e^T]$.

The first is the operational reading; the second is invariant-looking and used for proofs; the third is the basis for neural mutual-information estimators (MINE, InfoNCE, etc.). See infotheory/mi/mi1-bounds.html for the variational story.

Erasure Channel Computation

The erasure channel is the canonical example for "a bit comes through cleanly or is lost entirely". The fact that $I(X;Y) = (1-\alpha)$ when $X\sim\mathrm{Bern}(1/2)$ generalizes: for any input $p_X$, we have $I(X;Y) = (1-\alpha)H(X)$, since on the non-erased fraction the output reveals $X$ exactly.

Hence the capacity of the binary erasure channel is $C = 1 - \alpha$ bits/use, achieved by $p_X = \mathrm{Bern}(1/2)$.

Conditional MI — XOR Example Detail

$X, Z\sim\mathrm{Bern}(1/2)$ independent, $Y = X\oplus Z$. The joint $(X,Y,Z)$:

$$\Pr(X=x, Z=z, Y=x\oplus z) = 1/4 \quad \forall (x,z)\in\{0,1\}^2.$$

Marginals: $X, Y, Z$ are each $\mathrm{Bern}(1/2)$.

$I(X;Z) = 0$ by construction. But $I(X;Z\mid Y)$ asks how much $Z$ tells us about $X$ once $Y$ is known. Given $Y = y$, $Z = X\oplus y$, so $Z$ is a deterministic function of $X$ — $H(X\mid Y, Z) = 0$ but $H(X\mid Y) = 1$. Hence $I(X;Z\mid Y) = 1$.

This is the ur-example showing that "conditioning can create dependence" — a phenomenon called collider bias in causal inference (Berkson's paradox).

DPI — Equality Condition

$I(X;Z) = I(X;Y)$ iff $I(X;Y\mid Z) = 0$ (from the proof). This means $X$ and $Y$ are conditionally independent given $Z$, i.e. $X\to Z\to Y$ is also a Markov chain.

Combined with the original $X\to Y\to Z$, this is exactly the condition that $Y$ is a sufficient statistic for the family $\{p_{Y\mid X}: X\in\mathcal{X}\}$ — observing $Z$ in addition to $Y$ adds nothing about $X$.

Cascade BSC — Why Crossover Composes

If $X\to Y\to Z$ via two BSCs with crossovers $p_1, p_2$, then $Z = X\oplus N_1 \oplus N_2$ with $N_1\sim\mathrm{Bern}(p_1)$, $N_2\sim\mathrm{Bern}(p_2)$ independent. The error $N_1\oplus N_2 \sim \mathrm{Bern}(p_1(1-p_2) + (1-p_1)p_2)$.

For $p_1 = p_2 = p$: combined crossover $2p(1-p)$. As $p$ grows, the cascade becomes less informative; at $p = 1/2$, $Z$ is independent of $X$ and the crossover is also $1/2$.

Fano — Both Forms

The tight form $H(X\mid Y) \le H_b(P_e) + P_e \log(M-1)$ is achievable. The looser form $P_e \ge (H(X\mid Y) - 1)/\log M$ comes from $H_b(P_e)\le 1$ and $\log(M-1)\le\log M$, then solving for $P_e$. The looser form is more often quoted because it is linear in $P_e$.

Channel Coding Converse Sketch

Standard converse for memoryless channel of capacity $C$: if rate $R = (\log M)/n > C$, then $P_e\not\to 0$ as $n\to\infty$.

From Fano: $P_e \ge 1 - (I(W; Y^n) + 1)/\log M$. By the chain rule and DPI, $I(W;Y^n) \le \sum_i I(W; Y_i \mid Y^{i-1}) \le \sum_i I(X_i; Y_i)$ (where $X_i$ is the $i$-th channel input chosen as a function of $W$); each summand is $\le C$, so $I(W;Y^n) \le nC$.

Hence $P_e \ge 1 - (nC+1)/(nR) = 1 - C/R - 1/(nR)\to 1 - C/R > 0$ as $n\to\infty$. The error is bounded away from zero whenever $R > C$.

Application — Statistical Estimation

Fano is the basis of minimax lower bounds in statistics. Suppose we want to estimate a parameter $\theta$ from data $D$ drawn from $P_\theta$. Pick $M$ well-separated parameter values $\theta_1,\dots,\theta_M$, set $\theta = \theta_W$ with $W\sim\mathrm{Unif}\{1,\dots,M\}$, and apply Fano to lower-bound the probability of confusing two values:

$$\inf_{\hat W} \Pr(\hat W \ne W) \ge 1 - \frac{I(W; D) + \log 2}{\log M}.$$

Bounding $I(W;D)$ by $\max_{i,j} D(P_{\theta_i}\,\|\,P_{\theta_j})$ (Yang–Barron / Tsybakov) yields lower bounds on the minimax estimation error.
