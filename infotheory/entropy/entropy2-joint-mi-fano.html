Joint and Conditional

Two random variables, one notion of uncertainty

Joint Entropy

Definition.

For $(X,Y)$ with joint pmf $p_{X,Y}$ on $\mathcal{X}\times\mathcal{Y}$,

$$H(X,Y) \;=\; \sum_{x,y} p_{X,Y}(x,y)\,\log\frac{1}{p_{X,Y}(x,y)} \;=\; \mathbb{E}\bigl[-\log p_{X,Y}(X,Y)\bigr].$$

Just the entropy of the pair $(X,Y)$ as a single random variable on $\mathcal{X}\times\mathcal{Y}$.

Example — Two Coin Flips

Independent fair coins:

$$p_{X,Y}(x,y) = 1/4 \text{ for all } (x,y)\in\{0,1\}^2.$$

$H(X,Y) = \log 4 = 2$ bits $= H(X) + H(Y)$.

Now consider $Y = X$ (perfect correlation). Joint pmf places $1/2$ on $(0,0)$ and $(1,1)$:

$$H(X,Y) \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2}\log 2 + \tfrac{1}{2}\log 2 \;=\; 1 \text{ bit}.$$

$H(X,Y) = 1 \ne 2 = H(X) + H(Y)$ — correlation reduces joint uncertainty.

Conditional Entropy — Two Definitions

Definition.

For each fixed $y$ with $p_Y(y) > 0$,

$$H(X\mid Y=y) \;=\; \sum_x p_{X\mid Y}(x\mid y)\,\log\frac{1}{p_{X\mid Y}(x\mid y)}.$$

Average over $y$:

$$H(X\mid Y) \;=\; \sum_y p_Y(y)\, H(X\mid Y=y) \;=\; \mathbb{E}\bigl[-\log p_{X\mid Y}(X\mid Y)\bigr].$$

Reading the Definition

  • $H(X\mid Y)$ is an average · it is not the entropy of $X$ on a particular outcome of $Y$.
  • $H(X\mid Y=y)$ can exceed $H(X)$ for some $y$, but $H(X\mid Y)\le H(X)$ on average.
  • $H(X\mid X) = 0$ · once we know $X$, no surprise remains.

Theorem — Chain Rule

Theorem.

$$H(X,Y) \;=\; H(Y) + H(X\mid Y) \;=\; H(X) + H(Y\mid X).$$

More generally, for $X^n = (X_1,\dots,X_n)$:

$$H(X^n) \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^n H(X_i \mid X_1, \dots, X_{i-1}).$$

Proof — Chain Rule

Use $p_{X,Y}(x,y) = p_Y(y)\,p_{X\mid Y}(x\mid y)$ inside the expectation:

$$\begin{aligned} H(X,Y) &= \mathbb{E}\bigl[-\log p_{X,Y}(X,Y)\bigr] \\ &= \mathbb{E}\bigl[-\log p_Y(Y)\bigr] + \mathbb{E}\bigl[-\log p_{X\mid Y}(X\mid Y)\bigr] \\ &= H(Y) + H(X\mid Y). \end{aligned}$$

The $n$-variable form follows by induction with $p(X^n) = \prod_i p(X_i\mid X^{i-1})$. $\square$

Example — Two Correlated Coins

$p_{X,Y}$ given as a $2\times 2$ table (rows $X$, columns $Y$):

$Y=0$$Y=1$row
$X=0$$1/2$$1/4$$3/4$
$X=1$$0$$1/4$$1/4$
col$1/2$$1/2$
  • $H(X) = H_b(1/4) \approx 0.811$ · $H(Y) = 1$
  • $H(X,Y) = -\sum p\log p = \tfrac{3}{2}$
  • $H(X\mid Y) = H(X,Y) - H(Y) = 0.5$ · $H(Y\mid X) \approx 0.689$

Theorem — Conditioning Reduces Entropy

Theorem.

$$H(X\mid Y) \;\le\; H(X),$$

with equality iff $X$ and $Y$ are independent.

"On average, knowing $Y$ never hurts."

Caveat: $H(X\mid Y=y)$ can exceed $H(X)$ for a particular $y$ — only the average is monotone.

Proof — Conditioning Reduces Entropy

Write the difference as a KL divergence:

$$\begin{aligned} H(X) - H(X\mid Y) &= \sum_{x,y} p_{X,Y}(x,y) \log\frac{p_{X\mid Y}(x\mid y)}{p_X(x)} \\ &= \sum_{x,y} p_{X,Y}(x,y) \log\frac{p_{X,Y}(x,y)}{p_X(x)\,p_Y(y)} \\ &= D\bigl(p_{X,Y} \,\|\, p_X p_Y\bigr) \;\ge\; 0, \end{aligned}$$

by Gibbs. Equality iff $p_{X,Y} = p_X p_Y$, i.e. $X \perp Y$. $\square$

$H(X\mid Y=y) > H(X)$ — Concrete Case

$X\sim\mathrm{Unif}\{1,2,3,4\}$ independent of $B\sim\mathrm{Bern}(1/2)$, and $Y = X\cdot B$.

  • $Y = 0$ when $B=0$ · $Y = X$ when $B = 1$.
  • $\Pr(Y=0) = 1/2$ · $\Pr(Y=k) = 1/8$ for $k \in \{1,2,3,4\}$.

$H(X\mid Y=0) = H(X) = 2$ (no info), but $H(X\mid Y=k) = 0$ for $k\ne 0$.

$$H(X\mid Y) \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2}\cdot 2 + \tfrac{1}{2}\cdot 0 \;=\; 1 \;<\; 2 \;=\; H(X). \;\checkmark$$

Functional Dependence

Lemma.

$H(X\mid Y) = 0$ if and only if $X = g(Y)$ a.s. for some function $g$.

Proof. $H(X\mid Y) = \sum_y p_Y(y) H(X\mid Y=y) = 0$ requires $H(X\mid Y=y) = 0$ for every $y$ in the support, which forces $p_{X\mid Y}(\cdot\mid y)$ to be a point mass — that is, $X$ is a deterministic function of $Y$. $\square$

Conversely, if $X = g(Y)$ then $p_{X\mid Y}(g(y)\mid y) = 1$, so each conditional entropy vanishes.

Subadditivity — A Useful Bound

From the chain rule and "conditioning reduces entropy":

$$H(X^n) \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^n H(X_i \mid X^{i-1}) \;\le\; \sum_{i=1}^n H(X_i).$$

Equality iff $X_1, \dots, X_n$ are mutually independent.

Independence is the entropy-maximizing joint structure.

Mutual Information

Shared bits between $X$ and $Y$

Definition — Mutual Information

Definition.

$$I(X;Y) \;=\; H(X) - H(X\mid Y) \;=\; H(Y) - H(Y\mid X).$$

Equivalently, as a KL divergence:

$$I(X;Y) \;=\; D\bigl(p_{X,Y} \,\|\, p_X p_Y\bigr) \;=\; \sum_{x,y} p_{X,Y}(x,y)\,\log\frac{p_{X,Y}(x,y)}{p_X(x)\,p_Y(y)}.$$

Reading the Definition

  • "Reduction in uncertainty about $X$ from observing $Y$."
  • Symmetric: $I(X;Y) = I(Y;X)$.
  • Non-negative: $I(X;Y) = D(p_{X,Y}\,\|\,p_Xp_Y) \ge 0$ by Gibbs.
  • Self-information: $I(X;X) = H(X) - 0 = H(X)$.
$I(X;Y) = 0 \iff X \perp Y.$

Venn Picture

Identities visible at a glance:

  • $H(X,Y) = H(X) + H(Y) - I(X;Y)$
  • $H(X) = H(X\mid Y) + I(X;Y)$
  • $H(Y) = H(Y\mid X) + I(X;Y)$

Example — Bernoulli Pair

$X\sim\mathrm{Bern}(1/2)$ and $Y = X \oplus N$ with $N\sim\mathrm{Bern}(p)$, $X\perp N$.

This is the binary symmetric channel (BSC) with crossover $p$.

  • $H(X) = 1$ · $H(Y) = 1$ (output also $\mathrm{Bern}(1/2)$)
  • $H(Y\mid X) = H_b(p)$ · given $X$, $Y$ flips with prob $p$
$$I(X;Y) \;=\; H(Y) - H(Y\mid X) \;=\; 1 - H_b(p).$$

$p=1/2$: $I = 0$ (channel destroys info). $\;p \to 0$: $I\to 1$ (perfect channel).

Example — Erasure Channel

$X\sim\mathrm{Bern}(1/2)$. Output $Y = X$ with prob $1-\alpha$, $Y = e$ (erased) with prob $\alpha$.

Joint pmf places mass $(1-\alpha)/2$ on $(0,0)$ and $(1,1)$, and $\alpha/2$ on $(0,e)$ and $(1,e)$.

$$\begin{aligned} H(X\mid Y=0) &= 0,\quad H(X\mid Y=1) = 0,\quad H(X\mid Y=e) = 1. \end{aligned}$$
$$I(X;Y) \;=\; H(X) - H(X\mid Y) \;=\; 1 - \alpha.$$

Each erasure costs exactly one bit on average.

Bounds on Mutual Information

Theorem.

$$0 \;\le\; I(X;Y) \;\le\; \min\bigl\{H(X),\; H(Y)\bigr\}.$$

Lower bound: Gibbs. Upper bound: $I(X;Y) = H(X) - H(X\mid Y) \le H(X)$ since $H(X\mid Y)\ge 0$, similarly bounded by $H(Y)$.

Equality $I(X;Y) = H(X)$ iff $X = g(Y)$ a.s. (i.e. $H(X\mid Y) = 0$).

Theorem — Chain Rule for MI

Theorem.

$$I(X^n;Y) \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^n I(X_i; Y \mid X^{i-1}).$$

Proof. Apply the chain rule for entropy to both $H(X^n)$ and $H(X^n\mid Y)$:

$$\begin{aligned} I(X^n;Y) &= H(X^n) - H(X^n\mid Y) \\ &= \textstyle\sum_i \bigl[H(X_i\mid X^{i-1}) - H(X_i\mid X^{i-1}, Y)\bigr] \\ &= \textstyle\sum_i I(X_i; Y\mid X^{i-1}). \quad\square \end{aligned}$$
03

Conditional MI & DPI

Markov chains, the data processing inequality

Conditional Mutual Information

Definition.

$$I(X;Y\mid Z) \;=\; H(X\mid Z) - H(X\mid Y, Z).$$

Equivalently,

$$I(X;Y\mid Z) \;=\; \mathbb{E}_{Z}\bigl[\,D(p_{X,Y\mid Z=z} \,\|\, p_{X\mid Z=z}\,p_{Y\mid Z=z})\bigr] \;\ge\; 0.$$

Average reduction in uncertainty about $X$ given $Y$, averaged over a known $Z$.

Beware — Conditioning Can Increase MI

Unlike entropy ($H(X\mid Y)\le H(X)$), conditioning can increase mutual information:

Let $X, Z\sim\mathrm{Bern}(1/2)$ independent,  $Y = X\oplus Z$.

  • $X\perp Z$, so $I(X;Z) = 0$.
  • Given $Y$, $X$ determines $Z$: $Z = X\oplus Y$, hence $H(Z\mid X,Y) = 0$.
  • $H(Z\mid Y) = H(Z) = 1$ (still uniform).
$$I(X;Z\mid Y) \;=\; H(Z\mid Y) - H(Z\mid X,Y) \;=\; 1 - 0 \;=\; 1 \text{ bit}.$$

Markov Chains

Definition.

$X \to Y \to Z$ is a Markov chain if

$$p_{Z\mid X,Y}(z\mid x, y) \;=\; p_{Z\mid Y}(z\mid y),$$

i.e. given $Y$, $Z$ is conditionally independent of $X$.

  • $X \to Y \to Z \;\Leftrightarrow\; Z \to Y \to X$ (Markov is symmetric).
  • $Z = g(Y)$ implies $X\to Y \to Z$ for any $X$.
  • $X\to Y\to Z \;\Rightarrow\; I(X;Z\mid Y) = 0$.

Theorem — Data Processing Inequality

Theorem (DPI).

If $X\to Y\to Z$ is a Markov chain, then

$$I(X;Z) \;\le\; I(X;Y).$$

Equality iff $X \to Z \to Y$ is also Markov (i.e., $Y$ is a sufficient statistic for $X$ from $Z$).

"Processing data cannot create information about $X$."

Proof — DPI

Two-step expansion of $I(X;Y,Z)$:

$$\begin{aligned} I(X;Y,Z) &= I(X;Z) + I(X;Y\mid Z) \\ &= I(X;Y) + I(X;Z\mid Y). \end{aligned}$$

By the Markov assumption $I(X;Z\mid Y) = 0$, hence

$$I(X;Z) + I(X;Y\mid Z) \;=\; I(X;Y).$$

Since $I(X;Y\mid Z) \ge 0$, we get $I(X;Z)\le I(X;Y)$. $\square$

Corollary — Function of $Y$

If $Z = g(Y)$, then $X\to Y\to Z$ holds for any $X$, hence

$$I(X; g(Y)) \;\le\; I(X; Y).$$

Examples:

  • $Y$: full image · $g(Y)$: 8× downsample · less info about labels.
  • $Y$: noisy measurement · $g(Y)$: any post-processed estimate · bounded by $I(X;Y)$.
  • $Y$: gradient on full data · $g(Y)$: clipped + noised gradient (DP-SGD) · lower MI by design.

Example — Cascade of BSCs

$X\to Y\to Z$: each transition is a BSC with crossover $p$. Combined channel $X\to Z$ has crossover $p' = 2p(1-p)$.

$X\sim\mathrm{Bern}(1/2)$ ·

  • $I(X;Y) = 1 - H_b(p)$
  • $I(X;Z) = 1 - H_b(2p(1-p))$

For $p = 0.1$: $I(X;Y) \approx 0.531$, $I(X;Z) \approx 1 - H_b(0.18) \approx 0.320$.

$0.320 \le 0.531$ · DPI confirmed.

Fano's Inequality

Lower bounds on the probability of error

Setup — Inferring $X$ from $Y$

  • $X \in \mathcal{X}$ is the unknown · $|\mathcal{X}| = M$.
  • $Y$ is observed · $X\to Y\to \widehat X$ where $\widehat X = g(Y)$ is an estimate.
  • Error event: $E = \mathbb{1}\{\widehat X \ne X\}$, with $P_e = \Pr(E = 1)$.

Question: how small can $P_e$ be, given how much info $Y$ carries about $X$?

Theorem — Fano

Theorem (Fano, 1961).

For any estimator $\widehat X = g(Y)$ with error probability $P_e$,

$$H(X\mid Y) \;\le\; H_b(P_e) + P_e \log(|\mathcal{X}| - 1).$$

Looser but cleaner form:

$$P_e \;\ge\; \frac{H(X\mid Y) - 1}{\log|\mathcal{X}|}.$$
Fano, Transmission of Information, MIT Press 1961.

Proof — Fano (Step 1)

Define error indicator $E = \mathbb{1}\{\widehat X \ne X\}$. Apply chain rule two ways:

$$\begin{aligned} H(E, X \mid \widehat X) &= H(X\mid \widehat X) + \underbrace{H(E\mid X, \widehat X)}_{=\,0} \\ &= H(E\mid \widehat X) + H(X\mid E, \widehat X). \end{aligned}$$

$E$ is a function of $(X, \widehat X)$, so $H(E\mid X, \widehat X) = 0$. Hence

$$H(X\mid \widehat X) \;=\; H(E\mid\widehat X) + H(X\mid E, \widehat X).$$

Proof — Fano (Step 2)

Bound each term:

$$\begin{aligned} H(E\mid\widehat X) &\;\le\; H(E) \;=\; H_b(P_e) \\ H(X\mid E, \widehat X) &= \Pr(E=0)\cdot 0 + \Pr(E=1)\cdot H(X\mid E=1, \widehat X) \\ &\;\le\; P_e \cdot \log(|\mathcal{X}| - 1). \end{aligned}$$

(When $E=0$, $X = \widehat X$ a.s., zero entropy. When $E=1$, $X$ ranges over $\mathcal{X}\setminus\{\widehat X\}$ · at most $|\mathcal{X}|-1$ outcomes.)

Then $H(X\mid Y)\le H(X\mid\widehat X)$ by DPI on $X\to Y\to\widehat X$. Combine. $\square$

Application — Channel Coding Converse

Send message $W\sim\mathrm{Unif}\{1,\dots,M\}$ over channel, decoder outputs $\widehat W = g(Y^n)$.

  • $H(W) = \log M$.
  • By Fano: $H(W\mid Y^n) \le 1 + P_e\log M$.
  • $I(W; Y^n) = H(W) - H(W\mid Y^n) \ge \log M - 1 - P_e\log M$.
$$P_e \;\ge\; 1 - \frac{I(W;Y^n) + 1}{\log M}.$$

If $\log M$ outpaces $I(W;Y^n)$, error is bounded away from zero.

Example — Binary Estimation

$X\sim\mathrm{Bern}(1/2)$, $Y$ noisy with $I(X;Y) = 0.3$ bits.

  • $H(X\mid Y) = H(X) - I(X;Y) = 0.7$.
  • Fano: $0.7 \le H_b(P_e)$ (since $|\mathcal{X}|-1 = 1$, $\log(|\mathcal{X}|-1) = 0$).
  • Solve $H_b(P_e) \ge 0.7$ · $P_e \ge 0.189$.

No estimator can drive error below $\approx 18.9\%$ on this channel.

Example — $M$-ary Hypothesis Testing

Distinguish among $M = 1024$ hypotheses with $H(X\mid Y) = 4$ bits.

Loose Fano:

$$P_e \;\ge\; \frac{H(X\mid Y) - 1}{\log M} \;=\; \frac{4 - 1}{10} \;=\; 0.3.$$

At least 30% of estimates must be wrong, regardless of the algorithm.

Fano gives an unconditional lower bound — no assumption on the estimator.

Equality in Fano

Fano is tight. Construct a $Y$ such that:

  • With prob $1-P_e$: $Y$ reveals $X$ exactly (so $\widehat X = X$).
  • With prob $P_e$: $Y$ leaves $X$ uniformly distributed on $\mathcal{X}\setminus\{\widehat X\}$.

Then $H(X\mid Y) = H_b(P_e) + P_e\log(M-1)$ exactly. Both terms in Fano are individually saturated.

Recap — The Whole Identity Web

$$\begin{aligned} H(X,Y) &= H(X) + H(Y\mid X) \;=\; H(Y) + H(X\mid Y) \\ I(X;Y) &= H(X) - H(X\mid Y) \;=\; H(X) + H(Y) - H(X,Y) \\ I(X;Y) &= D(p_{X,Y}\,\|\,p_X p_Y) \end{aligned}$$
  • Conditioning reduces entropy · $I(X;Y) \ge 0$
  • DPI: $X\to Y\to Z \Rightarrow I(X;Z) \le I(X;Y)$
  • Fano: $H(X\mid Y)\le H_b(P_e) + P_e\log(M-1)$
