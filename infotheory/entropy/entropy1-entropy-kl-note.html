Long-form derivations and asides for the deck. Section headers match slide titles.

What Should "Information" Mean?

Khinchin's axiomatic characterization (1957) makes the three desiderata precise. He asks for a function $H(p_1,\dots,p_n)$ that is:

  1. continuous in each $p_i$;
  2. maximized at the uniform distribution $(1/n,\dots,1/n)$;
  3. invariant under appending a zero-probability outcome (extensible);
  4. satisfies the grouping axiom $H(p_1,\dots,p_n) = H(p_1+p_2, p_3,\dots,p_n) + (p_1+p_2) H\!\left(\tfrac{p_1}{p_1+p_2}, \tfrac{p_2}{p_1+p_2}\right)$.

The Shannon entropy is the unique function (up to a multiplicative constant) satisfying these axioms. The base of the logarithm fixes the constant: bits ($\log_2$), nats ($\ln$), or dits ($\log_{10}$).

Self-Information

The function $i(E) = -\log P(E)$ is sometimes called the information content or surprisal of event $E$. The choice of $-\log p$ (rather than, say, $1 - p$) is forced by the additivity requirement: any continuous function $\varphi:(0,1]\to\mathbb{R}_{\ge 0}$ satisfying $\varphi(p_1 p_2) = \varphi(p_1) + \varphi(p_2)$, $\varphi(1)=0$, must be of the form $-c\log p$ for some $c > 0$.

Definition — Entropy

The convention $0 \log 0 = 0$ is justified by continuity: $\lim_{p\to 0^+} p\log(1/p) = 0$ (de l'Hôpital, or just $p\log(1/p) = -p\ln p / \ln 2$). The expectation form $H(X) = \mathbb{E}[-\log p_X(X)]$ then makes sense even for distributions with countable support, provided the sum converges.

For countable $\mathcal{X}$, $H$ may be infinite; the geometric distribution example shows it can also be finite even on countable support.

Example — Geometric($p$)

Verifying the identity $\sum_{k\ge 1} (k-1)(1-p)^{k-1} p = (1-p)/p$. Let $S = \sum_{k\ge 1}(k-1)(1-p)^{k-1}p$. Note $S$ is the mean of $X-1$ where $X\sim\mathrm{Geom}(p)$, so $S = \mathbb{E}[X] - 1 = 1/p - 1 = (1-p)/p$. Done.

Alternative direct computation: differentiate $\sum_{k\ge 0} q^k = 1/(1-q)$ with $q = 1-p$ to get $\sum_{k\ge 1} k q^{k-1} = 1/(1-q)^2 = 1/p^2$. Then $\sum_{k\ge 1} (k-1) q^{k-1} = 1/p^2 - 1/p$. Multiply by $p$ to recover $1/p - 1$.

Why $\log$? — Additivity

This is sometimes called the Cauchy functional equation in disguise. The additivity requirement plus measurability fixes $-\log$ uniquely up to base. Without measurability, pathological solutions exist (Hamel-basis monstrosities), but they are nowhere measurable.

Concavity — Direct Second-Derivative Argument

$H(p)$ is a function on the probability simplex $\Delta_n = \{p\in\mathbb{R}_{\ge 0}^n : \sum p_i = 1\}$. Treating it as a function of the unconstrained $p_1,\dots,p_n > 0$ with $\sum p_i = 1$ as a constraint, the Hessian on the tangent space is

$$\partial_i \partial_j (-p_i\log p_i) = -\frac{\delta_{ij}}{p_i\ln 2}.$$

This is negative-definite, so $H$ is strictly concave on the open simplex. The "conditioning reduces entropy" route used on the slide is shorter; the second-derivative route is more direct but uses calculus on the simplex.

Worked Values for $H_b$

Computation of $H_b(1/4)$:

$$H_b(\tfrac{1}{4}) = \tfrac{1}{4}\log 4 + \tfrac{3}{4}\log\tfrac{4}{3} = \tfrac{1}{2} + \tfrac{3}{4}(\log 4 - \log 3) = \tfrac{1}{2} + \tfrac{3}{2} - \tfrac{3}{4}\log 3 = 2 - \tfrac{3}{4}\log 3.$$

Using $\log_2 3 \approx 1.585$, $H_b(1/4) \approx 2 - 1.189 = 0.811$.

Small-$p$ asymptotic: $H_b(p) \approx p \log(1/p) + p\log e + O(p^2)$ for small $p$, since $-(1-p)\log(1-p) = (1-p)\sum_{k\ge 1} p^k/(k\ln 2) = p/\ln 2 - p^2/(2\ln 2) - \cdots$

Recursive Identity for Entropy

The "splitting" identity

$$H(p_1,\dots,p_n) = H_b(p_1) + (1-p_1)\,H\!\left(\tfrac{p_2}{1-p_1}, \dots, \tfrac{p_n}{1-p_1}\right)$$

follows from the chain rule: introduce the binary indicator $E = \mathbb{1}\{X = x_1\}$. Then $H(X) = H(E) + H(X\mid E) = H_b(p_1) + (1-p_1)\,H(p_2/(1-p_1),\dots,p_n/(1-p_1))$.

This identity gives a quick recursive algorithm to compute $H$ for any pmf.

KL Divergence — Asymmetry

The asymmetry of $D(p\,\|\,q)$ has operational consequences. In coding (Lecture: Lossless 1), $D(p\,\|\,q)$ is the excess rate when the true distribution is $p$ but the code was designed for $q$. The reverse $D(q\,\|\,p)$ is the excess rate in the dual problem (true $q$, code for $p$).

In hypothesis testing, $D(p\,\|\,q)$ is the Stein exponent: the asymptotic rate at which type-II error decays under the null $p$ when type-I error is fixed at any level $\alpha\in(0,1)$.

Proof — Gibbs via Jensen

The proof on the slide uses Jensen on $\log$ (concave). An equivalent route uses the elementary inequality $\ln t \le t - 1$ for $t > 0$, with equality iff $t = 1$:

$$-D(p\,\|\,q)\ln 2 = \sum_x p(x)\ln\frac{q(x)}{p(x)} \le \sum_x p(x)\!\left(\frac{q(x)}{p(x)} - 1\right) = \sum_x q(x) - \sum_x p(x) = 0.$$

Equality iff $q(x)/p(x) = 1$ on the support of $p$.

Mismatch Cost — Cross-Entropy

The decomposition $\mathbb{E}_p[\ell(X)] = D(p\,\|\,q) + H(p)$ uses ideal codeword lengths $\ell(x) = -\log q(x)$. In practice, codeword lengths are integers, giving an additional $\le 1$ bit-per-symbol overhead (Shannon code). Arithmetic coding asymptotically achieves $\mathbb{E}_p[\ell(X^n)/n] \to H(p) + D(p\,\|\,q)$, eliminating the integer overhead per block.

Log-Sum — Why Useful

The log-sum inequality is the workhorse for proving:

  • Joint convexity of $D(p\,\|\,q)$.
  • Concavity of $H(p)$ in $p$.
  • Convexity of $I(X;Y) = D(p_{X,Y}\,\|\,p_X p_Y)$ in $p_{Y\mid X}$ for fixed $p_X$, and concavity in $p_X$ for fixed $p_{Y\mid X}$.
  • Channel capacity is achieved by a unique $p_X^\star$ (when the channel input is continuously parameterized).

Pinsker's Inequality — Proof Sketch

For Bernoullis, the inequality reduces to verifying $H_b(p) - H_b(p+\Delta) \ge (\ln 2) \cdot 2\Delta^2$ for $|\Delta|$ small enough — equivalently $-H_b''(p)\ge 4/\ln 2$ which fails (the actual proof is more subtle). The standard Csiszár proof reduces to Bernoullis via the data-processing inequality applied to the indicator of any subset $S\subseteq\mathcal{X}$:

$$D(p\,\|\,q) \ge D\bigl(\mathrm{Bern}(p(S))\,\|\,\mathrm{Bern}(q(S))\bigr) \ge 2(\ln 2)\bigl(p(S) - q(S)\bigr)^2.$$

Choosing $S = \{x : p(x) \ge q(x)\}$ gives $|p(S) - q(S)| = \|p-q\|_{\mathrm{TV}}$.
