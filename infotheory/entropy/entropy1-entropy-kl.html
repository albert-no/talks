01

Entropy

From surprise to expected surprise

What Should "Information" Mean?

Three intuitive desiderata for an information measure of an event $E$ with probability $p$:

  • Rare events carry more information · smaller $p$ $\Rightarrow$ more bits.
  • Certain events carry zero · $p=1 \Rightarrow$ $0$ bits.
  • Independent events add · if $E_1\perp E_2$ then $i(E_1\cap E_2) = i(E_1)+i(E_2)$.
Only $i(E) = -\log P(E)$ (up to a base) satisfies all three.

Self-Information

For an event $\{X = x\}$ with probability $p_X(x)$, define

$$i_X(x) \;=\; -\log p_X(x) \;=\; \log\frac{1}{p_X(x)}.$$

Units depend on the base:

  • $\log_2$ · bits
  • $\log_e = \ln$ · nats  ($1$ nat $= 1/\ln 2 \approx 1.443$ bits)
  • $\log_{10}$ · dits

Throughout, $\log = \log_2$ unless stated.

Definition — Entropy

Definition (Shannon, 1948).

Let $X$ take values in a finite or countable alphabet $\mathcal{X}$ with pmf $p_X$. The entropy of $X$ is the expected self-information:

$$H(X) \;=\; \mathbb{E}\bigl[-\log p_X(X)\bigr] \;=\; \sum_{x\in\mathcal{X}} p_X(x)\,\log\frac{1}{p_X(x)}.$$

By convention $0\,\log(1/0) = 0$ (continuity: $\lim_{p\to 0^+} p\log(1/p) = 0$).

Shannon, "A Mathematical Theory of Communication", Bell System Technical Journal 1948.

Reading the Definition

  • $H(X)$ depends only on the pmf · not the labels of $\mathcal{X}$.
  • $H(X) = \mathbb{E}[i_X(X)]$ · average bits of surprise per draw.
  • Operational meaning (preview): the minimum bits per symbol needed to losslessly compress an i.i.d. source $X_1, X_2, \dots$ approaches $H(X)$.
Entropy is the unique measure of average surprise consistent with the three desiderata.

Example — Bernoulli($p$)

$X \in \{0,1\}$ with $\Pr(X=1) = p$, $\Pr(X=0) = 1-p$.

$$H(X) \;=\; p\log\frac{1}{p} + (1-p)\log\frac{1}{1-p} \;\eqqcolon\; H_b(p).$$
$p$$0$$0.1$$0.25$$0.5$$0.9$$1$
$H_b(p)$$0$$0.469$$0.811$$1$$0.469$$0$

Symmetric: $H_b(p) = H_b(1-p)$. Maximum $1$ bit at $p=1/2$.

Example — Uniform on $n$ Symbols

$X \sim \mathrm{Unif}(\{1,\dots,n\})$, so $p_X(x) = 1/n$ for all $x$.

$$H(X) \;=\; \sum_{x=1}^n \frac{1}{n}\log n \;=\; \log n.$$
  • $n=2$ · $H = 1$ bit (fair coin)
  • $n=6$ · $H = \log_2 6 \approx 2.585$ bits (fair die)
  • $n=52$ · $H = \log_2 52 \approx 5.700$ bits (random card)

Example — Geometric($p$)

$X \in \{1, 2, 3, \dots\}$ with $\Pr(X=k) = (1-p)^{k-1} p$. Mean: $\mathbb{E}[X] = 1/p$.

$$\begin{aligned} H(X) &= -\sum_{k=1}^\infty (1-p)^{k-1}p\,\bigl[\log p + (k-1)\log(1-p)\bigr] \\ &= -\log p - \log(1-p)\,\frac{1-p}{p} \;=\; \frac{H_b(p)}{p}. \end{aligned}$$

Used the identity $\sum_{k\ge 1} (k-1)(1-p)^{k-1}p = (1-p)/p$.

$p = 1/2$ · $H = 2$ bits.  $p \to 0$ · $H \to \infty$ (heavy tail).

Example — Three-Symbol Source

$\mathcal{X} = \{a, b, c\}$ with $p_X(a) = 1/2$, $p_X(b) = 1/4$, $p_X(c) = 1/4$.

$$H(X) \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2}\cdot 1 + \tfrac{1}{4}\cdot 2 + \tfrac{1}{4}\cdot 2 \;=\; 1.5 \text{ bits}.$$

Compare with $\log_2 3 \approx 1.585$ bits (uniform). Non-uniform $\Rightarrow$ less surprise on average.

Exercise. Verify that the four-symbol pmf $(1/2, 1/4, 1/8, 1/8)$ has $H = 1.75$ bits.

Example — Dyadic vs Nearby

Tiny perturbations of dyadic probabilities barely change $H$:

$p_X$$H(X)$
$(1/2, 1/4, 1/4)$$1.500$
$(0.50, 0.30, 0.20)$$1.485$
$(0.40, 0.35, 0.25)$$1.559$
$(1/3, 1/3, 1/3)$$1.585$

Entropy is continuous in the pmf — a small change in $p_X$ gives a small change in $H$.

Why $\log$? — Additivity for Independence

If $X \perp Y$, then $p_{X,Y}(x,y) = p_X(x)\,p_Y(y)$, so

$$\begin{aligned} H(X,Y) &= \mathbb{E}\bigl[-\log p_X(X) p_Y(Y)\bigr] \\ &= \mathbb{E}[-\log p_X(X)] + \mathbb{E}[-\log p_Y(Y)] \;=\; H(X) + H(Y). \end{aligned}$$
Independent draws add their information. The logarithm is the only measurable function turning products into sums.
02

Basic Properties

Bounds, concavity, maximum at uniform

Theorem — Non-Negativity

Theorem.

For any pmf $p_X$ on a discrete alphabet,

$$0 \;\le\; H(X) \;\le\; \log|\mathcal{X}|.$$

The lower bound is attained iff $X$ is deterministic. The upper bound is attained iff $X$ is uniform on $\mathcal{X}$.

Proof — $H(X) \ge 0$

Each $p_X(x) \in [0,1]$, hence $-\log p_X(x) \ge 0$. The sum of non-negative numbers is non-negative:

$$H(X) \;=\; \sum_{x} p_X(x)\,\log\frac{1}{p_X(x)} \;\ge\; 0.$$

Equality requires each term to vanish, i.e. $p_X(x)\log(1/p_X(x)) = 0$ for all $x$.

This forces $p_X(x) \in \{0,1\}$ — one symbol has all the mass. $\square$

Recall — Jensen's Inequality

Lemma (Jensen).

If $\varphi: I \to \mathbb{R}$ is convex on the interval $I$ and $X$ is a random variable on $I$, then

$$\varphi\bigl(\mathbb{E}[X]\bigr) \;\le\; \mathbb{E}[\varphi(X)].$$

Reverse inequality if $\varphi$ is concave. Equality iff $X$ is constant a.s. or $\varphi$ is affine on the support.

$\log$ is concave on $(0,\infty)$ · $-\log$ is convex.

Theorem — $H(X) \le \log|\mathcal{X}|$

Apply Jensen's inequality with the concave function $\log(\cdot)$:

$$\begin{aligned} H(X) &= \sum_{x} p_X(x)\,\log\frac{1}{p_X(x)} \\ &\;\stackrel{\text{Jensen}}{\le}\; \log\!\left(\sum_x p_X(x) \cdot \frac{1}{p_X(x)}\right) \\ &= \log|\mathcal{X}|. \end{aligned}$$

Equality iff $1/p_X(x)$ is constant on the support — i.e. $p_X$ is uniform. $\square$

Concavity of Entropy

Theorem.

$H(p)$ is a concave function of the pmf $p$. For pmfs $p, q$ on $\mathcal{X}$ and $\lambda \in [0,1]$,

$$H\bigl(\lambda p + (1-\lambda) q\bigr) \;\ge\; \lambda H(p) + (1-\lambda) H(q).$$

Mixing two distributions increases uncertainty.

Proof — Concavity

Let $T \sim \mathrm{Bern}(\lambda)$, and define $X \mid T=1 \sim p$, $X \mid T=0 \sim q$. The marginal pmf of $X$ is $\lambda p + (1-\lambda) q$.

From "conditioning reduces entropy" (Lecture 2), for any pair $(X,T)$:

$$H(X) \;\ge\; H(X\mid T) \;=\; \lambda H(X\mid T=1) + (1-\lambda) H(X\mid T=0).$$

Substitute $H(X\mid T=1) = H(p)$, $H(X\mid T=0) = H(q)$. $\square$

(A direct second-derivative argument also works — see notes.)

Example — Mixing Two Coins

$p = \mathrm{Bern}(0.1)$ · $q = \mathrm{Bern}(0.9)$. Mix $\lambda = 1/2$:

$$\tfrac{1}{2}p + \tfrac{1}{2} q \;=\; \mathrm{Bern}(0.5).$$
  • $H(p) = H_b(0.1) \approx 0.469$ bits
  • $H(q) = H_b(0.9) \approx 0.469$ bits
  • $H(\tfrac{1}{2}p+\tfrac{1}{2}q) = H_b(0.5) = 1$ bit

$1 \;\ge\; \tfrac{1}{2}(0.469) + \tfrac{1}{2}(0.469) = 0.469$. $\;\checkmark$

03

Binary Entropy

$H_b(p)$ as the building block

Binary Entropy Function

$$H_b(p) \;=\; -p\log p - (1-p)\log(1-p), \qquad p \in [0,1].$$
  • $H_b(0) = H_b(1) = 0$
  • Symmetric: $H_b(p) = H_b(1-p)$
  • Concave on $[0,1]$
  • Max at $p=1/2$ · $H_b(1/2) = 1$

Concavity from $H_b''(p)$

Differentiate $H_b(p) = -p\log p - (1-p)\log(1-p)$ twice:

$$H_b'(p) \;=\; \log\frac{1-p}{p}, \qquad H_b''(p) \;=\; -\frac{1}{p(1-p)\ln 2}.$$

$H_b''(p) < 0$ on $(0,1)$ · $H_b$ strictly concave.

$H_b'(1/2) = 0$ confirms the maximum at $p = 1/2$.

Worked Values

By hand for selected $p$:

$$\begin{aligned} H_b(1/4) &= \tfrac{1}{4}\log 4 + \tfrac{3}{4}\log\tfrac{4}{3} \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2} + \tfrac{3}{4}(2 - \log 3) \\ &= 2 - \tfrac{3}{4}\log 3 \;\approx\; 0.811. \end{aligned}$$

Useful approximations for small $p$:

$$H_b(p) \;\approx\; p\log\frac{e}{p} \quad (p\to 0).$$

$H_b(0.01)\approx 0.0808$ · $H_b(0.001) \approx 0.01141$.

Aside — Binary $\to$ General

Any pmf $p$ on $\{1,\dots,n\}$ can be built by repeated "split" operations:

$$H(p_1,\dots,p_n) \;=\; H_b(p_1) + (1-p_1)\,H\!\left(\frac{p_2}{1-p_1},\dots,\frac{p_n}{1-p_1}\right).$$

Recursive identity · $H_b$ is the atom from which entropy is built.

Verify: $H(1/2, 1/4, 1/4) = H_b(1/2) + \tfrac{1}{2}\,H_b(1/2) = 1 + 1/2 = 1.5$. $\;\checkmark$

04

KL Divergence

Distance between distributions

Motivation

How different are two distributions $p$ and $q$ on the same alphabet?

  • $p$ · the true source
  • $q$ · a model we use to encode

Want a measure that:

  • Vanishes iff $p = q$
  • Is non-negative
  • Has an operational meaning — e.g. excess bits per symbol

Definition — KL Divergence

Definition.

For pmfs $p, q$ on the same alphabet $\mathcal{X}$, the Kullback–Leibler divergence of $p$ from $q$ is

$$D(p\,\|\,q) \;=\; \sum_{x\in\mathcal{X}} p(x)\log\frac{p(x)}{q(x)} \;=\; \mathbb{E}_p\!\left[\log\frac{p(X)}{q(X)}\right].$$

Conventions: $0\log\tfrac{0}{q} = 0$,  $p\log\tfrac{p}{0} = +\infty$ if $p>0$.

Kullback and Leibler, "On Information and Sufficiency", Ann. Math. Stat. 1951.

Reading the Definition

  • Asymmetric.  $D(p\,\|\,q) \ne D(q\,\|\,p)$ in general — not a metric.
  • Support condition. $D(p\,\|\,q) = \infty$ if $\exists\, x$ with $p(x) > 0$ but $q(x) = 0$.
  • Reference role. $q$ is the "code" or "prior"; $p$ is the truth.

Equivalent rewriting in terms of entropy and cross-entropy:

$$D(p\,\|\,q) \;=\; \underbrace{\textstyle\sum p\log\tfrac{1}{q}}_{\text{cross-entropy } H(p,q)} \;-\; \underbrace{\textstyle\sum p\log\tfrac{1}{p}}_{H(p)}.$$

Theorem — Gibbs' Inequality

Theorem.

For all pmfs $p, q$ on $\mathcal{X}$,

$$D(p\,\|\,q) \;\ge\; 0,$$

with equality if and only if $p(x) = q(x)$ for all $x \in \mathcal{X}$.

Cornerstone result — almost every information inequality flows from this.

Proof — Gibbs via Jensen

$\log$ is concave, so $-\log$ is convex. Apply Jensen on the random variable $\frac{q(X)}{p(X)}$ under $p$:

$$\begin{aligned} -D(p\,\|\,q) &= \mathbb{E}_p\!\left[\log\frac{q(X)}{p(X)}\right] \\ &\;\stackrel{\text{Jensen}}{\le}\; \log\!\left(\mathbb{E}_p\!\left[\frac{q(X)}{p(X)}\right]\right) \\ &= \log\!\Bigl(\textstyle\sum_x q(x)\Bigr) \;=\; \log 1 \;=\; 0. \end{aligned}$$

Equality iff $q/p$ is constant on the support of $p$ · iff $p = q$. $\square$

Corollary — Maximum Entropy

Take $q = \mathrm{Unif}(\mathcal{X})$, so $q(x) = 1/|\mathcal{X}|$:

$$\begin{aligned} 0 \;\le\; D(p\,\|\,q) &= \sum_x p(x)\log\frac{p(x)}{1/|\mathcal{X}|} \\ &= \log|\mathcal{X}| - H(p). \end{aligned}$$

Hence $H(p) \le \log|\mathcal{X}|$, with equality iff $p$ is uniform. Same theorem from a different angle.

Example — Two Bernoullis

$p = \mathrm{Bern}(1/2)$,  $q = \mathrm{Bern}(1/4)$.

$$\begin{aligned} D(p\,\|\,q) &= \tfrac{1}{2}\log\tfrac{1/2}{1/4} + \tfrac{1}{2}\log\tfrac{1/2}{3/4} \\ &= \tfrac{1}{2}\log 2 + \tfrac{1}{2}\log\tfrac{2}{3} \;=\; 1 - \tfrac{1}{2}\log 3 \;\approx\; 0.208. \end{aligned}$$

Reverse direction:

$$D(q\,\|\,p) \;=\; \tfrac{1}{4}\log\tfrac{1}{2} + \tfrac{3}{4}\log\tfrac{3}{2} \;\approx\; 0.189.$$

Confirms $D(p\,\|\,q) \ne D(q\,\|\,p)$.

Example — Mismatch Cost in Coding

Encode source $X \sim p$ with codeword lengths $\ell(x) = \log\tfrac{1}{q(x)}$:

$$\begin{aligned} \mathbb{E}_p[\ell(X)] &= \sum_x p(x)\log\tfrac{1}{q(x)} \\ &= \sum_x p(x)\log\tfrac{p(x)}{q(x)} + \sum_x p(x)\log\tfrac{1}{p(x)} \\ &= D(p\,\|\,q) + H(p). \end{aligned}$$
Mismatch penalty: $D(p\,\|\,q)$ extra bits per symbol vs the optimal $H(p)$.

Theorem — Log-Sum Inequality

Theorem.

For non-negative $a_1, \dots, a_n$ and positive $b_1, \dots, b_n$,

$$\sum_{i=1}^n a_i \log\frac{a_i}{b_i} \;\ge\; \Bigl(\textstyle\sum_i a_i\Bigr) \log\frac{\sum_i a_i}{\sum_i b_i},$$

with equality iff $a_i / b_i$ is constant in $i$.

Generalization of Jensen for the function $f(t) = t\log t$.

Proof — Log-Sum

Set $A = \sum_i a_i$, $B = \sum_i b_i$,  $\lambda_i = b_i / B$,  $t_i = a_i / b_i$ (defined when $a_i > 0$).

Then $\sum_i \lambda_i = 1$,  $\sum_i \lambda_i t_i = A/B$. Apply Jensen to the convex $f(t) = t\log t$:

$$f\!\Bigl(\sum_i \lambda_i t_i\Bigr) \;\le\; \sum_i \lambda_i f(t_i).$$

Multiply both sides by $B$:

$$A\log\tfrac{A}{B} \;\le\; \sum_i b_i \cdot \tfrac{a_i}{b_i} \log\tfrac{a_i}{b_i} \;=\; \sum_i a_i\log\tfrac{a_i}{b_i}. \qquad\square$$

Corollary — Convexity of KL

Apply log-sum coordinate-wise to $a_i = \lambda p_1(i) + (1-\lambda) p_2(i)$ vs $b_i = \lambda q_1(i) + (1-\lambda) q_2(i)$:

$$D\bigl(\lambda p_1 + (1-\lambda) p_2 \,\|\, \lambda q_1 + (1-\lambda) q_2\bigr) \;\le\; \lambda D(p_1\,\|\,q_1) + (1-\lambda) D(p_2\,\|\,q_2).$$

$D(\cdot\,\|\,\cdot)$ is jointly convex in its two arguments.

Used heavily in convex-optimization views of MLE and channel capacity.

Pinsker's Inequality (statement)

Theorem (Pinsker).

For any pmfs $p, q$ on $\mathcal{X}$,

$$\| p - q\|_{\mathrm{TV}} \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2}\sum_x |p(x) - q(x)| \;\le\; \sqrt{\tfrac{1}{2}\,D(p\,\|\,q)\,\ln 2}.$$

Small KL $\Rightarrow$ small total-variation distance. The bound is tight (Bernoulli example saturates up to a factor).

Proof sketch in companion notes.

Recap

$$\begin{aligned} H(X) &\;\eqdef\; \mathbb{E}\bigl[-\log p_X(X)\bigr] \in [0, \log|\mathcal{X}|] \\ H_b(p) &\;=\; -p\log p - (1-p)\log(1-p) \\ D(p\,\|\,q) &\;\eqdef\; \mathbb{E}_p\bigl[\log\tfrac{p}{q}\bigr] \;\ge\; 0 \end{aligned}$$
  • Entropy: expected surprise · max at uniform · concave
  • KL: divergence "from $q$ to $p$" · mismatch penalty in coding
  • Gibbs & log-sum: workhorses for every later inequality
