01

From Discrete to Continuous

What needs to change?

Why a New Definition?

For continuous $X$, the discrete entropy is infinite:

  • Each individual outcome has probability $0$.
  • $H(X) = \mathbb{E}[-\log p_X(X)]$ blows up if interpreted with a density — densities can exceed $1$.

We need a quantity that:

  • Reduces to $H(X)$ for fine discretizations (up to a known offset).
  • Plays well with KL divergence and mutual information.
  • Makes "Gaussian is special" precise.

Definition — Differential Entropy

Definition.

For a continuous random variable $X$ with density $f_X$,

$$h(X) \;=\; -\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f_X(x)\,\log f_X(x)\,\mathrm{d}x \;=\; \mathbb{E}\bigl[-\log f_X(X)\bigr].$$

(Whenever the integral exists. Convention: $0\log 0 = 0$.)

Lowercase $h$ to distinguish from the discrete $H$.

Discretization Bridge

Bin $X$ to width $\Delta$: $X^\Delta = i$ if $X\in [i\Delta, (i+1)\Delta)$. Then

$$\Pr(X^\Delta = i) \;=\; \int_{i\Delta}^{(i+1)\Delta} f_X(x)\,dx \;\approx\; \Delta\, f_X(x_i)$$

for some $x_i\in [i\Delta, (i+1)\Delta)$. The discrete entropy:

$$\begin{aligned} H(X^\Delta) &\;\approx\; -\sum_i \Delta f_X(x_i) \log\bigl(\Delta f_X(x_i)\bigr) \\ &\;=\; h(X) + \log\tfrac{1}{\Delta} + o(1). \end{aligned}$$

$H(X^\Delta) \to \infty$ as $\Delta\to 0$. The $\log(1/\Delta)$ term diverges.

Reading the Bridge

  • $h(X)$ is the part of $H(X^\Delta)$ that does not depend on $\Delta$.
  • The $\log(1/\Delta)$ term is the bits needed to specify position to precision $\Delta$.
  • Differences such as $h(X) - h(Y)$ and $I(X;Y)$ do have invariant meaning.
  • $h(X)$ alone has no operational meaning — only differences and divergences.
Differential entropy is a relative quantity.
02

Examples

Compute $h(X)$ for the standard families

Uniform on $[a, b]$

$f_X(x) = 1/(b-a)$ on $[a, b]$, zero elsewhere.

$$h(X) \;=\; -\int_a^b \frac{1}{b-a}\log\frac{1}{b-a}\,dx \;=\; \log(b-a).$$
  • $b - a = 1$: $h = 0$.
  • $b - a = 1/2$: $h = -1$. Negative differential entropy.
  • $b - a = 2$: $h = 1$.

Negative $h$ is fine — recall, $h$ is relative.

Gaussian $\mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$

$f_X(x) = (2\pi\sigma^2)^{-1/2}\exp(-(x-\mu)^2/(2\sigma^2))$.

$$\begin{aligned} h(X) &= -\mathbb{E}[\log f_X(X)] \\ &= \mathbb{E}\!\left[\tfrac{1}{2}\log(2\pi\sigma^2) + \tfrac{(X-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}\log e\right] \\ &= \tfrac{1}{2}\log(2\pi\sigma^2) + \tfrac{1}{2}\log e \\ &= \tfrac{1}{2}\log(2\pi e \sigma^2). \end{aligned}$$

$\sigma = 1$: $h \approx 2.047$ bits. · $\sigma = 1/(\sqrt{2\pi e})$: $h = 0$.

Exponential, Rate $\lambda$

$f_X(x) = \lambda e^{-\lambda x}$ for $x\ge 0$. Mean $1/\lambda$.

$$\begin{aligned} h(X) &= -\int_0^\infty \lambda e^{-\lambda x}\bigl(\log\lambda - \lambda x\log e\bigr) dx \\ &= -\log\lambda + \log e \\ &= \log\frac{e}{\lambda}. \end{aligned}$$

$\lambda = 1$: $h = \log e \approx 1.443$ bits.

Laplace, Scale $b$

$f_X(x) = \tfrac{1}{2b}\exp(-|x|/b)$. Variance $2b^2$.

$$\begin{aligned} h(X) &= -\mathbb{E}\!\left[-\log(2b) - |X|/b \cdot \log e\right] \\ &= \log(2b) + b\,\log e / b \\ &= \log(2eb). \end{aligned}$$

$b = 1$: $h = \log(2e) \approx 2.443$ bits.

Heavier-tailed than Gaussian of the same variance, hence higher $h$ relative to a Gaussian comparison.

Cauchy

$f_X(x) = \tfrac{1}{\pi}\tfrac{\gamma}{x^2 + \gamma^2}$. Mean undefined, infinite variance.

$$h(X) \;=\; \log(4\pi\gamma).$$

Exercise. Verify by computing $\mathbb{E}[\log(x^2 + \gamma^2)] = 2\log(2\gamma)$ via complex analysis (or by recognizing the integral $\int \log(1+u^2)/(1+u^2)\,du = 2\pi\log 2$).

Cauchy is the maximum-entropy distribution on $\mathbb{R}$ subject to a constraint of the form $\mathbb{E}[\log(1+(X/\gamma)^2)]\le c$ — an unusual constraint, but worth knowing.

Multivariate Gaussian

$\mathbf{X}\sim\mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\mu}, \boldsymbol{\Sigma})$ on $\mathbb{R}^d$.

$$h(\mathbf{X}) \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2}\log\bigl((2\pi e)^d \det\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\bigr).$$

Special cases:

  • $\boldsymbol{\Sigma} = \sigma^2 I$: $h = \tfrac{d}{2}\log(2\pi e \sigma^2)$.
  • $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}$ diagonal: $h = \sum_i \tfrac{1}{2}\log(2\pi e \sigma_i^2) = \sum_i h(X_i)$.

$\det\boldsymbol{\Sigma}$ encodes the volume of the concentration ellipsoid.

03

Properties

Scaling, translation, the negativity issue

Translation Invariance

Lemma.

For any constant $c$,

$$h(X + c) \;=\; h(X).$$

Density $f_{X+c}(y) = f_X(y - c)$. Substitute $y\to y - c$ in the integral — Jacobian is $1$, density values are the same. $\square$

Same as discrete: relabeling does not change uncertainty.

Scaling — The Crucial Difference

Lemma.

For nonzero $a\in\mathbb{R}$,

$$h(aX) \;=\; h(X) + \log|a|.$$

Proof. If $Y = aX$, the density is $f_Y(y) = f_X(y/a)/|a|$. Then

$$h(Y) \;=\; -\int f_Y(y)\log f_Y(y)\,dy \;=\; h(X) + \log|a|. \qquad\square$$

Discrete entropy is unchanged by a relabeling like $aX$ — differential entropy is not.

Linear Transformation in $\mathbb{R}^d$

Lemma.

For invertible $A\in\mathbb{R}^{d\times d}$ and $\mathbf{X}\in\mathbb{R}^d$,

$$h(A\mathbf{X}) \;=\; h(\mathbf{X}) + \log|\det A|.$$

Linear map stretches volume by $|\det A|$ · differential entropy gains $\log$ of that factor.

Consequence: $h$ is not invariant under $L^2$-isometries with $|\det A|\ne 1$ — e.g. dilation. Invariant under orthogonal transforms ($|\det| = 1$).

Differential Entropy Can Be Negative

$X\sim\mathrm{Unif}[0, 1/2]$:

$$h(X) \;=\; \log(1/2) \;=\; -1 \text{ bit}.$$

And as $b - a \to 0$ for $\mathrm{Unif}[a, b]$: $h \to -\infty$.

Negative $h$ means the density concentrates: per the bridge, the discretization $H(X^\Delta) = h(X) + \log(1/\Delta)$ is still positive (large $1/\Delta$ wins).

So What Quantities Are Meaningful?

Even though $h(X)$ alone is base/scale-dependent, the following are invariant and operational:

  • $D(f \| g) \;=\; \int f\log(f/g)$ · KL divergence.
  • $I(X; Y) \;=\; D(f_{X,Y} \| f_X f_Y)$ · mutual information.
  • $h(X) - h(X\mid Y) \;=\; I(X;Y)$ · reduction.

All three are non-negative, scaling-invariant (Lecture 3), and reduce to the discrete versions in the bin-width limit.

04

Joint and Conditional

Chain rule, KL, all the same identities

Joint Differential Entropy

Definition.

For $(X, Y)$ with joint density $f_{X,Y}$,

$$h(X, Y) \;=\; -\iint f_{X,Y}(x,y)\,\log f_{X,Y}(x,y)\,dx\,dy.$$

Just $h$ of the random vector $(X,Y)$. Same definition extends to any dimension.

Conditional Differential Entropy

Definition.

$$h(X\mid Y) \;=\; -\iint f_{X,Y}(x,y)\,\log f_{X\mid Y}(x\mid y)\,dx\,dy.$$

Equivalently, $h(X\mid Y) = \mathbb{E}_Y[h(X\mid Y = y)]$ where

$$h(X\mid Y=y) \;=\; -\int f_{X\mid Y}(x\mid y)\,\log f_{X\mid Y}(x\mid y)\,dx.$$

Chain Rule

Theorem.

$$h(X, Y) \;=\; h(Y) + h(X\mid Y) \;=\; h(X) + h(Y\mid X).$$

Proof identical to the discrete case: substitute $f_{X,Y} = f_Y \cdot f_{X\mid Y}$ in $\log f_{X,Y} = \log f_Y + \log f_{X\mid Y}$ and integrate. $\square$

Multivariable chain rule: $h(X^n) = \sum_i h(X_i\mid X_1, \dots, X_{i-1})$.

KL Divergence (Continuous)

Definition.

For densities $f, g$ on $\mathbb{R}^d$ with $g(x) > 0$ wherever $f(x) > 0$,

$$D(f\|g) \;=\; \int f(x)\log\frac{f(x)}{g(x)}\,dx \;=\; \mathbb{E}_f\!\left[\log\frac{f(X)}{g(X)}\right].$$

Same form as the discrete case. Gibbs inequality, log-sum, joint convexity all transfer (proof via Jensen, identical).

$$D(f\|g) \;\ge\; 0 \text{ with equality iff } f = g \text{ a.e.}$$

Why KL Is Scale-Invariant

Apply the same change of variables $y = ax$ to both $f$ and $g$:

$$\begin{aligned} D(f\,\|\,g) &= \int f(x)\log\frac{f(x)}{g(x)}\,dx \\ &= \int \frac{f(y/a)}{|a|}\log\frac{f(y/a)/|a|}{g(y/a)/|a|}\,dy \\ &= D(\tilde f \,\|\, \tilde g) \quad \text{where } \tilde f(y) = f(y/a)/|a|. \end{aligned}$$

The $|a|$ Jacobians cancel inside the log. KL has no $\log|a|$ shift.

Why $h(X\mid Y) \le h(X)$ Still Holds

Same as discrete:

$$h(X) - h(X\mid Y) \;=\; D(f_{X,Y}\,\|\,f_X f_Y) \;\ge\; 0.$$

Equality iff $X\perp Y$.

Caveat: $h(X\mid Y = y)$ for a particular $y$ can exceed $h(X)$ — only the average is monotone. Identical to the discrete situation.

Mutual Information — Definition

Definition.

$$I(X;Y) \;=\; D(f_{X,Y}\,\|\,f_X f_Y) \;=\; h(X) - h(X\mid Y) \;=\; h(Y) - h(Y\mid X).$$

Same identities as discrete · same Venn picture.

$I$ is the right object: not affected by scaling, not affected by binning, not affected by relabeling. Negative $h$ does not break it.

Theorem — Invariance of MI Under Scaling

Theorem.

For nonzero $a$,

$$I(aX; Y) \;=\; I(X; Y).$$

Proof.

$$\begin{aligned} I(aX; Y) &= h(aX) - h(aX\mid Y) \\ &= \bigl(h(X) + \log|a|\bigr) - \bigl(h(X\mid Y) + \log|a|\bigr) \\ &= I(X; Y). \qquad\square \end{aligned}$$

Both terms shift by $\log|a|$ and cancel. MI sees only relative structure.

Example — Independent Gaussians

$X\sim\mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_X^2)$, $Y\sim\mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_Y^2)$, independent.

  • $h(X, Y) = \tfrac{1}{2}\log(2\pi e\sigma_X^2) + \tfrac{1}{2}\log(2\pi e\sigma_Y^2)$.
  • $h(X) + h(Y) = $ same value — independence is additive.
  • $I(X; Y) = h(X) + h(Y) - h(X, Y) = 0$. $\;\checkmark$

Example — Correlated Gaussians

$(X, Y)\sim\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \boldsymbol{\Sigma})$ with $\boldsymbol{\Sigma} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & \rho \\ \rho & 1 \end{pmatrix}$, $|\rho| < 1$.

$\det\boldsymbol{\Sigma} = 1 - \rho^2$, so $h(X, Y) = \tfrac{1}{2}\log\bigl((2\pi e)^2(1-\rho^2)\bigr)$.

$h(X) = h(Y) = \tfrac{1}{2}\log(2\pi e)$. Hence:

$$I(X; Y) \;=\; h(X) + h(Y) - h(X, Y) \;=\; -\tfrac{1}{2}\log(1 - \rho^2).$$

$\rho = 0$: $I = 0$. · $\rho \to 1$: $I \to \infty$ (perfect determinism).

Recap

  • Definition: $h(X) = -\int f\log f$ · can be negative.
  • Bridge: $H(X^\Delta) = h(X) + \log(1/\Delta) + o(1)$.
  • Scaling: $h(aX) = h(X) + \log|a|$ · not invariant.
  • Linear map: $h(A\mathbf{X}) = h(\mathbf{X}) + \log|\det A|$.
  • Chain rule, KL, MI: identical form to discrete · KL and MI are invariant.
  • Use $D$ and $I$, not $h$ alone.
