01

Maximum-Entropy Principle

Among all densities matching given moments, which is "maximally agnostic"?

The MaxEnt Question

Given moment constraints $\mathbb{E}[T_k(X)] = \alpha_k$ for $k = 1, \dots, m$, find the density

$$f^\star \;=\; \arg\max_{f}\; h(f) \;=\; -\int f\log f\,dx,$$

subject to $f\ge 0$, $\int f = 1$, $\int f T_k = \alpha_k$.

Reading: among all densities consistent with the data we have, $f^\star$ assumes no extra structure.

Lagrangian

Lagrangian (with multipliers $\lambda_0$ for normalization, $\lambda_k$ for moments):

$$\mathcal{L}[f] \;=\; -\int f\log f \;-\; \lambda_0\!\left(\int f - 1\right) \;-\; \sum_{k=1}^m \lambda_k\!\left(\int f T_k - \alpha_k\right).$$

Variational derivative:

$$\frac{\delta\mathcal{L}}{\delta f}(x) \;=\; -\log f(x) - 1 - \lambda_0 - \sum_k \lambda_k T_k(x) \;=\; 0.$$

The MaxEnt Density

Solve for $f$:

$$f^\star(x) \;=\; \exp\!\left(-1 - \lambda_0 - \sum_k \lambda_k T_k(x)\right) \;=\; \frac{1}{Z}\exp\!\left(-\textstyle\sum_k \lambda_k T_k(x)\right).$$

$Z = e^{1+\lambda_0}$ is the normalizing constant. The multipliers $\lambda_k$ are tuned so that $\mathbb{E}[T_k] = \alpha_k$.

MaxEnt distributions are exponential families with sufficient statistics $T_k$.

Catalogue — Three Constraint Types

supportconstraintMaxEnt density

Why MaxEnt Is Useful

  • Modeling principle. Default to MaxEnt unless additional structure justifies otherwise.
  • Conjugate inequalities. "Gaussian is hardest" for any squared-error problem with Gaussian-like constraints.
  • Hypothesis testing. The least favorable prior (worst case) is often MaxEnt.
  • Channel coding. Capacity-achieving inputs match the constraints.
02

Gaussian = MaxEnt

Variance constraint $\Rightarrow$ Gaussian

Theorem — Gaussian Maximum Entropy

Theorem.

Among all densities on $\mathbb{R}$ with $\mathbb{E}[X^2] \le P$,

$$h(X) \;\le\; \tfrac{1}{2}\log(2\pi e P),$$

with equality if and only if $X\sim\mathcal{N}(0, P)$.

Three proofs follow: KL inequality, Lagrangian, EPI corollary (later).

Proof — KL Inequality (Setup)

Let $g$ be the density of $\mathcal{N}(0, P)$ and $f$ any density with $\int x^2 f(x)\,dx \le P$. Translation-invariance: WLOG $\mathbb{E}_f[X] = 0$.

Use $D(f\,\|\,g) \ge 0$:

$$0 \;\le\; D(f\,\|\,g) \;=\; -h(f) \;-\; \int f(x)\log g(x)\,dx.$$

Proof — KL Inequality (Cross-Term)

Compute the cross-entropy term:

$$\begin{aligned} -\int f\log g\,dx &= -\int f(x)\!\left[-\tfrac{1}{2}\log(2\pi P) - \tfrac{x^2}{2P}\log e\right]\!dx \\ &= \tfrac{1}{2}\log(2\pi P) + \frac{\log e}{2P}\,\mathbb{E}_f[X^2] \\ &\le \tfrac{1}{2}\log(2\pi P) + \tfrac{1}{2}\log e \\ &= \tfrac{1}{2}\log(2\pi e P) \;=\; h(g). \end{aligned}$$

Proof — Combining

Plug back:

$$\begin{aligned} 0 &\;\le\; D(f\,\|\,g) \;=\; -h(f) + (-\int f\log g) \;\le\; -h(f) + h(g). \end{aligned}$$

Hence $h(f) \le h(g) = \tfrac{1}{2}\log(2\pi e P)$.

Equality iff $D(f\,\|\,g) = 0$, i.e. $f = g$ — the Gaussian. $\square$

Trick: the cross-entropy of any density with the Gaussian depends only on the second moment.

Proof Variant — Lagrangian

Constraints: $\int f = 1$, $\int x f = 0$, $\int x^2 f = P$. The MaxEnt template:

$$f^\star(x) \;\propto\; \exp\!\bigl(-\lambda_0 - \lambda_1 x - \lambda_2 x^2\bigr).$$

This is a Gaussian: complete the square. Solving for $\lambda_1, \lambda_2$ to match $0$ mean and variance $P$ gives

$$\lambda_1 = 0, \quad \lambda_2 = \frac{1}{2P\ln 2}, \quad f^\star = \mathcal{N}(0, P).$$

Same answer. $\square$

Corollary — Hadamard's Inequality

For any positive-definite $K\in\mathbb{R}^{d\times d}$:

$$\det K \;\le\; \prod_{i=1}^d K_{ii}.$$

Proof via maxent. Let $\mathbf{X}\sim\mathcal{N}(0, K)$. Then $h(\mathbf{X}) = \tfrac{1}{2}\log\bigl((2\pi e)^d\det K\bigr)$. Apply chain rule and "conditioning reduces entropy":

$$h(\mathbf{X}) \;\le\; \sum_i h(X_i) \;=\; \sum_i \tfrac{1}{2}\log(2\pi e K_{ii}).$$

Exponentiate to get $\det K \le \prod K_{ii}$. $\square$

03

Multivariate Gaussian

Covariance constraint, Hadamard, conditioning

Theorem — Multivariate MaxEnt

Theorem.

Among all densities on $\mathbb{R}^d$ with covariance matrix $\preceq K$,

$$h(\mathbf{X}) \;\le\; \tfrac{1}{2}\log\bigl((2\pi e)^d \det K\bigr),$$

with equality iff $\mathbf{X}\sim\mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\mu}, K)$ for some $\boldsymbol{\mu}$.

Proof: identical to scalar case, using the multivariate KL with cross-entropy depending on covariance only.

Conditioning Gaussians

$(X, Y)\sim\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, K)$ with $K = \begin{pmatrix} K_{XX} & K_{XY} \\ K_{XY}^\top & K_{YY} \end{pmatrix}$.

The conditional $X\mid Y = y$ is Gaussian with

$$\mathbb{E}[X\mid Y = y] \;=\; K_{XY} K_{YY}^{-1} y, \qquad \mathrm{Cov}(X\mid Y) \;=\; K_{XX} - K_{XY} K_{YY}^{-1} K_{XY}^\top.$$

Hence

$$h(X\mid Y) \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2}\log\bigl((2\pi e)^{d_X} \det(K_{XX} - K_{XY} K_{YY}^{-1} K_{XY}^\top)\bigr).$$

Mutual Information for Gaussians

For jointly Gaussian $(X, Y)$:

$$\begin{aligned} I(X; Y) &= h(X) - h(X\mid Y) \\ &= \tfrac{1}{2}\log\frac{\det K_{XX}}{\det(K_{XX} - K_{XY} K_{YY}^{-1} K_{XY}^\top)} \\ &= \tfrac{1}{2}\log\frac{\det K_{XX}\det K_{YY}}{\det K}. \end{aligned}$$

Scalar: $I(X;Y) = -\tfrac{1}{2}\log(1 - \rho^2)$ where $\rho$ is the correlation.

Translation Invariance Revisited

Let $\mathbf{Y} = A\mathbf{X} + \mathbf{b}$ for invertible $A$. Then

$$h(\mathbf{Y}) \;=\; h(\mathbf{X}) + \log|\det A|.$$

Application: orthogonal transformations ($|\det A| = 1$) preserve $h$. Examples:

  • Rotations of $\mathbb{R}^d$.
  • Discrete Fourier transform (unitary).
  • Hadamard / Walsh transforms.

Used heavily in lossy compression (QUIP#, TURBOQUANT) to "Gaussianize" weights without changing entropy.

04

Entropy Power Inequality

How entropies add for sums of independent RVs

Definition — Entropy Power

Definition.

The entropy power of $\mathbf{X}\in\mathbb{R}^d$ is

$$N(\mathbf{X}) \;=\; \frac{1}{2\pi e}\,\exp\!\left(\frac{2}{d}\,h(\mathbf{X})\right).$$
  • For $\mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2 I)$: $N = \sigma^2$ — "the variance of an equivalent isotropic Gaussian".
  • $N$ is non-negative, scaling: $N(a\mathbf{X}) = a^2 N(\mathbf{X})$ (matches variance scaling).

Theorem — Entropy Power Inequality

Theorem (Shannon, 1948; Stam, 1959).

For independent $\mathbf{X}, \mathbf{Y}\in\mathbb{R}^d$,

$$N(\mathbf{X} + \mathbf{Y}) \;\ge\; N(\mathbf{X}) + N(\mathbf{Y}),$$

with equality iff $\mathbf{X}, \mathbf{Y}$ are Gaussians with proportional covariances.

Stam, "Some inequalities satisfied by the quantities of information of Fisher and Shannon", Information and Control 1959.

Equivalent Form

Taking $\log$:

$$\exp\!\left(\tfrac{2}{d}h(\mathbf{X} + \mathbf{Y})\right) \;\ge\; \exp\!\left(\tfrac{2}{d}h(\mathbf{X})\right) + \exp\!\left(\tfrac{2}{d}h(\mathbf{Y})\right).$$

Or: "the entropy power of a sum is at least the sum of entropy powers".

Proof is classical but technical — uses Fisher information and the heat semigroup. Sketch in companion notes.

Special Case — Both Gaussian

$\mathbf{X}\sim\mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_X^2 I)$, $\mathbf{Y}\sim\mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_Y^2 I)$, independent.

  • $\mathbf{X} + \mathbf{Y}\sim\mathcal{N}(0, (\sigma_X^2 + \sigma_Y^2) I)$.
  • $N(\mathbf{X} + \mathbf{Y}) = \sigma_X^2 + \sigma_Y^2 = N(\mathbf{X}) + N(\mathbf{Y})$.

Equality — the EPI is saturated when both are isotropic Gaussians.

EPI — Why "Gaussians Are Hardest"

For variance $\sigma^2$, the maximum-entropy density is the Gaussian. For an entropy budget, the minimum-variance density is the Gaussian.

EPI sharpens this: under convolution (sums), Gaussianity is preserved, and any deviation strictly increases the entropy power gap.

Among inputs to a noise-adding channel, Gaussian input + Gaussian noise is the worst-case rate. Used in the AWGN converse (Lecture 3).

Application — AWGN Capacity Converse Sketch

AWGN: $Y = X + Z$, $Z\sim\mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)$, $\mathbb{E}[X^2] \le P$.

For any input distribution:

$$\begin{aligned} h(Y) &\;\le\; \tfrac{1}{2}\log(2\pi e (P + \sigma^2)) \quad\text{(MaxEnt for variance } \le P+\sigma^2\text{)} \\ h(Y\mid X) &\;=\; h(Z) \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2}\log(2\pi e \sigma^2) \\ I(X;Y) &= h(Y) - h(Y\mid X) \;\le\; \tfrac{1}{2}\log\!\left(1 + \tfrac{P}{\sigma^2}\right). \end{aligned}$$

Equality with $X\sim\mathcal{N}(0, P)$. Shannon–Hartley capacity.

Application — CLT-Style Result

For i.i.d. $X_1, \dots, X_n$ with finite variance,

$$\bar X_n \;=\; \tfrac{1}{n}\sum_i X_i \;\xrightarrow{d}\; \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2/n).$$

EPI gives a related entropy-monotonicity result: $h(\bar X_n)$ increases with $n$ (when normalized properly), reflecting the fact that the sum is "more Gaussian" than its summands.

The monotonicity of entropy along the CLT (Artstein–Ball–Barthe–Naor 2004) makes this rigorous.

Recap

  • MaxEnt: $f^\star \propto \exp(-\sum \lambda_k T_k)$ · exponential family.
  • Gaussian: max $h$ for fixed variance · bound $\tfrac{1}{2}\log(2\pi e P)$.
  • Multivariate: MaxEnt under covariance $K$ is $\mathcal{N}(0, K)$.
  • Hadamard: $\det K\le\prod K_{ii}$ from MaxEnt + chain rule.
  • EPI: $N(\mathbf{X}+\mathbf{Y})\ge N(\mathbf{X}) + N(\mathbf{Y})$.
Gaussian is the "extremal" object — the central object in continuous information theory.
