01

The Complexity Wall of Unstructured VQ

Shannon's bound says go to high dimension. The cost says you can't.

Standard VQ recap

Group $d$ scalar parameters into a vector $\mathbf{w}\in\mathbb{R}^d$, and map to the nearest centroid in a codebook

$$\mathcal{C}=\{\mathbf{c}_1,\ldots,\mathbf{c}_K\}\subset\mathbb{R}^d.$$

For rate $R$ bits/coordinate: $K=2^{dR}$. Larger $d$ buys "rounder" cells, lower distortion at fixed rate.

So why not just take $d$ very large?

Storage and search blow up exponentially in $d$

Storage

Codebook $\mathcal{C}$: $\;d\cdot K=d\cdot 2^{dR}$ floating-point numbers.

For $d=8,\,R=4$:  $\approx 34$ billion floats.

Search

Nearest-neighbor query: $\;\mathcal{O}(d\cdot 2^{dR})$ per vector.

For an LLM with $10^{10}$ weights and the same $d,R$: infeasible.

Direct VQ is theoretically optimal but operationally dead at LLM scale.

What we want

Capture the rate–distortion benefits of high-$d$ vector quantization without the exponential storage and search.

Ingredients we will need:

  • An implicit codebook — described by a deterministic rule, not a list.
  • A fast nearest-neighbor algorithm for that rule, $\mathcal{O}(d)$ time.
  • A way to match the codebook to the data — LLM weights are spiky, but the lattice is rigid.
02

Lattice Codes

A regular grid in $\mathbb{R}^d$ — with uniform Voronoi cells and algebraic rounding

Definition: lattice

A lattice $\Lambda\subset\mathbb{R}^d$ is the set of all integer linear combinations of basis vectors $b_1,\ldots,b_d$:

$$\Lambda\;=\;\Bigl\{\sum_{i=1}^{d}n_i\,b_i:\;n_i\in\mathbb{Z}\Bigr\}\;=\;B\mathbb{Z}^d,\qquad B=[b_1\,|\,b_2\,|\,\cdots\,|\,b_d].$$

An infinite, periodic set of points.

Voronoi region of $\lambda\in\Lambda$: $\;\mathcal{V}(\lambda)=\{x:\|x-\lambda\|\le\|x-\lambda'\|\;\forall\lambda'\in\Lambda\}$. By translation symmetry, all Voronoi regions are congruent.

Voronoi regions: identical, symmetric, near-spherical

Unstructured VQ codebooks have arbitrary cell shapes. A lattice has one cell shape, repeated everywhere.

Best lattices in dimension $d$ have Voronoi regions that are nearly spherical: small second moment for given volume.

Examples: $D_4$ (4-D), $E_8$ (8-D), Leech lattice (24-D). $E_8$ is provably the densest 8-D sphere packing.

Packing gain: regularity buys distortion

Normalized second moment of a lattice $\Lambda$ with Voronoi region $\mathcal{V}$ of volume $V$:

$$G(\Lambda)\;\coloneqq\;\frac{1}{V^{1+2/d}}\int_{\mathcal{V}}\|x\|^2\,dx.$$

$G(\Lambda)$ measures cell "roundness" — smaller is better.

  • Cubic lattice $\mathbb{Z}^d$: $\;G=1/12$ for any $d$. Worst case of practical interest.
  • $E_8$: $G\approx 0.0717$ — about 14% better than $\mathbb{Z}^8$.
  • Leech ($d=24$): $G\approx 0.0658$ — close to the sphere bound.
  • Sphere bound: $G\to\frac{1}{2\pi e}\approx 0.0585$ as $d\to\infty$.

This is the shape gain: free distortion reduction at fixed rate, just from picking the right grid.

Fast lattice rounding: $\mathcal{O}(d)$, not $\mathcal{O}(2^{dR})$

For $\mathbb{Z}^d$: nearest lattice point to $x\in\mathbb{R}^d$ is $\lfloor x+\tfrac12\rfloor$ coordinate-wise — $\mathcal{O}(d)$.

For $D_d$ (even-sum integer lattice): round each coordinate, then if the sum is odd, flip the coordinate that had the largest rounding error. Still $\mathcal{O}(d)$.

For $E_8$: combine $D_8$ rounding and a half-integer translate; pick whichever is closer. $\mathcal{O}(d)$.

The codebook has size $2^{dR}$ in principle, but search runs in $\mathcal{O}(d)$ via the algebraic structure.

03

Effective Codebook Construction

Base lattice $+$ scaling $+$ shaping

Component 1 — the base lattice (implicit coordinates)

Pick $\Lambda$ once (e.g. $E_8$). Store nothing — the lattice is defined by its basis matrix $B$, a few floats.

Index a lattice point by its integer coordinates $\mathbf{n}\in\mathbb{Z}^d$:

$$\mathbf{c}_{\mathbf{n}}\;=\;B\mathbf{n}\in\Lambda.$$

Decoding an index is just one matrix-vector product. Encoding (nearest-neighbor) uses fast rounding above.

Component 2 — scaling $\Delta$ controls distortion

Scale the lattice by $\Delta>0$:

$$\Lambda_\Delta\;=\;\Delta\cdot\Lambda\;=\;\{\Delta\mathbf{c}:\mathbf{c}\in\Lambda\}.$$

Quantization-error variance scales with $\Delta^2$:

$$\mathbb{E}\|x-Q_{\Lambda_\Delta}(x)\|^2\;=\;\Delta^2\cdot G(\Lambda)\cdot V_\Lambda^{2/d}.$$

Smaller $\Delta$ means denser grid, more bits to index a given region; larger $\Delta$ saves bits at the cost of distortion.

Component 3 — shaping by intersection with a sphere

Truncate the infinite lattice to a finite codebook by intersecting with a shaping region $\mathcal{S}$:

$$\mathcal{C}_{\text{lattice}}\;=\;\Lambda_\Delta\cap\mathcal{S}(r).$$

For high-dim Gaussian-like data, take $\mathcal{S}$ to be a $d$-dimensional ball of radius $r$.

A spherical $\mathcal{S}$ matches the typical set of an isotropic high-dim Gaussian source: no codepoints wasted in low-density corners.

Why a sphere: matching the source's typical set

Concentration argument

For $X\sim\mathcal{N}(0,I_d)$, the squared norm $\|X\|^2\sim\chi^2_d$ has mean $d$ and standard deviation $\sqrt{2d}$. So

$$\Pr\bigl[\,\bigl|\|X\|^2-d\bigr|\ge\varepsilon d\,\bigr]\;\le\;2e^{-\Omega(\varepsilon^2 d)}.$$

Probability mass concentrates on a thin shell of radius $\sqrt{d}$. A spherical shaping region of radius $\sqrt{d}+O(1)$ contains nearly all of the probability while wasting no codepoints on corners.

Cubic shaping $[-A,A]^d$, by contrast, has $(2A)^d$ volume but only a $(d^{-1/2})^d$ fraction near the typical sphere. Catastrophic in high $d$. $\;\blacksquare$

04

QUIP\#: Lattice Quantization for LLMs

Quantization with Incoherence Processing — Tseng et al. 2024

Why the lattice can't be applied to raw weights

Lattices are uniform, isotropic. LLM weights are not: a few outliers dominate the energy, the typical set is heavy-tailed and skewed.

Drop a spherical $\mathcal{C}_{\text{lattice}}$ on raw weights: most of the codebook is empty, most of the data lies far from the nearest lattice point.

Source–channel mismatch. Either bend the lattice to fit the weights (destroys fast rounding) or bend the weights to fit the lattice. QUIP\# does the latter.

Step 1 — incoherence processing (orthogonal rotation)

Pick orthogonal matrices $\mathbf{U}\in\mathbb{R}^{m\times m}$, $\mathbf{V}\in\mathbb{R}^{n\times n}$. Pre- and post-multiply each weight matrix:

$$\widetilde{\mathbf{W}}\;=\;\mathbf{U}\,\mathbf{W}\,\mathbf{V}^\top.$$

Practical choice: random Hadamard transforms. Cost $\mathcal{O}(d\log d)$ per multiplication, no learnable parameters.

Why incoherence processing is "free"

Isometry + invertibility

Orthogonal matrices preserve $\ell_2$ inner products and norms:

$$\|\mathbf{U}\mathbf{W}\mathbf{V}^\top\|_F^2=\operatorname{tr}\bigl((\mathbf{U}\mathbf{W}\mathbf{V}^\top)^\top \mathbf{U}\mathbf{W}\mathbf{V}^\top\bigr)=\operatorname{tr}(\mathbf{V}\mathbf{W}^\top\mathbf{W}\mathbf{V}^\top)=\|\mathbf{W}\|_F^2.$$

Likewise the layer's input-output map: pre-/post-rotating intermediate activations leaves the network's function unchanged. So the rotated weights $\widetilde{\mathbf{W}}$ encode the same model. $\;\blacksquare$

Hadamard rotations Gaussianize the spike

Each entry of $\widetilde{\mathbf{W}}$ is a sum of many products of independent random signs and weight entries. By the central limit theorem, the marginal distribution converges to Gaussian.

$$\widetilde{W}_{ij}\;\approx\;\mathcal{N}\!\bigl(0,\,\tfrac{1}{mn}\|\mathbf{W}\|_F^2\bigr)\quad\text{for typical }(i,j).$$

Outliers are spread evenly across all coordinates. The transformed weights now match the geometric assumption of the lattice (isotropic, near-spherical typical set).

Step 2 — lattice quantization on the transformed weights

Group the (incoherent) entries of $\widetilde{\mathbf{W}}$ into $d$-dimensional vectors (e.g. $d=8$, the $E_8$ block).

Quantize each block using fast lattice rounding:

$$\widehat{\mathbf{W}}\;=\;Q_{\Lambda_\Delta}(\widetilde{\mathbf{W}}).$$

Store the integer index $\mathbf{n}_b$ of the chosen lattice point per block.

Entropy code: indices $\mathbf{n}_b$ are concentrated near the origin for spherically-symmetric data, so an arithmetic coder can compress them down to roughly the Shannon entropy of the index distribution.

Step 3 — effective codebook in the original space

At inference, undo the rotation:

$$\mathbf{W}_q\;=\;\mathbf{U}^\top\,\widehat{\mathbf{W}}\,\mathbf{V}.$$

The implicit codebook in the original weight space:

$$\mathcal{C}_{\text{eff}}\;=\;\{\mathbf{U}^\top \mathbf{c}\,\mathbf{V}\;:\;\mathbf{c}\in\mathcal{C}_{\text{lattice}}\}.$$

By the random rotation, $\mathcal{C}_{\text{eff}}$ looks like a dense, pseudo-random Gaussian codebook in the original space — matching the heavy-tailed, skewed weight distribution.

The two-faced effective codebook

To the data

$\mathcal{C}_{\text{eff}}$ is a pseudo-random scattering of points contoured to the LLM weight distribution — behaves like the Shannon-optimal random Gaussian codebook from the achievability proof.

To the hardware

Underneath the rotation, it is a rigid grid $\Lambda$. Zero memory to store the codebook; algebraic $\mathcal{O}(d)$ rounding per block.

QUIP\# harvests the rate–distortion benefits of Shannon's theory without paying its exponential cost.

Tseng et al., "QUIP\#: Even Better LLM Quantization with Hadamard Incoherence and Lattice Codebooks", ICML 2024

Recap of Lecture 3

  • Complexity wall: unstructured VQ at LLM scale needs $\sim 2^{dR}$ codewords — impossible.
  • Lattices: regular grids with congruent Voronoi cells, near-spherical for good lattices ($E_8$, Leech), fast rounding $\mathcal{O}(d)$.
  • Effective codebook: base lattice + scaling $\Delta$ + spherical shaping — described by a rule, not a list.
  • QUIP\#: rotate weights with $\mathbf{U},\mathbf{V}$ Hadamard transforms $\to$ Gaussianize $\to$ lattice quantize $\to$ entropy code.
  • Effective codebook in original space: dense pseudo-random; behaves like a Shannon-optimal codebook for the data, like a rigid grid for hardware.
