01

Motivation and Setup

Density ratios, samples-only access, the central problem

Why estimate mutual information?

MI quantifies statistical dependence between two random variables:

$$I(X;Y)\;=\;D_{\mathrm{KL}}\bigl(p_{X,Y}\,\Vert\,p_X p_Y\bigr)\;=\;\mathbb{E}\!\left[\log\dfrac{p(X,Y)}{p(X)p(Y)}\right].$$

Used as an objective in:

  • Representation learning: maximize $I(T;Y)$ to retain task-relevant info.
  • Contrastive learning: maximize $I(T_1;T_2)$ across views (SimCLR, CLIP).
  • Information bottleneck: minimize $I(T;X)$ under a fidelity constraint.

Problem: $p(x,y)$ and the marginals are unknown. We have only samples.

Samples-only setting

Given i.i.d. samples $(x_i,y_i)\sim p_{X,Y}$ for $i=1,\ldots,N$.

From these we can also sample from the product of marginals: shuffle pairs to form $(x_i,y_j)$ with $j\ne i$. So we have empirical access to:

$$P=p_{X,Y}\quad\text{(joint)},\qquad Q=p_X p_Y\quad\text{(product of marginals)}.$$

$I(X;Y)=D_{\mathrm{KL}}(P\Vert Q)$. The question becomes: how do we estimate KL from samples of $P$ and $Q$?

The density-ratio framing

Define

$$r(x,y)\;\coloneqq\;\dfrac{p(x,y)}{p(x)p(y)},\qquad I(X;Y)=\mathbb{E}_{p_{X,Y}}[\log r(X,Y)].$$

If we knew $r$, MI would be a single expectation under $p_{X,Y}$ — estimable from samples.

Strategy: learn a critic $T_\theta(x,y)\approx\log r(x,y)$ (or an unnormalized version) by optimizing a variational lower bound. The optimum recovers $\log r$.

02

Barber–Agakov (BA) Bound

Use a conditional model $q(x\mid y)$

The BA bound

For any conditional density $q(x\mid y)$ on the same alphabet:

$$I(X;Y)\;\ge\;H(X)+\mathbb{E}_{p(x,y)}[\log q(x\mid y)]\;\eqqcolon\;I_{\mathrm{BA}}(q).$$

Tight when $q(x\mid y)=p(x\mid y)$.

BA proof

Proof

Start from $I(X;Y)=H(X)-H(X\mid Y)$ and expand the conditional entropy:

$$H(X\mid Y)=-\mathbb{E}_{p(x,y)}[\log p(x\mid y)]=-\mathbb{E}_{p(x,y)}[\log q(x\mid y)]-\mathbb{E}_{p(y)}\!\bigl[D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p(\cdot\mid y)\,\Vert\,q(\cdot\mid y))\bigr].$$

Substitute back:

$$I(X;Y)=H(X)+\mathbb{E}_{p(x,y)}[\log q(x\mid y)]+\mathbb{E}_{p(y)}D_{\mathrm{KL}}(\cdot)\;\ge\;H(X)+\mathbb{E}_{p(x,y)}[\log q(x\mid y)].\;\blacksquare$$

BA in practice

$H(X)$ is independent of $q$ — it is a constant. So:

  • Optimization use: maximize $\mathbb{E}_{p(x,y)}[\log q_\theta(x\mid y)]$ — just maximum likelihood of $X\mid Y$.
  • Numerical estimate: need $H(X)$, which is usually unknown.

BA is typically used to train representations, not to report MI numbers. When the task is "make $T$ predictive of $Y$", BA reduces to MLE of the conditional — no MI machinery needed.

03

Donsker–Varadhan (DV) Bound

Dual representation of KL, basis of MINE

Donsker–Varadhan representation of KL

For any distributions $P,Q$ on a common space:

$$D_{\mathrm{KL}}(P\,\Vert\,Q)\;=\;\sup_T\;\bigl\{\,\mathbb{E}_P[T]-\log\mathbb{E}_Q[e^T]\,\bigr\},$$

supremum over measurable $T$ for which the expectations exist.

Apply with $P=p_{X,Y}$, $Q=p_X p_Y$:

$$I(X;Y)\;\ge\;\mathbb{E}_{p(x,y)}[T(x,y)]-\log\mathbb{E}_{p(x)p(y)}[e^{T(x,y)}]\;\eqqcolon\;I_{\mathrm{DV}}(T).$$

DV proof

Proof of the variational form

For any $T$ with $\mathbb{E}_Q[e^T]<\infty$, define a tilted distribution $G$ via $dG/dQ=e^T/\mathbb{E}_Q[e^T]$. Compute

$$D_{\mathrm{KL}}(P\Vert G)=\mathbb{E}_P\!\left[\log\dfrac{dP/dQ}{e^T/\mathbb{E}_Q[e^T]}\right]=D_{\mathrm{KL}}(P\Vert Q)-\mathbb{E}_P[T]+\log\mathbb{E}_Q[e^T].$$

Non-negativity of KL: $D_{\mathrm{KL}}(P\Vert Q)\ge \mathbb{E}_P[T]-\log\mathbb{E}_Q[e^T]$, with equality iff $P=G$, i.e. $T=\log(dP/dQ)+c$. $\;\blacksquare$

The optimal critic recovers $\log r$

Equality in DV holds at $T^\star(x,y)=\log r(x,y)+c$ for any constant $c$. So learning the critic by maximizing $I_{\mathrm{DV}}(T)$ recovers the log-density-ratio (up to a constant).

$$I_{\mathrm{DV}}(T^\star)=\mathbb{E}[\log r(X,Y)]+c-\log\mathbb{E}_Q[e^{c}\,r(X,Y)]=\mathbb{E}[\log r]+c-c-\log\,1=I(X;Y).$$

(Using $\mathbb{E}_Q[r]=\mathbb{E}_{p_X p_Y}[p_{X,Y}/(p_X p_Y)]=1$.) Tight bound when fully optimized.

MINE: parameterize $T$ as a neural network

Mutual Information Neural Estimator (Belghazi et al. 2018):

  • Parameterize $T_\theta(x,y)$ by a neural network.
  • Approximate the two expectations by minibatches: positives from $(x_i,y_i)\sim p_{X,Y}$, negatives by shuffling.
  • Gradient-ascend $I_{\mathrm{DV}}(T_\theta)$.

Catch. The $\log\mathbb{E}_Q[e^T]$ term is volatile under finite samples — a single large $T$ value dominates the inner expectation. Variance grows exponentially in MI.

Belghazi, Baratin, Rajeswar, Ozair, Bengio, Courville, Hjelm, "MINE: Mutual Information Neural Estimation", ICML 2018
04

Nguyen–Wainwright–Jordan (NWJ) Bound

Replace the log-partition function with a linear surrogate

From DV to NWJ via $\log u\le u-1$

Derivation

For $u>0$, $\log u\le u-1$. Apply to the DV log-partition with $u=\mathbb{E}_Q[e^T]$:

$$\log\mathbb{E}_Q[e^T]\;\le\;\mathbb{E}_Q[e^T]-1.$$

Therefore

$$I_{\mathrm{DV}}(T)=\mathbb{E}_P[T]-\log\mathbb{E}_Q[e^T]\;\ge\;\mathbb{E}_P[T]-\mathbb{E}_Q[e^T]+1.$$

Reparameterize $T\to T+1$ (shift) to get the cleaner form.

The NWJ bound

For any critic $T$:

$$I(X;Y)\;\ge\;\mathbb{E}_{p(x,y)}[T(x,y)]-\mathbb{E}_{p(x)p(y)}[e^{T(x,y)-1}]\;\eqqcolon\;I_{\mathrm{NWJ}}(T).$$

Tight at $T^\star(x,y)=\log r(x,y)+1$.

NWJ replaces the variance-heavy $\log\mathbb{E}[e^T]$ with a tractable $\mathbb{E}[e^{T-1}]$ — cheaper to estimate, but the bound is generally looser at finite-sample optima.

DV vs. NWJ vs. BA

BoundFormOptimal criticTrade-off
BA$H(X)+\mathbb{E}\log q(x\mid y)$$q(x\mid y)=p(x\mid y)$Needs $H(X)$; reduces to MLE.

05

Bias / Variance Tradeoffs

High-MI regimes, finite-batch effects

What goes wrong at high MI

For a strongly dependent pair, $\log r$ has a long right tail. The DV term $\log\mathbb{E}_Q[e^T]$ is dominated by the largest sample of $T$. McAllester & Stratos (2020):

$$\text{Variance of empirical }\log\mathbb{E}_Q[e^T]\;\sim\;\dfrac{e^{I(X;Y)}}{N}.$$

Variance grows exponentially in the true MI. To estimate $I=10$ nats, need $N\sim e^{10}\approx 22000$ samples just to control variance — before bias.

No fully sample-efficient estimator of high MI exists. This is a genuine information-theoretic barrier, not a software bug.

Toy benchmark: bivariate Gaussian

Let $(X,Y)$ be jointly Gaussian with correlation $\rho\in(-1,1)$, unit variances. Closed-form MI:

$$I(X;Y)\;=\;-\tfrac{1}{2}\log(1-\rho^2).$$

Behavior of estimators:

  • $|\rho|\le 0.5$ ($I\le 0.14$ nats): all estimators converge with $\sim 10^3$ samples.
  • $|\rho|=0.99$ ($I\approx 1.96$ nats): DV diverges without huge batches; NWJ stable but biased low.

Standard benchmark for sanity-checking new estimators.

Which bound for which task?

  • Optimization-only objective (representation learning): use BA — reduces to MLE. Don't worry about MI numbers.
  • Stable contrastive objective: InfoNCE (next lecture). Bounded above by $\log K$, very stable, used by SimCLR / CLIP.
  • Want a tight numerical MI estimate at low-to-moderate MI: NWJ or DV with careful sample budget.
  • High MI ($I\gg 1$ nat): no estimator is reliable. Report an interval with a clear caveat.

Recap of Lecture 1

  • MI estimation problem: only samples from $P=p_{X,Y}$ and $Q=p_X p_Y$.
  • Density-ratio framing: learn a critic $T\approx\log r$ by optimizing a variational lower bound.
  • BA: $I\ge H(X)+\mathbb{E}\log q(x\mid y)$, reduces to conditional MLE.
  • DV: $I\ge\mathbb{E}_P[T]-\log\mathbb{E}_Q[e^T]$, tight at $T=\log r+c$. Basis of MINE.
  • NWJ: $I\ge\mathbb{E}_P[T]-\mathbb{E}_Q[e^{T-1}]$, more stable, slightly looser.
  • High-MI barrier: variance grows like $e^I/N$. Reliable estimation only at low-to-moderate MI.
