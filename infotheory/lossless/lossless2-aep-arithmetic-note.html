Lossless 2 — Companion Notes

AEP — Stronger Statements

The slide states the weak AEP: convergence in probability. Stronger versions:

  • Strong AEP (Birkhoff/Shannon–McMillan–Breiman): for any stationary ergodic source, $-(1/n)\log p(X^n) \to H(\mathscr{X})$ almost surely. This requires the ergodic theorem rather than the weak law.
  • Method of types: for finite alphabets, the typical set can be replaced by the "type class" $T(\hat p)$ of sequences with empirical distribution $\hat p$. Sanov's theorem gives $|T(\hat p)|\doteq 2^{nH(\hat p)}$ and $\Pr(T(\hat p))\doteq 2^{-nD(\hat p\,\|\,p)}$.

Why "Equipartition"?

In the typical set, every sequence has probability $\approx 2^{-nH(X)}$ — the mass is "equally partitioned" across the $\approx 2^{nH(X)}$ typical sequences, even though their individual probabilities differ at the $2^{n\varepsilon}$ scale.

Outside the typical set: probability mass is large (a single sequence may have prob much larger than $2^{-nH}$ when $X$ is highly skewed) but the total probability of atypical sequences vanishes.

Bernoulli Example — Numbers

$X_i\sim\mathrm{Bern}(0.1)$, $n=100$. By Hoeffding, $\Pr(|\hat p - 0.1| > \varepsilon) \le 2 e^{-2 n\varepsilon^2}$. For $\varepsilon = 0.04$, this is $2 e^{-0.32}\approx 1.45$ — useless. Need $\varepsilon \ge 1/\sqrt{2n} \approx 0.07$.

Hence at $n=100$ the "typical set" still contains sequences with empirical $p$ ranging from $\sim 0.03$ to $\sim 0.17$. AEP becomes operationally tight only at $n\gtrsim 10^4$ for binary sources.

Source Coding Theorem — Both Directions

Achievability uses random partitioning of $\mathcal{X}^n$ into $2^{nR}$ buckets via the typical-set construction. The encoder sends the bucket index; the decoder uses the typical-set membership to recover.

Converse uses Fano-style accounting: $H(X^n) \le H(W) + H(X^n\mid W)\le \log|\mathrm{range}(f)| + 1 + P_e \cdot n\log|\mathcal{X}|$, where $W = f(X^n)$. Rearrange to get $P_e \ge 1 - R/H(X) - 1/(n\log|\mathcal{X}|)$, bounded away from 0 when $R < H$.

Arithmetic Coding — Why Length $\le \log 1/p + 2$?

The binary expansion of any $z\in[L_n, H_n)$ to $K$ bits satisfies $\hat z\in[\hat z, \hat z + 2^{-K})$. We need this $K$-bit window to fit inside $[L_n, H_n)$. The window has length $2^{-K}$; if $2^{-K} \le \tfrac{1}{2}(H_n - L_n)$, then there exists a window inside $[L_n, H_n)$.

The condition $2^{-K} \le \tfrac{1}{2}p(x^n)$ gives $K \ge \log(2/p(x^n)) = \log(1/p) + 1$. The proof in the slide gets the bound $K \le \log(1/p) + 2$ via a slightly looser analysis around the "smallest such $K$" requirement.

Arithmetic Coding — Practical Issues

Real implementations face two issues:

  1. Floating-point precision. The interval $[L_n, H_n)$ shrinks geometrically; a 64-bit float overflows after ~50 symbols. Real codecs use integer arithmetic with renormalization: emit prefix bits as the interval becomes determined, shifting the integer state.
  2. Patents (historical). Most arithmetic-coding patents expired after 2010; range coding (a near-equivalent variant) was used to skirt them. Modern codecs use range coding or arithmetic coding interchangeably.

Connection to Universal Coding (Lecture 3)

Arithmetic coding is the workhorse: any sequential probability model $q(x_t\mid x_1^{t-1})$ plugs in directly. The achieved rate is $H(p) + D(p\,\|\,q)$, with $D \to 0$ as the model improves. In modern LLM-based compression, $q$ is the LLM's next-token distribution, achieving compression rates near the entropy of the source distribution as estimated by the LLM.
