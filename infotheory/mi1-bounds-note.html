MI Estimation 1 · Variational Bounds — Notes

Why density-ratio estimation is the unifying view

Each variational MI bound boils down to learning the log-density ratio $\log r(x,y)=\log\frac{p(x,y)}{p(x)p(y)}$ via a different surrogate loss. The density ratio is also the optimal classifier between samples from $P=p_{X,Y}$ (positives) and $Q=p_X p_Y$ (negatives) up to a logit scale. So MI estimation, classification, and contrastive learning are formally equivalent up to choice of variational objective.

This is also the reason CLIP "works" as an MI maximizer: the contrastive logit between an image and its caption is exactly a learned $\log r$.

The MINE estimator's bias

Naive empirical estimate of DV uses the same minibatch for both expectations. The empirical $\widehat{\log\mathbb{E}_Q[e^T]}=\log\frac{1}{N}\sum e^{T(x_i,y_j)}$ is biased downward (Jensen on the empirical mean), giving an upward bias on the MI estimate. Belghazi et al. introduce a moving-average correction in the denominator to reduce this bias.

For learning representations (gradient-based optimization of $T_\theta$), the bias matters less than the gradient direction. For reporting MI numbers, it matters a lot.

The McAllester–Stratos lower bound on MI estimator variance

McAllester & Stratos (2020) showed that any sample-efficient MI estimator with $N$ samples has variance at least $\Omega(e^I/N)$. The argument is by reduction to two-sample testing: distinguishing $P$ from $Q$ when $D_{\mathrm{KL}}(P\Vert Q)=I$ requires a test whose variance scales with $e^I$ (Le Cam-style lower bound).

This is unconditional — it doesn't depend on the estimator class. Nothing in the variational-bounds literature beats this bound for high-MI estimation.

Toy bivariate Gaussian, in detail

For $\binom{X}{Y}\sim\mathcal{N}(0,\Sigma)$ with $\Sigma=\begin{pmatrix}1&\rho\\\rho&1\end{pmatrix}$, the joint density is $\propto \exp\bigl(-\frac{1}{2(1-\rho^2)}(x^2-2\rho xy+y^2)\bigr)$. Marginals are $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$ each. The log-density ratio is

\[ \log r(x,y)=-\tfrac12\log(1-\rho^2)+\tfrac{\rho}{1-\rho^2}xy-\tfrac{\rho^2}{2(1-\rho^2)}(x^2+y^2). \]

The first term is the constant offset; the rest depend on $(x,y)$. Plug into $I(X;Y)=\mathbb{E}_{p(x,y)}[\log r]$, evaluate the Gaussian moments: $\mathbb{E}[XY]=\rho$, $\mathbb{E}[X^2]=\mathbb{E}[Y^2]=1$. Combine to get $I=-\tfrac12\log(1-\rho^2)$.

For $\rho=0.5$: $I\approx 0.144$ nats. For $\rho=0.9$: $I\approx 0.83$ nats. For $\rho=0.99$: $I\approx 1.96$ nats. The variance penalty $e^I$ goes from $1.15$ to $2.3$ to $7.1$ — manageable. At $\rho=0.999$, $I\approx 3.1$ nats and $e^I\approx 22$, getting into hard territory.

Connection to $f$-divergences and the $f$-GAN family

Both DV and NWJ are special cases of $f$-divergence variational bounds. Nguyen, Wainwright & Jordan (2010) gave a general dual representation:

\[ D_f(P\Vert Q)=\sup_T\bigl\{\mathbb{E}_P[T]-\mathbb{E}_Q[f^*(T)]\bigr\}, \]

where $f^*$ is the convex conjugate of $f$. For $f(u)=u\log u$ (KL), $f^*(t)=e^{t-1}$, recovering NWJ. The DV form is actually a tightening: subtract $1$ and apply the inequality $f^*(t)\le f^*(\sup t)+\ldots$ to get the $\log\mathbb{E}_Q[e^T]$ form. Both bounds are valid; DV is tighter.

This connects to the $f$-GAN family of generative models: each choice of $f$ gives a different divergence and different generator-critic dynamics.

Why CLIP / InfoNCE is a different story

InfoNCE (next lecture) is also a variational lower bound on MI, but with a different ceiling: bounded by $\log K$ where $K$ is the number of contrastive negatives per minibatch. For $K=256$, $\log K\approx 5.5$ nats — well above the regime where DV/NWJ work, but still finite.

The trade-off: InfoNCE is much more stable than DV, can train with stochastic gradients on huge batches, and produces useful representations even when its numerical MI estimate is biased low. For optimization, InfoNCE wins; for tight estimation, NWJ.
