01

The Rate–Distortion Problem

Real-valued sources cannot be stored exactly: trade bits for fidelity

Lossless Is Not Enough

Discrete source $X\sim P_X$: lossless coding achieves rate $H(X)$ bits/symbol.

Real-valued $X\in\mathbb{R}$: perfect reconstruction with finitely many bits is impossible. $H(X)=\infty$.

Lossy compression: trade rate (bits/symbol) for fidelity, measured by a distortion function $d(x,\hat x)\ge 0$.

Central question: how many bits per sample to describe a random object up to distortion $D$?

Three Distortion Functions

Squared error

$$d(x,\hat x)=(x-\hat x)^2$$

Default for continuous sources. Gives the Gaussian theory.

Hamming

$$d_H(x,\hat x)=\mathbb{1}\{x\ne\hat x\}$$

Default for discrete sources. Counts mismatches.

Absolute error

$$d_1(x,\hat x)=|x-\hat x|$$

Natural for sparse representations and pruning.

Block Code: Encoder and Decoder

An $(n,R)$ lossy code:

$$f:\mathcal{X}^n\to\{1,\dots,2^{nR}\},\qquad g:\{1,\dots,2^{nR}\}\to\widehat{\mathcal{X}}^n.$$

Reconstruction $\widehat{X}^n=g(f(X^n))$. Rate $R$ = bits per source symbol.

Per-letter average distortion:

$$d^{(n)}(x^n,\hat x^n)\;\coloneqq\;\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n d(x_i,\hat x_i).$$

Achievable Rate–Distortion Pairs

Achievable $(R,D)$: for every $\varepsilon>0$ and large $n$, there exists an $(n,R+\varepsilon)$ code with $\mathbb{E}\,d^{(n)}\le D+\varepsilon$.

$$R(D)\coloneqq \inf\{R:(R,D)\text{ achievable}\}.$$

Properties: $R(D)$ is non-increasing, convex, and continuous on $(D_{\min},D_{\max})$.

Covering the Typical Set

Lossless coding distinguishes essentially every typical sequence.

Lossy coding distinguishes only distortion balls: two source sequences share the same description if a single reconstruction is close to both.

Geometric picture: $R(D)$ is the smallest log-count of distortion-$D$ balls needed to cover the typical set.

This intuition becomes exact via Shannon's mutual-information formula.

02

Scalar Quantization

Heights, $R\in\{0,1,2\}$, Lloyd–Max

Toy Source: Heights of a Population

Model:

$$X\sim\mathcal{N}(\mu,\sigma^2),\quad \mu=170,\ \sigma^2=10.$$

Distortion: squared error $d(x,\hat x)=(x-\hat x)^2$.

Goal: store each $X$ using $R$ bits.

$R=0$: Store Nothing

No bits sent: decoder outputs a constant $\widehat X = c$.

Question: which $c$ minimizes $\mathbb{E}[(X-c)^2]$?

Claim: $c^\star = \mathbb{E}[X] = \mu$, with optimal distortion $D(0)=\operatorname{Var}(X)=\sigma^2$.

$R=0$: Proof that $c^\star=\mu$

Proof

Add and subtract $\mu$, then expand:

$$\mathbb{E}[(X-c)^2]=\sigma^2+\underbrace{2(\mu-c)\,\mathbb{E}[X-\mu]}_{=\,0}+(\mu-c)^2.$$

Cross-term vanishes since $\mathbb{E}[X-\mu]=0$. So $\mathbb{E}[(X-c)^2]=\sigma^2+(\mu-c)^2\ge\sigma^2$, equality iff $c=\mu$. Hence $c^\star=\mu$ and $D(0)=\sigma^2$. $\;\blacksquare$

$R=1$: One Threshold, Two Cells

Encoder partitions $\mathbb{R}$ at the median (mean for Gaussian):

$$f(x)=\mathbb{1}\{x>\mu\},\qquad \hat x_0=\mathbb{E}[X\mid X\le\mu],\quad \hat x_1=\mathbb{E}[X\mid X>\mu].$$

(These reps are optimal under squared error: same proof as $R=0$, restricted to each cell.)

Goal: compute $D(1)=\mathbb{E}[(X-\widehat X)^2]$.

$R=1$: Half-Gaussian Conditional Mean

Lemma 1

Standardize $X=\mu+\sigma Z$ with $Z\sim\mathcal{N}(0,1)$. Then $X>\mu\Leftrightarrow Z>0$, so

$$\mathbb{E}[Z\mid Z>0]\;=\;\frac{\int_0^\infty z\,\phi(z)\,dz}{1/2}\;=\;\frac{1/\sqrt{2\pi}}{1/2}\;=\;\sqrt{\tfrac{2}{\pi}}.$$

Therefore $\mathbb{E}[X\mid X>\mu]=\mu+\sigma\sqrt{2/\pi}$. $\;\blacksquare$

$R=1$: Conclude $D(1)=\sigma^2(1-2/\pi)$

Proof

By symmetry of the Gaussian, both cells contribute equally. The optimal rep is the conditional mean, so squared error equals the conditional variance:

$$D(1)=\sigma^2\,\operatorname{Var}(Z\mid Z>0)=\sigma^2\bigl(1-\tfrac{2}{\pi}\bigr)\approx 0.363\sigma^2.$$

Used: $\mathbb{E}[Z^2\mid Z>0]=1$ (by symmetry of $z^2\phi(z)$) and Lemma 1 for the cross-term. $\;\blacksquare$

$R=1$ Picture: Half-Line Cells

The bit identifies the half-line; decoder outputs its conditional mean. Distortion $\sigma^2(1-2/\pi)$ is the conditional variance.

$R=2$: Four-Cell Thresholds

Naive choice: thresholds at $165, 170, 175$. Optimal in general? No.

Optimal thresholds and reps depend on each other: best rep is the cell's conditional mean; best threshold is the midpoint of adjacent reps.

This circular dependence is solved by Lloyd–Max.

Lloyd–Max Algorithm

Fix $K$ reconstruction levels. Alternate until convergence.

① Centroid update

Thresholds $\{\tau_i\}$ fixed:

$$\hat x_i\leftarrow \mathbb{E}[X\mid \tau_i\le X<\tau_{i+1}].$$

Each rep is the conditional mean of its cell.

② Boundary update

Reps $\{\hat x_i\}$ fixed:

$$\tau_{i+1}\leftarrow \tfrac{1}{2}(\hat x_i+\hat x_{i+1}).$$

Each threshold is the midpoint of adjacent reps.

Lloyd–Max: Centroid Update Is Optimal

Step 1 of monotone-decrease proof

Total distortion:

$$D(\tau,\hat x)=\sum_{i=1}^{K}\int_{\tau_i}^{\tau_{i+1}}(x-\hat x_i)^2 p(x)\,dx.$$

For fixed $\tau$, each integral is minimized over $\hat x_i$ by the conditional mean (same argument as $R=0$). So step ① globally minimizes $D$ over $\hat x$ at fixed $\tau$:

$$D(\tau,\hat x^{\,\text{new}})\le D(\tau,\hat x^{\,\text{old}}).$$

Lloyd–Max: Boundary Update + Convergence

Steps 2 and 3 of monotone-decrease proof

Step 2. For fixed $\hat x$, the integrand at each $x$ is the smaller of $(x-\hat x_i)^2$ and $(x-\hat x_{i+1})^2$. The minimum switches at the midpoint $\frac{1}{2}(\hat x_i+\hat x_{i+1})$. So the midpoint globally minimizes $D$ over $\tau$ at fixed $\hat x$.

Step 3. The sequence $D_t=D(\tau_t,\hat x_t)$ is non-increasing and bounded below by $0$:

$$D_0\ge D_1\ge D_2\ge\cdots\ge 0\;\Longrightarrow\; D_t\text{ converges.}\;\blacksquare$$
03

Vector Quantization

Quantizing blocks beats quantizing coordinates, even for i.i.d. components

Quantize Blocks, Not Coordinates

Vector quantization quantizes a block jointly:

$$Q^{(n)}:\mathbb{R}^{n}\longrightarrow \{1,\dots,2^{nR}\}.$$

Codebook of $2^{nR}$ representatives in $\mathbb{R}^n$. Encoder picks the nearest; decoder looks it up.

Even for i.i.d. components, VQ can beat scalar quantization. The reason is geometric.

Scalar = Rectangles, Vector = Round Cells

Scalar quantization (2-D)

Vector quantization (hex)

Why Round Cells Win at Fixed Area

For a uniform source on a cell, the average squared distance to the centroid is the cell's second moment:

$$M(\mathcal{C})=\frac{1}{|\mathcal{C}|}\int_{\mathcal{C}}\|x-c\|^2\,dx.$$

Among 2-D cells of fixed area, the disk minimizes $M$. A square is about 8% worse; rectangles much worse.

In high dimension, mass concentrates in a typical shell. Good VQ covers this shell with near-spherical cells; scalar quantization wastes budget on empty corners.

04

Shannon's Theorem

$R(D)=R^{(I)}(D)$ via random coding and data processing

Information Rate–Distortion Function

Single-letter optimization over test channels $P_{\widehat X\mid X}$:

$$R^{(I)}(D)\;\coloneqq\;\inf_{\substack{P_{\widehat X\mid X}\\ \mathbb{E}\,d(X,\widehat X)\le D}}\; I(X;\widehat X).$$

Shannon's rate–distortion theorem: for a memoryless source under single-letter distortion,

$$\boxed{\;R(D)=R^{(I)}(D).\;}$$

Two parts: achievability $R\le R^{(I)}(D)$ via random coding, and converse $R\ge R^{(I)}(D)$ via data processing.

Why Mutual Information Appears

A rate-$R$ code selects one out of $2^{nR}$ reconstructions. The index $M=f(X^n)$ takes at most $2^{nR}$ values, so

$$nR\ge \log_2|\operatorname{range}(f)|\ge H(M)\ge I(X^n;M)\ge I(X^n;\widehat X^n).$$

(Last step: $\widehat X^n=g(M)$ is a deterministic function of $M$, data-processing inequality.)

Smallest information $\widehat X$ must carry about $X$ to keep distortion $\le D$ is exactly $\inf I(X;\widehat X)$ subject to $\mathbb{E}\,d\le D$.

Random Codebook Construction

Fix test channel $P_{\widehat X\mid X}$ with $\mathbb{E}\,d\le D-\delta$ and $I(X;\widehat X)=I$. Let $P_{\widehat X}$ be the marginal.

Generate $M=2^{nR}$ codewords i.i.d. from this marginal:

$$\widehat X^n(1),\ldots,\widehat X^n(M),\qquad \widehat X^n(m)\stackrel{\text{i.i.d.}}{\sim}P_{\widehat X}^{\otimes n}.$$

Encoder: find $m$ with $(X^n,\widehat X^n(m))\in T_\varepsilon^{(n)}$; send $m$ using $nR$ bits.

Decoder: output $\widehat X^n(m)$.

Joint Typicality Lemma

Lemma (joint AEP)

For any $x^n\in T_\varepsilon^{(n)}(P_X)$ and $\widehat X^n\sim P_{\widehat X}^{\otimes n}$ independent of $x^n$:

$$2^{-n(I(X;\widehat X)+3\varepsilon)}\;\le\;\Pr\bigl[(x^n,\widehat X^n)\in T_\varepsilon^{(n)}\bigr]\;\le\;2^{-n(I(X;\widehat X)-3\varepsilon)}.$$

Sketch: $|T_\varepsilon^{(n)}|\approx 2^{nH(X,\widehat X)}$; each $\hat x^n$ has marginal probability $\approx 2^{-nH(\widehat X)}$. Product $\approx 2^{-nI(X;\widehat X)}$ after normalizing. The $3\varepsilon$ absorbs typical-set entropy bounds. $\;\blacksquare$

Achievability: No Jointly Typical Codeword

Proof (encoder error)

Condition on typical $X^n=x^n$. Codewords are i.i.d., so events $\{(x^n,\widehat X^n(m))\in T_\varepsilon\}$ are independent. Each has probability $\ge 2^{-n(I+3\varepsilon)}$ by the joint AEP. Using $(1-u)^M\le e^{-Mu}$:

$$\Pr[\text{no match}\mid X^n=x^n]\le \exp\!\bigl(-2^{n(R-I-3\varepsilon)}\bigr).$$

For $R>I+3\varepsilon$, the right side is doubly-exponentially small. So $\Pr[\text{no match}]\to 0$. $\;\blacksquare$

Achievability: Distortion under Match Found

Proof (distortion bound)

Let $\mathcal{E}=\{\text{no match}\}$. On $\mathcal{E}^c$, joint typicality forces empirical means near true means:

$$\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}d(X_i,\widehat X^n_i(m^\star))\;\le\;\mathbb{E}\,d(X,\widehat X)+\varepsilon\;\le\;D-\delta+\varepsilon.$$

On $\mathcal{E}$, bound by $d_{\max}$. Total: $\mathbb{E}\,d^{(n)}\to D-\delta+\varepsilon$. Choose $\varepsilon<\delta$. Every $R>I(X;\widehat X)$ is achievable; optimizing over the test channel gives achievability of $R^{(I)}(D)$. $\;\blacksquare$

Search Creates the Dependence

Codewords are independent of $X^n$ by construction. The encoder picks one satisfying a rare typicality event. Conditioned on that event, the empirical joint of $(X^n,\widehat X^n(m^\star))$ matches $P_{X,\widehat X}$.

Generate many independent fakes; one of them, by sheer count, looks as if drawn jointly with the source.

Converse: Rate Bounds Total Mutual Information

Assume $(R,D)$ achievable. Fix a code with $M=f(X^n)\in\{1,\ldots,2^{nR}\}$, $\widehat X^n=g(M)$, $\mathbb{E}\,d^{(n)}\le D$.

Step 1

$M$ has range size $\le 2^{nR}$, and $\widehat X^n=g(M)$ is a deterministic function of $M$. By data processing on $X^n\to M\to\widehat X^n$:

$$nR\;\ge\;\log_2|\operatorname{range}(M)|\;\ge\;H(M)\;\ge\;I(X^n;M)\;\ge\;I(X^n;\widehat X^n).$$

Converse: Single-Letterization

Step 2

Memoryless source: $h(X^n)=\sum_i h(X_i)$. By the chain rule, $h(X^n\mid\widehat X^n)\le\sum_i h(X_i\mid\widehat X_i)$ (conditioning reduces entropy). Therefore

$$I(X^n;\widehat X^n)=h(X^n)-h(X^n\mid\widehat X^n)\;\ge\;\sum_{i=1}^n I(X_i;\widehat X_i).$$

Converse: Closing via Convexity

Step 3

By definition $I(X_i;\widehat X_i)\ge R^{(I)}(D_i)$ where $D_i=\mathbb{E}\,d(X_i,\widehat X_i)$. Apply Jensen to the convex non-increasing $R^{(I)}$:

$$\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n R^{(I)}(D_i)\;\ge\;R^{(I)}\!\left(\tfrac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n D_i\right)\;\ge\;R^{(I)}(D).$$

Combining steps 1–3: $\;nR\ge n R^{(I)}(D)\;\Longrightarrow\;R\ge R^{(I)}(D).\;\blacksquare$

Binary Toy: $n=24,\ D=1/4$

Literal bit strings, ball-volume proof, threshold rate

Bernoulli Source, Hamming Distortion

Source: $X_i\stackrel{\text{i.i.d.}}{\sim}\mathrm{Bern}(1/2)$. Distortion: $d_H(x,\hat x)=\mathbb{1}\{x\ne\hat x\}$.

For $0

$$X=\widehat X\oplus Z,\qquad \widehat X\sim\mathrm{Bern}(1/2),\quad Z\sim\mathrm{Bern}(D),\quad \widehat X\perp Z.$$

Then $\Pr[X\ne\widehat X]=\Pr[Z=1]=D$, so $\mathbb{E}\,d_H=D$.

Binary $R^{(I)}(D)\le 1-H_b(D)$

Achievability of the formula

The proposed channel has $\widehat X$ marginal Bernoulli$(1/2)$. Compute mutual information:

$$I(X;\widehat X)=H(X)-H(X\mid\widehat X)=H(X)-H(\widehat X\oplus Z\mid\widehat X)=H(X)-H(Z)=1-H_b(D).$$

So $R^{(I)}(D)\le 1-H_b(D)$. $\;\blacksquare$

Binary $R^{(I)}(D)\ge 1-H_b(D)$

Converse for the binary formula

Let $Z'=X\oplus\widehat X$. Since $\Pr[Z'=1]\le D\le 1/2$, $H(Z')\le H_b(D)$. Then

$$I(X;\widehat X)=H(X)-H(Z'\mid\widehat X)\;\ge\;H(X)-H(Z')\;\ge\;1-H_b(D).$$

Combining: $R(D)=R^{(I)}(D)=1-H_b(D)$ for $0\le D\le 1/2$. $\;\blacksquare$

Hamming Ball Volume Bound

Lemma: $|B(0,nD)|\le 2^{nH_b(D)}$ for $D\le 1/2$

The ball is $B(0,nD)=\{y^n:\sum y_i\le nD\}$, so $|B(0,nD)|=\sum_{k=0}^{\lfloor nD\rfloor}\binom{n}{k}$. For $k\le nD\le n/2$, $D^k(1-D)^{n-k}$ is non-increasing in $k$. Hence each term of the Bin$(n,D)$ pmf is at least the $k=nD$ term:

$$1\ge D^{nD}(1-D)^{n-nD}\sum_{k=0}^{\lfloor nD\rfloor}\binom{n}{k}.$$

Solving: $|B(0,nD)|\le 2^{nH_b(D)}.\;\blacksquare$

Volume Counting Recovers the Threshold Rate

A random Bernoulli$(1/2)$ codeword falls in $B(X^n,nD)$ with probability

$$\frac{|B(0,nD)|}{2^n}\le\frac{2^{nH_b(D)}}{2^n}=2^{-n(1-H_b(D))}.$$

A codebook of size $M=2^{nR}$ contains a close match w.h.p. iff $R>1-H_b(D)=R(D)$.

For $D=1/4$: $R(D)\approx 0.189$ bits/symbol. At $n=24$, $\approx 4.5$ bits total ($\approx 23$ codewords). The matched-candidate slide at 64 codewords is in the "almost sure" regime.

Recap of Lecture 1

  • R–D problem: trade rate for fidelity under a fixed distortion $d$.
  • Scalar quantization: $D(0)=\sigma^2$, $D(1)=\sigma^2(1-2/\pi)$. Lloyd–Max is monotone-decreasing alternation.
  • Vector quantization: joint quantization shapes round cells; lower distortion at the same rate.
  • Shannon's theorem: $R(D)=R^{(I)}(D)=\inf I(X;\widehat X)$ subject to $\mathbb{E}\,d\le D$.
  • Achievability: random codebook of $2^{nR}$ contains a jointly typical codeword w.h.p. for $R>I$.
  • Converse: $nR\ge I(X^n;\widehat X^n)\ge\sum I(X_i;\widehat X_i)\ge nR^{(I)}(D)$ via DPI and convexity.
  • Binary toy: $R(D)=1-H_b(D)$, with explicit MI computation and ball-volume bound.
