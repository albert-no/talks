Setup & The Random-Rotation Idea

Why rotation alone produces a known coordinate distribution

Why ordinary scalar quantization fails on real vectors

Scalar quantization rounds each coordinate to a small set of levels — simple, but assumes coordinates are uniformly distributed.

In practice (LLM KV caches, embeddings) a few coordinate outliers dominate the energy. Low-bit scalar quantization wastes resolution on the outliers and starves the typical coordinates.

A random rotation spreads the energy: every coordinate gets the same predictable distribution, with typical magnitude $\sim 1/\sqrt d$.

Target distortion exponent: $4^{-b}$

Gaussian distortion–rate function (Lecture 2): $D(R)=\sigma^2\cdot 2^{-2R}$.

For a unit vector in $\mathbb{R}^d$, after rotation each coordinate has variance $\approx 1/d$. Quantizing each at $b$ bits suggests

$$\text{total MSE}\;\approx\;d\cdot\dfrac{1}{d}\cdot 2^{-2b}\;=\;4^{-b}.$$

TURBOQUANT achieves this exponent up to a small constant factor, without assuming Gaussian inputs.

Problem formulation

A vector quantizer is a pair

$$Q:\mathbb{R}^d\to\{0,1\}^B,\qquad Q^{-1}:\{0,1\}^B\to\mathbb{R}^d,\qquad B=bd.$$

Reconstruction $\widetilde x=Q^{-1}(Q(x))$. Bit-width $b$ = bits per coordinate.

Restrict to unit vectors $x\in S^{d-1}$. For general $x\ne 0$, write $x=\|x\|_2 u$ and store the scalar norm separately ($\mathcal{O}(1)$ bits, negligible per coordinate).

Two distortion notions

MSE distortion

$$D_{\mathrm{mse}}(Q;x)=\mathbb{E}\|x-\widetilde x\|_2^2.$$

Reconstruction quality. Target: $\sim 4^{-b}$.

Inner-product distortion

$$D_{\mathrm{prod}}(Q;x,y)=\mathbb{E}|\langle y,x\rangle-\langle y,\widetilde x\rangle|^2.$$

For attention & nearest-neighbor: want unbiased $\langle y,\widetilde x\rangle$.

The MSE-optimized TURBOQUANT is biased for inner products. Fixing the bias is the second half of the lecture.

The random rotation idea

Let $\Pi\in\mathbb{R}^{d\times d}$ be a uniformly random (Haar) orthogonal matrix. For any fixed $x\in S^{d-1}$,

$$Y\;\coloneqq\;\Pi x\;\sim\;\mathrm{Unif}(S^{d-1}).$$

The output distribution depends only on $\|x\|=1$, not on the direction of $x$. So the algorithm does not need to know the input distribution: rotation creates a known coordinate law.

Lemma: one coordinate of a uniform sphere point

Lemma 1

Let $Y\sim\mathrm{Unif}(S^{d-1})$. Each coordinate $Y_j$ has density

$$f_d(t)\;=\;\frac{\Gamma(d/2)}{\sqrt\pi\,\Gamma((d-1)/2)}\,(1-t^2)^{(d-3)/2},\qquad -1\le t\le 1.$$

Equivalently $Y_j^2\sim\mathrm{Beta}(1/2,(d-1)/2)$. For large $d$:

$$\sqrt d\,Y_j\;\Rightarrow\;\mathcal{N}(0,1).$$

Lemma 1 — proof by the slice argument

Proof of Lemma 1

Fix $Y_1=t$. The remaining $(d-1)$-coordinates lie on the sphere of radius $\sqrt{1-t^2}$ in $\mathbb{R}^{d-1}$. The surface-area of that slice is proportional to $(1-t^2)^{(d-3)/2}$. Normalize over $t\in[-1,1]$ to get the density.

Gaussian limit: when $t=u/\sqrt d$ with $u=O(1)$,

$$(1-t^2)^{(d-3)/2}=\exp\!\left(\tfrac{d-3}{2}\log(1-t^2)\right)\approx\exp\!\bigl(-\tfrac{d t^2}{2}\bigr)=\exp\!\bigl(-u^2/2\bigr).$$

So $\sqrt d\,Y_j$ has approximately the standard Gaussian density. $\;\blacksquare$

Coordinates are nearly independent in high dimension

The coordinates of $Y\sim\mathrm{Unif}(S^{d-1})$ are not exactly independent — they satisfy $\sum_j Y_j^2=1$. But for any fixed $k\ll d$,

$$\sqrt d\,(Y_1,\ldots,Y_k)\;\Rightarrow\;\mathcal{N}(0,I_k).$$

This is the heuristic that justifies coordinate-wise scalar quantization after rotation: each coordinate behaves like an independent $\mathcal{N}(0,1/d)$ sample.

Numerical anchor: $d=1000$

For a unit vector in $d=1000$:

  • Typical rotated coordinate: standard deviation $1/\sqrt{1000}\approx 0.0316$.
  • One-bit Gaussian Lloyd–Max: levels $\pm\sqrt{2/(\pi d)}\approx\pm 0.0252$.
  • Typical coordinate magnitude is dwarfed by $1$. The quantizer represents many small coordinates near zero, not arbitrary reals.

After rotation, the quantizer faces a known scalar source with predictable scale. The vector-quantization problem reduces to (nearly) independent scalar quantization.

MSE-Optimized TURBOQUANT

Algorithm, theorem, proof via Panter–Dite

Algorithm: TURBOQUANT$_{\mathrm{mse}}$

Public: rotation $\Pi$, scalar codebook $\{c_1,\ldots,c_K\}$ for the density $f_d$ ($K=2^b$).

encode(x): y = Π·x for j = 1 .. d: idx[j] = argmini |y[j] − ci| return idx # d·b bits decode(idx): for j = 1 .. d: ỹ[j] = c[idx[j]] return ΠT·ỹ

The codebook is computed once (offline Lloyd–Max on $f_d$) and reused for every input. No data-dependent training.

The optimal scalar cost is a Lloyd–Max problem on $f_d$

With ordered centroids $c_1\le\cdots\le c_K$ and midpoint thresholds $\tau_i=(c_i+c_{i+1})/2$:

$$C(f_d,b)\;=\;\min_{c_1,\ldots,c_K}\;\sum_{i=1}^{K}\int_{\tau_{i-1}}^{\tau_i}(t-c_i)^2 f_d(t)\,dt.$$

For moderate or large $d$, $f_d\approx\mathcal{N}(0,1/d)$. Lloyd–Max on a Gaussian is a textbook computation. Resulting MSE values:

The number $1-2/\pi$ is the one-bit MSE

Computation for $b=1$

For $Y\sim\mathcal{N}(0,1/d)$ and a sign-quantizer $\widehat Y=a\,\operatorname{sign}(Y)$:

$$\mathbb{E}[(Y-a\operatorname{sign}Y)^2]=\mathbb{E}[Y^2]-2a\,\mathbb{E}|Y|+a^2,\qquad a^\star=\mathbb{E}|Y|=\sqrt{\tfrac{2}{\pi d}}.$$

Plug in: $\mathbb{E}[(Y-a^\star\operatorname{sign}Y)^2]=\frac{1}{d}-\frac{2}{\pi d}=\frac{1}{d}(1-\frac{2}{\pi})$. Multiplied by $d$ coordinates:

$$\text{total MSE}\;=\;1-\tfrac{2}{\pi}\;\approx\;0.363.\;\blacksquare$$

Theorem: MSE guarantee

Theorem (TURBOQUANT$_{\mathrm{mse}}$)

For every unit vector $x\in S^{d-1}$,

$$D_{\mathrm{mse}}(Q_{\mathrm{mse}};x)\;=\;\mathbb{E}\|x-\widetilde x\|_2^2\;\le\;\frac{\sqrt 3\,\pi}{2}\cdot 4^{-b}\;\approx\;2.72\cdot 4^{-b}.$$

The exponent $4^{-b}$ matches the Gaussian rate–distortion bound.

Proof step 1 — orthogonal invariance reduces to scalar problem

Step 1

Let $Y=\Pi x$ and $\widetilde Y$ the coordinate-wise reconstruction. Since $\Pi$ is orthogonal:

$$\|x-\widetilde x\|_2^2\;=\;\|\Pi(x-\widetilde x)\|_2^2\;=\;\|\Pi x-\Pi\widetilde x\|_2^2\;=\;\|Y-\widetilde Y\|_2^2.$$

So the vector MSE equals the sum of $d$ scalar MSEs, each on a coordinate with the same marginal density $f_d$:

$$D_{\mathrm{mse}}=\sum_{j=1}^{d}\mathbb{E}(Y_j-\widetilde Y_j)^2\;=\;d\cdot C(f_d,b).$$

Proof step 2 — Panter–Dite high-resolution bound

Step 2 — Panter–Dite formula

For Lloyd–Max scalar quantization of a density $f$ at $K=2^b$ levels, in the high-resolution regime,

$$C(f,b)\;\le\;\frac{1}{12}\Bigl(\int f(t)^{1/3}\,dt\Bigr)^{3}\!4^{-b}.$$

For the sphere density $f_d$, the integral evaluates to

$$\Bigl(\int f_d(t)^{1/3}\,dt\Bigr)^3\;=\;\frac{6\sqrt 3\,\pi}{d}\quad\Longrightarrow\quad d\cdot C(f_d,b)\le\tfrac{\sqrt 3\,\pi}{2}\cdot 4^{-b}.\;\blacksquare$$
03

Information-Theoretic Lower Bound

Why $4^{-b}$ cannot be improved — Shannon LB plus Yao's minimax

Shannon lower bound for vector MSE

Lemma (vector SLB)

For $X\in\mathbb{R}^d$ with differential entropy $h(X)$ (in bits) and any reconstruction $\widehat X$ with

$$I(X;\widehat X)\le B,\qquad \mathbb{E}\|X-\widehat X\|_2^2\le D,$$

we have

$$D\;\ge\;\dfrac{d}{2\pi e}\cdot 2^{\frac{2}{d}(h(X)-B)}.$$

SLB proof

Proof

Let $Z=X-\widehat X$. Apply the Gaussian-maxent bound:

$$B\ge I(X;\widehat X)=h(X)-h(Z\mid\widehat X)\ge h(X)-h(Z)\ge h(X)-\tfrac{d}{2}\log_2\!\bigl(2\pi e\tfrac{D}{d}\bigr).$$

Rearrange for $D$:

$$D\;\ge\;\dfrac{d}{2\pi e}\cdot 2^{\frac{2}{d}(h(X)-B)}.\;\blacksquare$$

Specialize to the sphere: $D(B)\ge 2^{-2B/d}$

For $X\sim\mathrm{Unif}(S^{d-1})$, the differential entropy is $h(X)=\log_2 A_d$ where $A_d=\frac{2\pi^{d/2}}{\Gamma(d/2)}$ is the sphere's surface area. Stirling's approximation:

$$A_d^{2/d}\;\approx\;\frac{2\pi e}{d}\quad\Longrightarrow\quad D(B)\;\ge\;2^{-2B/d}.$$

For $B=bd$: $\;D(B)\ge 2^{-2b}=4^{-b}$.

So any $bd$-bit quantizer on the sphere has MSE at least $4^{-b}$ on average over $\mathrm{Unif}(S^{d-1})$.

Yao's minimax: average-case to worst-case

Yao's principle. The expected cost of any randomized algorithm on the worst input is at least the expected cost of the best deterministic algorithm on the worst input distribution.

Application to TURBOQUANT

If every deterministic $bd$-bit quantizer has expected MSE $\ge 4^{-b}$ for $X\sim\mathrm{Unif}(S^{d-1})$, then for every randomized quantizer there exists at least one hard input $x\in S^{d-1}$ with

$$\mathbb{E}\|x-Q^{-1}(Q(x))\|_2^2\;\ge\;4^{-b}.$$

Optimality gap: TURBOQUANT is within a constant of the best

Combining the upper bound (Theorem) and the lower bound (Yao):

$$\boxed{\;4^{-b}\;\le\;D_{\mathrm{mse}}^{\star}(b)\;\le\;D_{\mathrm{mse}}(Q_{\mathrm{mse}})\;\le\;\tfrac{\sqrt 3\,\pi}{2}\cdot 4^{-b}.\;}$$

The constant $\sqrt 3\pi/2\approx 2.72$ is not the main point. The main point is that the exponent $4^{-b}$ is unavoidable, and TURBOQUANT achieves it.

04

Why MSE Quantization Is Biased for Inner Products

A clean MSE reconstruction can systematically shrink projections

One-bit MSE quantizer in the rotated domain

Use the Gaussian approximation for $f_d$. The one-bit MSE quantizer is

$$\widetilde Y_j\;=\;a\,\operatorname{sign}(Y_j),\qquad a=\sqrt{\tfrac{2}{\pi d}}.$$

Reconstruction $\widetilde x=\Pi^\top\widetilde Y$. By rotational symmetry, $\mathbb{E}[\widetilde x\mid x]=\alpha x$ for some scalar $\alpha$. Compute it.

Derivation: $\alpha\approx 2/\pi$

Bias calculation

Take inner product with $x$:

$$\alpha=\mathbb{E}\langle x,\widetilde x\rangle=\mathbb{E}\langle\Pi x,\widetilde Y\rangle=\mathbb{E}\sum_{j=1}^{d}a\,|Y_j|=d\,a\,\mathbb{E}|Y_1|.$$

Using $Y_1\approx\mathcal{N}(0,1/d)$, $\mathbb{E}|Y_1|=\sqrt{2/(\pi d)}=a$. Therefore

$$\alpha\;\approx\;d\cdot\frac{2}{\pi d}\;=\;\dfrac{2}{\pi}\quad\Longrightarrow\quad\mathbb{E}\langle y,\widetilde x\rangle\approx\dfrac{2}{\pi}\langle y,x\rangle.\;\blacksquare$$

Geometric reason: the reconstruction has shrunken norm

The one-bit MSE reconstruction has approximately constant magnitude in every rotated coordinate. Squared norm:

$$\|\widetilde Y\|_2^2\;=\;d\cdot a^2\;=\;d\cdot\dfrac{2}{\pi d}\;=\;\dfrac{2}{\pi}.$$

So $\|\widetilde x\|_2=\sqrt{2/\pi}<1$. It is a good MSE representative of a "sign cell," but its projection on any direction is multiplicatively shrunk.

Good MSE reconstruction does not imply good linear-functional preservation. We need a different tool.

05

QJL: One-Bit Unbiased Sketch

Quantized Johnson–Lindenstrauss for the residual

QJL definition

Let $S\in\mathbb{R}^{d\times d}$ have i.i.d. entries $S_{ij}\sim\mathcal{N}(0,1)$. For a unit vector $u\in S^{d-1}$:

$$Q_{\mathrm{qjl}}(u)\;=\;\operatorname{sign}(Su)\in\{-1,+1\}^{d}\quad\text{(one bit per coordinate).}$$

Decoder, given a sign vector $z\in\{-1,+1\}^d$:

$$Q_{\mathrm{qjl}}^{-1}(z)\;=\;\dfrac{\sqrt{\pi/2}}{d}\,S^\top z.$$

Lemma: QJL is unbiased with $\mathcal{O}(1/d)$ variance

Lemma (QJL)

For $u\in S^{d-1}$ and any query $y\in\mathbb{R}^d$:

$$\mathbb{E}_S\bigl[\langle y,Q_{\mathrm{qjl}}^{-1}(Q_{\mathrm{qjl}}(u))\rangle\bigr]\;=\;\langle y,u\rangle,\qquad \operatorname{Var}_S(\cdot)\;\le\;\dfrac{\pi}{2d}\,\|y\|_2^2.$$

Unbiased estimator with variance shrinking like $1/d$. This is exactly what we need to fix the inner-product bias.

QJL unbiasedness proof

Proof of unbiasedness

Write $\langle y,Q_{\mathrm{qjl}}^{-1}(Q_{\mathrm{qjl}}(u))\rangle=\frac{1}{d}\sum_{i=1}^{d}\sqrt{\frac{\pi}{2}}\,(s_i^\top y)\operatorname{sign}(s_i^\top u)$, where $s_i$ is row $i$ of $S$.

Key Gaussian identity. For $s\sim\mathcal{N}(0,I)$ and any unit $u$, $\mathbb{E}\bigl[s\operatorname{sign}(s^\top u)\bigr]=\sqrt{2/\pi}\,u$ (rotate to align $u$ with $e_1$ and integrate).

$$\mathbb{E}\bigl[\sqrt{\tfrac\pi 2}(s_i^\top y)\operatorname{sign}(s_i^\top u)\bigr]\;=\;\sqrt{\tfrac\pi 2}\Bigl\langle y,\sqrt{\tfrac{2}{\pi}}u\Bigr\rangle\;=\;\langle y,u\rangle.$$

Average $d$ i.i.d. copies: same expectation. $\;\blacksquare$

QJL variance proof

Proof of variance bound

Define $Z_i=\sqrt{\pi/2}\,(s_i^\top y)\operatorname{sign}(s_i^\top u)$. Bound the second moment trivially:

$$\operatorname{Var}(Z_i)\;\le\;\mathbb{E} Z_i^2\;=\;\tfrac{\pi}{2}\,\mathbb{E}(s_i^\top y)^2\;=\;\tfrac{\pi}{2}\,\|y\|_2^2.$$

(Used $|\operatorname{sign}|=1$ and $s_i^\top y\sim\mathcal{N}(0,\|y\|^2)$.) The estimator is the average of $d$ independent $Z_i$'s, so its variance is $\le\frac{\pi}{2d}\|y\|_2^2$. $\;\blacksquare$

Non-unit residuals: scale by the residual norm

For $r\ne 0$ with $\gamma=\|r\|_2$, write $u=r/\gamma$ and notice $\operatorname{sign}(Sr)=\operatorname{sign}(Su)$. The scaled QJL reconstruction

$$\widetilde r_{\mathrm{qjl}}\;=\;\gamma\cdot\dfrac{\sqrt{\pi/2}}{d}\,S^\top \operatorname{sign}(Sr)$$

satisfies $\mathbb{E}\langle y,\widetilde r_{\mathrm{qjl}}\rangle=\langle y,r\rangle$ with variance $\le\frac{\pi}{2d}\gamma^2\|y\|_2^2$.

Variance scales with the residual norm $\gamma^2$. If we can make $\gamma$ small, QJL is very accurate.

06

Inner-Product TURBOQUANT

Two-stage: $b-1$ bits MSE on the vector, $1$ bit QJL on the residual

Two-stage decomposition

Write $x=\widetilde x_{\mathrm{mse}}+r$ where $\widetilde x_{\mathrm{mse}}$ is an MSE-quantized version. Then

$$\langle y,x\rangle\;=\;\langle y,\widetilde x_{\mathrm{mse}}\rangle\;+\;\langle y,r\rangle.$$

The first term is computable from the MSE-quantized vector. The second — estimate by QJL on the residual.

MSE stage makes $\|r\|$ small; QJL stage corrects the bias. Variance of QJL is $\propto\|r\|^2/d$ — small precisely when MSE worked well.

Algorithm: TURBOQUANT$_{\mathrm{prod}}$

encode(x, b): idx = TurboQuantmse(x, b−1) # (b−1)·d bits x̃mse = TurboQuantmse−1(idx) r = x − x̃mse; γ = ||r|| q = sign(S·r) ∈ {−1,+1}d # d bits return (idx, q, γ) decode(idx, q, γ): x̃qjl = γ · (√(π/2)/d) · ST·q return x̃mse + x̃qjl

Total bits: $(b-1)d+d=bd$. Plus a scalar $\gamma$ ($\mathcal{O}(1)$ overhead).

Theorem: inner-product guarantee

Theorem (TURBOQUANT$_{\mathrm{prod}}$)

For every unit $x\in S^{d-1}$ and every query $y\in\mathbb{R}^d$:

$$\mathbb{E}\langle y,\widetilde x\rangle=\langle y,x\rangle\quad\text{(unbiased)},\qquad D_{\mathrm{prod}}\;\le\;\frac{\sqrt 3\,\pi^2\,\|y\|_2^2}{d}\cdot 4^{-b}.$$

The $1/d$ factor matters: in high dimension, inner-product errors decay $d$ times faster than MSE errors.

Proof of the inner-product theorem

Proof

Unbiasedness. Condition on $\widetilde x_{\mathrm{mse}}$. Then $r$ is fixed for QJL, so

$$\mathbb{E}[\langle y,\widetilde x\rangle\mid\widetilde x_{\mathrm{mse}}]=\langle y,\widetilde x_{\mathrm{mse}}\rangle+\mathbb{E}\langle y,\widetilde x_{\mathrm{qjl}}\rangle=\langle y,\widetilde x_{\mathrm{mse}}\rangle+\langle y,r\rangle=\langle y,x\rangle.$$

Take outer expectation over $\widetilde x_{\mathrm{mse}}$ — same answer. Unbiased.

Variance. Conditional on $\widetilde x_{\mathrm{mse}}$, by QJL: $\operatorname{Var}\le\frac{\pi}{2d}\|y\|^2\|r\|^2$. Take outer expectation and use the MSE theorem at $b-1$ bits:

$$D_{\mathrm{prod}}\le\frac{\pi}{2d}\|y\|^2\cdot\tfrac{\sqrt 3\,\pi}{2}4^{-(b-1)}\;=\;\frac{\sqrt 3\,\pi^2\,\|y\|^2}{d}\cdot 4^{-b}.\;\blacksquare$$

Inner-product lower bound: the $1/d$ factor is optimal

Argument

Let $e=x-Q^{-1}(Q(x))$. Then $\|e\|_2^2=\sum_j\langle e_j,e\rangle^2$ where $e_j$ is a basis vector. If every coordinate had expected squared error $<4^{-b}/d$, the total MSE would be $<4^{-b}$, contradicting the MSE lower bound.

So some basis direction $e_j$ has $\mathbb{E}|\langle e_j,x\rangle-\langle e_j,\widehat x\rangle|^2\ge\frac{1}{d}4^{-b}$. For general $y$, scale gives

$$D_{\mathrm{prod}}\;\ge\;\dfrac{\|y\|_2^2}{d}\cdot 4^{-b}.\;\blacksquare$$

TURBOQUANT$_{\mathrm{prod}}$ matches this exponent.

07

Application: KV Cache Quantization

Online, data-oblivious, inner-product-preserving

The KV cache problem

Decoder-only transformer: at each new token, the model uses inner products $\langle q_t,k_s\rangle$ between the new query and past keys. For long contexts, the stored KV cache dominates memory and bandwidth.

Quantizing keys perturbs attention logits. Inner-product preservation $\Rightarrow$ attention preservation.

TURBOQUANT$_{\mathrm{prod}}$ matches all three needs:

  • Online: each new key is quantized as it arrives.
  • No calibration: codebook is universal, not dataset-specific.
  • Inner-product control: unbiased estimator with $\mathcal{O}(1/d)$ variance per query.

Bias / variance tradeoff: why two stages

MethodInner-product biasInner-product variance
MSE-only (after rotation)shrinks by $\approx 2/\pi$small for large $b$
QJL only (1 bit)zero$\approx \pi\|y\|^2/(2d)$
MSE $+$ QJL on residualzero$\propto \|y\|^2 \|r\|^2/d$

Two stages keep QJL's unbiasedness while shrinking its variance by the residual norm. The residual norm is small precisely because the MSE stage worked well.

Online and data-oblivious

Public state: rotation $\Pi$, scalar codebook for $f_d$, projection matrix $S$.

Each vector quantized immediately as it arrives. No $k$-means training, no calibration set, no dataset dependence.

Contrast with product quantization (offline codebook training): TURBOQUANT trades data-dependent optimality for universal worst-case scaling.

Empirically: near quality-neutral on long-context benchmarks at $\sim 3.5$ bits/channel; gradual degradation around $2.5$ bits.

The TURBOQUANT recipe in one line

Rotate to Gaussianize, scalar-quantize for MSE, sketch the residual to preserve inner products.

Each step uses one classical idea:

  • Rotation: uniformizes the input on the sphere; coordinates look Gaussian (Lecture 2 / random coding intuition).
  • Lloyd–Max: optimal scalar quantization on a known density (Lecture 1).
  • QJL: one-bit Johnson–Lindenstrauss for unbiased linear-functional estimation.

Sum of pieces: a vector quantizer that matches the Gaussian rate–distortion exponent up to a constant of $\sim 2.7$.

Recap of Lecture 4

  • Random rotation: turns any unit vector into a uniform sphere point with known coordinate density $f_d$.
  • MSE TURBOQUANT: Lloyd–Max on $f_d$ achieves $D_{\mathrm{mse}}\le\frac{\sqrt 3\pi}{2}\cdot 4^{-b}$.
  • Lower bound: Shannon LB on the sphere $+$ Yao's minimax give $D_{\mathrm{mse}}\ge 4^{-b}$. Constant gap, optimal exponent.
  • Bias issue: MSE quantization shrinks inner products by $\approx 2/\pi$ at one bit.
  • QJL: one-bit unbiased estimator for inner products with variance $\propto 1/d$.
  • Inner-product TURBOQUANT: MSE on the vector, QJL on the residual. Unbiased, with $D_{\mathrm{prod}}\le\frac{\sqrt 3\pi^2\|y\|^2}{d}4^{-b}$.
  • Application: KV cache quantization — online, no calibration, attention-preserving.
