What a real quantizer must do — numbers

For dimension $d=8$ and rate $R=4$ bits/coordinate, an unstructured codebook has $2^{32}\approx 4.29\times 10^9$ entries, each a vector of $8$ floats. At 4 bytes each, that is $\approx 137$ GB just to hold the codebook — before you do any inference at all. Searching it for $10^{10}$ weights would cost $10^{10}\cdot 4.29\times 10^9 \approx 4\times 10^{19}$ inner products.

Even at the modest $d=4, R=2$ regime ($K=256$ codepoints, fits in cache), per-vector search of 256 candidates over $10^{10}/4=2.5\times 10^9$ blocks is borderline. The point is not the precise crossover but that scaling $d$ to where the Shannon rate-distortion gain matters destroys feasibility under any constant-factor change to GPUs.

This is why "structured codebook" is non-negotiable for LLMs: not because it is theoretically prettier, but because storage and search both scale as $2^{dR}$ and one cannot run them per layer per token.

Linear (uniform) quantization — what it buys algorithmically

The simplest possible quantizer is $\mathbb{Z}^d$ with step $\Delta$: $\hat x_i = \Delta \cdot \mathrm{round}(x_i / \Delta)$. Coordinate-independent, no list to store, no search.

  • $\mathcal{O}(d)$ encode — a few additions and a floor per coordinate.
  • Zero codebook RAM — only the rule (the step $\Delta$) is stored.
  • Embarrassingly parallel — each coordinate is independent, perfect for GPU lanes.
  • MSE penalty — $G(\mathbb{Z}^d) = 1/12$ at every $d$, vs. the sphere bound $1/(2\pi e) \approx 0.0585$. Penalty is at most a factor $\frac{1/12}{1/(2\pi e)} = \pi e/6 \approx 1.42$, i.e. $\le 1.53$ dB across all $d$.

So scalar uniform quantization gives up at most ~1.5 dB to any structured codebook. For very high $d$, $E_8$ and Leech close most of that gap. The lesson: the algorithmic cost reduction (search $\to$ rounding) is enormous; the MSE cost is small. This is the actual reason structured codebooks dominate practice.

Lattice as additive subgroup — consequences

$\Lambda = B\mathbb{Z}^d$ is closed under addition: $\lambda + \lambda' \in \Lambda$, $-\lambda \in \Lambda$. Two structural consequences feed everything else.

  1. Translation symmetry of Voronoi cells. $\mathcal{V}(\lambda) = \mathcal{V}(0) + \lambda$. So all Voronoi regions are translates of one fundamental cell — one shape, repeated.
  2. Quantization noise distribution. If $X$ is uniform on $\mathbb{R}^d$, then $X - Q_\Lambda(X)$ is uniform on $\mathcal{V}(0)$, independent of which cell $X$ sat in. Encoded over $\Lambda$, the error is data-independent in distribution.

Both properties fail for unstructured VQ: each codepoint has a different cell shape, and the error distribution depends on the cell.

Packing gain — why $G(\Lambda)$ is the right normalization

Let $V = |\mathcal{V}|$ be the cell volume and $E = \int_\mathcal{V}\|x\|^2 dx / V$ the mean squared distance from the centroid. Both scale: if you replace $\Lambda$ by $\alpha\Lambda$, then $V \to \alpha^d V$ and $E \to \alpha^2 E$. So

$G(\Lambda) = \dfrac{E}{V^{2/d}} = \dfrac{1}{V^{1+2/d}}\int_\mathcal{V}\|x\|^2 dx$

is scale-invariant. It depends only on the shape of the cell, not its volume. At fixed rate $R$ (= fixed cell volume), MSE is proportional to $G(\Lambda)$. So $G$ is the dimensionless figure of merit.

Lower bound (Zador): for any $d$, $G(\Lambda) \ge \frac{\Gamma(1+2/d)}{(d+2)\pi}\cdot\frac{1}{V_d^{2/d}}$ where $V_d$ is the volume of the unit ball in $\mathbb{R}^d$. As $d\to\infty$, this approaches $1/(2\pi e)$ — the same bound that the rate-distortion function attains.

Why $E_8$ is optimal in 8D

$E_8$ is the lattice of vectors in $\mathbb{R}^8$ whose coordinates are either all integers or all half-integers, and whose coordinate sum is even.

$E_8 = \bigl(\mathbb{Z}^8 \cup (\mathbb{Z}^8 + \tfrac12 \mathbf{1})\bigr) \cap \{x : \mathbf{1}^\top x \in 2\mathbb{Z}\}.$

It has 240 minimum-norm vectors (the "kissing number" in 8D), vs. 16 for $\mathbb{Z}^8$. Densely packed sphere centers $\Rightarrow$ rounder Voronoi cells.

Numerically, $G(E_8) \approx 0.0717$ vs. $G(\mathbb{Z}^8) = 1/12 \approx 0.0833$ — about 14% lower MSE at the same rate, by switching the lattice with no other change to the algorithm.

Viazovska (2017, Fields Medal) proved that $E_8$ achieves the densest sphere packing in 8D. This bounds how much further any 8D lattice can reduce MSE: $E_8$ is essentially as good as it gets at $d=8$.

QUIP\# uses $E_8$ specifically because at $\sim 2$ bits per weight (i.e. $\log_2 256 = 8$ bits per 8-block, $R \approx 1$ per coordinate), the gap between scalar quantization and $E_8$ is measurable in LLM perplexity, and $E_8$ recovers it.

Fast rounding for $E_8$ — the algorithm

  1. Round to $\mathbb{Z}^8$: $\mathbf{n}_1 = \lfloor x + \tfrac12\rfloor$ component-wise.
  2. Fix parity: if $\sum n_{1,i}$ is odd, find the index $i^\star$ with the largest absolute rounding error, and flip its rounding direction. Result: $\mathbf{n}_1 \in D_8$ (even-sum integer lattice).
  3. Try the half-integer translate: repeat steps 1–2 on $x - (\tfrac12,\ldots,\tfrac12)$, then add back $(\tfrac12,\ldots,\tfrac12)$, giving $\mathbf{n}_2$.
  4. Pick the closer of $\mathbf{n}_1, \mathbf{n}_2$ to $x$.

Total work: $\mathcal{O}(d)$ rounds plus $\mathcal{O}(d)$ comparisons. No codebook lookup at all. Compare with naive search over $|E_8 \cap \mathcal{B}(r)|$ candidates.

The QUIP proxy loss — why this is the right objective

For a linear layer $y = Wx$, replacing $W$ by $\widehat W$ produces output error $\|(W-\widehat W)x\|^2$. Averaging over a calibration distribution of activations $x$:

$\ell(\widehat W) = \mathbb{E}_x\bigl\|(\widehat W - W)x\bigr\|^2 = \mathrm{tr}\bigl((\widehat W - W) H (\widehat W - W)^\top\bigr),$
$H = \mathbb{E}_x[xx^\top].$

This is per-layer, decouples across rows, and makes quantization tractable for LLMs (Nagel et al. 2020). $H$ is positive semi-definite; high curvature in some direction $v$ means errors along $v$ matter more — the quantizer should round those directions more carefully.

Crucially, $\ell(\widehat W)$ is invariant under conjugation by orthogonal $V$: $\mathrm{tr}((U W V^\top)(V H V^\top)(V W^\top U^\top)) = \mathrm{tr}(U W H W^\top U^\top) = \mathrm{tr}(W H W^\top)$. So we can rotate freely without changing the proxy.

Incoherence definition, formally

(Chee et al. 2023, Defn. 1.) A symmetric PSD matrix $H \in \mathbb{R}^{n\times n}$ is $\mu$-incoherent if its eigendecomposition $H = Q\Lambda Q^\top$ has

$\max_{i,j}|Q_{ij}| = \max_{i,j}|e_i^\top Q e_j| \;\le\; \mu/\sqrt n.$

A matrix $W \in \mathbb{R}^{m\times n}$ is $\mu$-incoherent if $\max_{i,j}|W_{ij}| \le \mu \|W\|_F / \sqrt{mn}$.

"Most" $n\times n$ orthogonal matrices are $\mathcal{O}(\sqrt{\log n})$-incoherent: a random orthogonal column has entries with squared magnitudes concentrating around their mean $1/n$, so $|Q_{ij}|$ is $\widetilde{\mathcal{O}}(1/\sqrt n)$.

Operationally: incoherent = no entry much larger than the typical scale. The maximum-to-typical ratio is bounded. So a single $\Delta$ can quantize all entries to similar relative error — no outlier wastes the dynamic range.

QUIP's rotation — why Kronecker

Conjugating by a uniformly random orthogonal $V \in \mathbb{R}^{n\times n}$ would give the cleanest incoherence guarantee but cost $\Theta(n^2)$ per multiply — same order as the linear layer, doubling inference cost.

Solution: factor $n = pq$ with $p \approx q \approx \sqrt n$, draw $V_1$ uniform on $\mathbb{R}^{p\times p}$ orthogonals and $V_2$ uniform on $\mathbb{R}^{q\times q}$, set $V = V_1 \otimes V_2$. Multiplying $V x$ reshapes $x$ to $p\times q$, applies $V_1$ on the left and $V_2^\top$ on the right: $\Theta(n(p+q)) = \Theta(n\sqrt n)$.

Lemma 5: with $k=2$ factors, $V H V^\top$ is $\mu_H$-incoherent with $\mu_H = \mathcal{O}(\log^2(n/\delta))$ w.p. $\ge 1-\delta$. Polylog dependence is nearly as good as the unstructured case.

LDLQ — precise statement

After incoherence processing, QUIP rounds per coordinate but with feedback. For $k=1, \ldots, n$:

$\widehat W_k = Q\bigl(W_k + (W_{1:k-1} - \widehat W_{1:k-1}) a_k\bigr),$

where $W_k$ is the $k$-th column, $a_k \in \mathbb{R}^{k-1}$, and $Q$ is element-wise nearest or stochastic rounding. Equivalently $\widehat W = Q(W + (W - \widehat W) U)$ with $U$ strictly upper triangular.

Optimal choice: take $U = \bar U$ from the LDL factorization of $H$:

$H = (\bar U + I) D (\bar U + I)^\top.$

(Chee et al. Theorem 1.) LDLQ is worst-case and average-case optimal among linear-feedback rounding methods. Worst-case loss equals $\frac{m}{4}\mathrm{tr}(D)$; nearest/stochastic baselines pay $\frac{m}{c}\mathrm{tr}(H)$ where $c\in\{12,6\}$.

Combined with Lemma 2 (incoherence implies $\mathrm{tr}(D) \le \frac{\mu^2}{n}\mathrm{tr}(H^{1/2})^2$): under $\mu$-incoherence, LDLQ improves over baselines by a factor $\mu^2 k/n$ where $k$ is the effective rank of $H$. This is what gives QUIP its 2-bit guarantees.

Note the rounding is per-coordinate; it is the order and feedback across coordinates that couples them, not a vector quantizer. Vector quantization is the QUIP\# extension.

Equivalence to OPTQ/GPTQ

Theorem 6 of QUIP: OPTQ (a.k.a. GPTQ; Frantar et al. 2023) is the same procedure as LDLQ. OPTQ's description is iterative-with-error-correction; LDLQ's is matrix-algebraic. They produce identical outputs.

Practical consequence: QUIP's implementation needs one Cholesky decomposition of $H$ and no inverse. OPTQ does an inverse plus two Choleskys. Same answer, less work.

This is also the first theoretical analysis of OPTQ — the QUIP framework retroactively explains why OPTQ works.

QUIP\# step 1: Randomized Hadamard Transform

A Hadamard matrix $H_n$ has entries in $\{-1, +1\}$ and $H_n H_n^\top = n I_n$. So $\frac{1}{\sqrt n} H_n$ is orthogonal. Multiplying $H_n x$ via the Fast Walsh–Hadamard Transform takes $\Theta(n \log n)$ operations — entirely additions, no float multiplies.

Randomized version: let $S \in \mathbb{R}^{n\times n}$ be a diagonal of independent uniform $\pm 1$ signs. The Randomized Hadamard Transform is $V = \frac{1}{\sqrt n} H_n S$. The $S$ is essential: without it, $H_n W$ has predictable failure modes for adversarial $W$ (e.g. $W$ aligned with a Hadamard column would not become incoherent). The random signs guarantee the CLT applies to every $W$.

QUIP\# Lemma 3.1: with $S_U \sim \mathrm{Unif}\{\pm 1\}^m$ and $S_V \sim \mathrm{Unif}\{\pm 1\}^n$ diagonals, set $\widetilde W = (\frac{1}{\sqrt m} H_m S_U) W (\frac{1}{\sqrt n} H_n S_V)^\top$, $\widetilde H = (\frac{1}{\sqrt n} H_n S_V) H (\frac{1}{\sqrt n} H_n S_V)^\top$. Then for any $\delta > 0$,

$\mu_H = 2\sqrt{\log(2n^2/\delta)}, \qquad \mu_W = 2\sqrt{\log(4mn/\delta)}.$

Compare QUIP's 2-factor Kronecker: $\mu_W^{\mathrm{Kron}} = A^2 \log^2(4Cmn/\delta)$. RHT has square-root-log dependence, vs. $\log^2$ for Kronecker. Strictly tighter.

The CLT argument made precise

After RHT, each entry of $\widetilde W = (H_m S_U / \sqrt m) W (H_n S_V/\sqrt n)^\top$ is

$\widetilde W_{ij} = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{mn}} \sum_{k,\ell} (H_m)_{ik} S_{U,k} W_{k\ell} (H_n)_{j\ell} S_{V,\ell}.$

The signs $S_{U,k} S_{V,\ell}$ are independent $\pm 1$. Conditional on $W$, $\widetilde W_{ij}$ is a weighted sum of $mn$ independent random signs with weights $W_{k\ell}/\sqrt{mn}$. Variance: $\frac{1}{mn}\sum_{k,\ell} W_{k\ell}^2 = \|W\|_F^2 / (mn)$.

Berry–Esseen: the marginal CDF of $\widetilde W_{ij}$ deviates from the Gaussian with the same variance by $\mathcal{O}(\rho/(\sigma^3 \sqrt N))$ where $\rho$ is the third absolute moment of the summands and $N = mn$. For LLM matrices with $mn \gtrsim 10^6$, this is small.

Maximum-entry control (the actual incoherence statement) is a union bound: $\Pr[\max_{i,j} |\widetilde W_{ij}| > t] \le mn \cdot \Pr[|\widetilde W_{1,1}| > t]$, and the per-entry tail is sub-Gaussian by Hoeffding (sum of $\pm 1$). Choose $t = \mu \|W\|_F / \sqrt{mn}$ with $\mu = 2\sqrt{\log(4mn/\delta)}$; the union bound gives $\delta$.

QUIP\# step 2: BlockLDLQ + $E_8$

RHT-rotated weights are roughly Gaussian and ball-shaped. Per-coordinate rounding (LDLQ) packs them into a hypercube — mismatch.

BlockLDLQ extends LDLQ to vector quantization. Pick block size $g$ dividing $n$. Decompose $H = L^\top D L$ where $L$ is unit block-lower-triangular ($g\times g$ blocks; diagonal blocks all $I$, blocks above diagonal all $0$) and $D$ is block-diagonal. Set $U = L^\top - I$. Round in $g$-column blocks:

$\widehat W_k = Q_{E_8}\bigl(W_k + (W_{1:k-g} - \widehat W_{1:k-g})A_k\bigr).$

Theorem 4.1: if $H$ is $\mu$-incoherent and $Q$ is a vector quantizer with $\mathbb{E}[(Q(x)-x)(Q(x)-x)^\top] \preceq \sigma^2 I$ for any $x \in \mathbb{R}^g$, then

$\mathbb{E}\,\mathrm{tr}\bigl((\widehat W - W)H(\widehat W - W)^\top\bigr) \le \dfrac{g m \mu^2}{n}\sigma^2 \mathrm{tr}(H^{1/2})^2.$

Same $\mathrm{tr}(H) \to \mathrm{tr}(H^{1/2})^2 / n$ improvement that QUIP gets in the scalar case, now for VQ.

The E8P codebook — how 2 bits fit in 1 KiB

QUIP\#'s 2-bit codebook for blocks of 8 has $2^{16}$ entries, but stores only $2^8$. The trick uses $E_8$'s symmetry.

$E_8 = \widehat D_8 \cup (\widehat D_8 + \frac12)$ where $\widehat D_8 = \{x \in \mathbb{Z}^8 + \frac12 : \mathbf{1}^\top x \in 2\mathbb{Z}\}$ is the half-integer even-parity sublattice. Each entry of $\widehat D_8$ factors as $|s| \odot \sigma$ where $|s| \in |\widehat D_8|$ (absolute values) and $\sigma$ is a sign vector of correct parity.

Storage: 8 bits to index into 256 absolute-value entries, 7 bits of explicit sign flips (the 8th sign is determined by parity), 1 bit to choose $\widehat D_8$ vs. $\widehat D_8 + \frac12$. Total: 16 bits = 2 bits/weight, 256-entry table = 1 KiB. Fits in L1 even after 32× bank-conflict duplication on a GPU.

Decode: lookup, sign-flip, optional half-shift. A handful of ALU ops per 8-block.

Practical caveats not on the slides

  • Calibration data. The proxy $H = \mathbb{E}_x[xx^\top]$ requires a small calibration set of activations. Quality depends on how representative this set is; QUIP/QUIP\# typically use a few hundred sequences.
  • Per-channel scales. Different output channels have different weight magnitudes. QUIP's Algorithm 1 line 4 diagonally rescales $W$ and $H$ before incoherence processing, trading off the spectrum.
  • Higher bitrates. $E_8$ alone is good at $\sim 2$ bits/weight ($K = 256$). For 3 or 4 bits, QUIP\# uses Residual Vector Quantization (RVQ): quantize, then quantize the residual, repeat. Stays inside $E_8$'s structure at every step.
  • Fine-tuning. QUIP\# adds an inter-layer fine-tuning pass after quantization, recovering some perplexity. This is QAT-lite, much cheaper than full QAT, and is on top of (not instead of) the lattice quantization.
  • Activation quantization. The deck and these notes cover only weight quantization. Activation quantization (post-rotation activations, KV cache) is the topic of Lecture 4 (TURBOQUANT) and a separate strand of the literature.
  • Dimensions not power of 2. Hadamard matrices need $n$ a power of 2 (in general). QUIP\# factors $n = pq$ with $p$ the largest power-of-2 factor, then uses $V = H_p \otimes H_q$ — a hybrid Hadamard–Kronecker scheme. RFFT (random FFT with random complex phase) is the fallback when no Hadamard factorization is available.
