Where the storage / search numbers come from

For dimension $d=8$ and rate $R=4$ bits/coordinate, the codebook has $2^{32}\approx 4.29\times 10^9$ entries, each a vector of $8$ floats. At 4 bytes each, that is $\approx 137$ GB just to hold the codebook. Search through it for $10^{10}$ weights: $10^{10}\cdot 4.29\times 10^9$ inner products, far beyond what is achievable with any finite compute budget.

Even at $d=4,R=2$ (8 billion weights, $K=256$ codepoints): codebook fits in cache, but per-vector search of 256 candidates over $10^{10}/4=2.5\times 10^9$ blocks is borderline. The point is not the precise crossover; it is that scaling $d$ to where the rate-distortion gain matters destroys feasibility.

What does $E_8$ buy you in practice?

$E_8$ is the lattice of vectors in $\mathbb{R}^8$ whose coordinates are either all integers or all half-integers, and whose coordinate sum is even. It has 240 nearest neighbors (vs. 16 for $\mathbb{Z}^8$), giving denser sphere packing and rounder Voronoi cells.

Numerically, $G(E_8)\approx 0.0717$ vs. $G(\mathbb{Z}^8)=1/12\approx 0.0833$. The MSE improvement at fixed rate is the ratio: $\approx 14\%$ lower distortion for free, just by switching the lattice.

QUIP\# uses $E_8$ specifically because of this gain: at 8 bits per block (i.e. $R\approx 1$ per coordinate, since $\log_2 256 = 8$), scalar quantization (or $\mathbb{Z}^8$) leaves measurable LLM perplexity on the table that $E_8$ recovers.

Fast rounding for $E_8$ — the algorithm

  1. Round to $\mathbb{Z}^8$: $\mathbf{n}_1=\lfloor x+\tfrac12\rfloor$ component-wise.
  2. Fix parity: if $\sum n_{1,i}$ is odd, find the index $i^\star$ with the largest absolute rounding error $|x_{i^\star}-(n_{1,i^\star}-\operatorname{sgn}(\cdot))|$ and flip its rounding direction. Result: $\mathbf{n}_1\in D_8$.
  3. Try the half-integer translate: $\mathbf{n}_2$ obtained by repeating steps 1–2 on $x-(\tfrac12,\ldots,\tfrac12)$ and adding back $(\tfrac12,\ldots,\tfrac12)$.
  4. Pick whichever is closer to $x$.

Total work: $\mathcal{O}(d)$ rounding plus $\mathcal{O}(d)$ comparisons. No codebook lookup at all.

Why Hadamard, specifically

A Hadamard matrix $H_d$ has entries in $\{-1,+1\}$ and $H_d H_d^\top = d I$, so $\frac{1}{\sqrt d}H_d$ is orthogonal. The multiplication is structured: $H_d x$ can be computed in $\mathcal{O}(d\log d)$ time via the fast Walsh–Hadamard transform, far cheaper than a generic dense rotation $\mathcal{O}(d^2)$.

QUIP\# uses randomized Hadamard transforms: $\mathbf{U}=H_d D_U/\sqrt{d}$ where $D_U$ is a diagonal of independent $\pm 1$ random signs. The randomization is what makes the central-limit argument apply to every weight matrix, regardless of its structure. Without the diagonal, $H_d \mathbf{W}$ would have predictable failure modes for adversarial $\mathbf{W}$.

The CLT argument made precise

After randomized Hadamard rotation, each entry of $\widetilde{\mathbf{W}}=\mathbf{U}\mathbf{W}\mathbf{V}^\top$ is a weighted sum of all $mn$ entries of $\mathbf{W}$ with random $\pm 1/\sqrt{mn}$ coefficients (up to constants). The Berry–Esseen theorem gives a quantitative CLT: the marginal CDF of $\widetilde{W}_{ij}$ deviates from the Gaussian CDF by $\mathcal{O}(\rho/\sigma^3\sqrt{N})$ where $\rho$ is the third absolute moment and $N$ is the effective sample size.

For LLM weight matrices with millions of entries, this deviation is small. The maximum entry of $\widetilde{\mathbf{W}}$ is also tightly controlled — this is the "incoherence" property: no entry is much larger than the typical scale, so the lattice can quantize everything at the same $\Delta$.

What entropy coding adds on top

After lattice quantization, integer indices $\mathbf{n}_b$ are not uniformly distributed: they concentrate near the origin (because the data is concentrated). Entropy coding compresses these indices to roughly the Shannon entropy of the index distribution, which can be substantially less than $\log_2|\mathcal{C}_{\text{lattice}}|$.

This is where the "rate–distortion limit" actually closes: lattice rounding handles the geometric matching, entropy coding handles the rest. Together they approach the Shannon bound up to small lattice-shaping losses.

Practical caveats not covered on the slides

  • Calibration data: the QUIP\# pipeline uses a small calibration dataset to compute Hessian-aware error bounds (the GPTQ-style proxy). This refines which weights to round in which direction. The lattice rule alone is not the end of the story.
  • Per-channel scales: different output channels have different weight magnitudes. QUIP\# scales each channel before incoherence processing.
  • Activation quantization: the slide discussion only covers weight quantization. Activation quantization (KV cache, post-rotation activations) is the topic of Lecture 4 and a separate strand of the literature.
